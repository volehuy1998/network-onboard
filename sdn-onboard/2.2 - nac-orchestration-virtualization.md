## Learning objectives

After finishing this part, you can:

1. **List (Remember)** three lines of enterprise control plane between 2000 and 2010: NAC (Network Access Control with RADIUS plus COPS plus 802.1X), Orchestration (HP OpenView plus IBM Tivoli plus CA Unicenter), and Virtualization plug-ins (VMware vCenter plus vDS, XenServer with OVS).

3. **Connect (Analyze)** how the three lines converge into the modular plug-in architecture of later network control systems, where the mechanism driver is similar to the Orchestration device driver, the type driver (VLAN, VXLAN, Geneve) is similar to the Virtualization plug-in, and the security-group policy inherits the NAC model.

## Context: enterprise did not travel through ATM

The two previous parts (2.0 DCAN and GSMP, 2.1 Ipsilon and Active Networking) followed the research-driven lineage tied to ATM and later to IP Switching and MPLS. During the same period (2000-2010), enterprise networking followed a different trajectory, less revolutionary but commercially more widespread. The three enterprise lines did not start from the goal "programmable network". They started from three concrete operational needs:

- NAC was born because the BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) movement of the early 2000s forced enterprises to control network access for unknown laptops, in contrast to the previous "every port has access by default" model.
- Orchestration platforms were born because the number of network devices in an enterprise grew quickly (driven by the Internet boom), and managing each device manually was no longer feasible.
- Virtualization plug-ins were born when VMware ESX (2001) and KVM (2007) turned servers into multi-tenant systems, shifting the network edge from the ToR switch into the hypervisor.

The three lines did not know about each other initially, and none of them realized that they were laying a foundation for SDN. But when OpenStack Neutron was born in 2012-2013 and needed to design a network control plane for multi-tenant cloud, the architects borrowed ideas from all three lines. The result was the Neutron ML2 plug-in architecture (which the rev 3 curriculum does not go into) and, further along, OVN NBDB (Part 13). Both carry traces of this lineage.

Understanding these three lines helps answer a question that learners often ask: "Why does the OVN NBDB have `Logical_Switch`, `Logical_Switch_Port`, ACL, and `Port_Group`?" The short answer is: from the hypervisor vDS (`Logical_Switch`), from NAC (ACL and security policy), and from orchestration (declarative API management), all merged together.

## 2.2.1 NAC (RADIUS plus COPS plus 802.1X): policy enforcement from an external controller

### Enterprise context 2000-2008: BYOD and guest access

Before the mid 2000s, enterprise networks operated under the assumption that every device plugged into a port belonged to the company, had been approved by IT, and had joined an Active Directory or LDAP domain. Switch-port access was open by default. Plugging in a cable was enough to get connectivity. Security relied on physical security (locked doors, badge access) rather than on logical controls.

In the mid 2000s, three changes broke this assumption simultaneously. First, laptops became standard, replacing desktops. Employees carried laptops between the office and home, and they had to plug back in when returning to the office. Second, consultants, contractors, and vendors regularly needed guest access, and pre-provisioning every individual was not feasible. Third, wireless LAN (802.11) became widespread, blurring the boundary "physical access equals trust".

Enterprises needed a new mechanism: **check the identity of a device (and the user) before granting network access**. This is the origin of NAC, Network Access Control.

### Three pillar protocols of NAC

NAC was built on three existing protocols, combined into an enforcement framework:

**RADIUS (Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service, RFC 2865, Carl Rigney et al., June 2000).** This is a client-server authentication protocol, originally designed for dial-up ISPs (the name "Dial-In" reflects that origin). It was quickly adapted for enterprise LAN. The Authenticator (the access switch) sends a user credential to the Authentication Server (the RADIUS server), and the server replies Accept or Reject. The credential can be a password (PAP or CHAP), an X.509 certificate, or a token. Extensions RFC 2866 (Accounting) and RFC 2869 (Extensions) appeared at the same time.

**COPS (Common Open Policy Service, RFC 2748, David Durham et al., January 2000).** This is a policy-push protocol designed for the scenario where a central Policy Decision Point (PDP) pushes policy down to distributed Policy Enforcement Points (PEP). The PDP has the policy knowledge, and the PEP has access to the enforcement mechanism (ACLs on a switch, quotas on a router). COPS is a clear precursor to the OpenFlow controller-switch relationship.

**IEEE 802.1X (Port-based Network Access Control, standardized in 2001, revised in 2010).** This is the standard framework for port-level authentication on Ethernet switches. Before authentication completes, the port allows only EAPOL (Extensible Authentication Protocol over LAN) traffic, that is, only authentication traffic. After successful authentication (through RADIUS or another method), the port opens for normal traffic. 802.1X is especially important because it enforces at the switch port hardware layer, independent of the endpoint OS or the wireless AP.

### A typical NAC deployment architecture

A 2005-2010 enterprise NAC deployment usually had three tiers:

Tier 1 is the **supplicant**, software on the endpoint that implements 802.1X EAPOL. Windows XP SP2 and later has it built in, Linux has `wpa_supplicant`, and macOS has it built in. The supplicant sends the credential.

Tier 2 is the **authenticator**, an access switch or a wireless AP with 802.1X enabled. The switch acts as a proxy between the supplicant and the authentication server: it receives EAPOL from the supplicant, encapsulates it in RADIUS, and sends it to the server.

Tier 3 is the **authentication server**, such as Microsoft NPS (Network Policy Server), FreeRADIUS, or Cisco ACS. The server has knowledge of the user or device database (LDAP, AD), evaluates the credential, and returns Accept or Reject together with attributes (for example, a VLAN assignment).

When the supplicant authenticates successfully, the authentication server does not just return Accept; it also pushes policy attributes. User X belongs to group "Engineering" and is therefore placed in VLAN 100. User Y belongs to "Guest" and is placed in VLAN 200. The switch reads the attribute and assigns the port to the corresponding VLAN. This is the first time in enterprise networking that the switch has policy **decided by an external controller** rather than configured manually port by port by the operator.

### NAC's legacy in SDN

Three NAC patterns were inherited almost intact into SDN:

**Policy push from the controller.** Just as the RADIUS server pushes a VLAN assignment, the OpenFlow controller pushes flow entries. In both cases, the switch does not decide; the controller decides.

**Stateful per session.** NAC tracks each user session (who is logged in, when the session expires), not each packet. The SDN flow table tracks each flow, not each packet. Both operate at "connection" granularity rather than "packet" granularity, which significantly reduces overhead.

**Attribute-based access.** NAC uses attributes (group membership, device type, time of day) to decide access. SDN ACLs use similar attributes (source IP range, port, protocol, security group). OVN `Port_Group` (Part 13.3) is an abstraction inherited directly from the NAC group model.

> **Key point:** NAC is not SDN, but NAC socialized the pattern "controller decides policy, switch executes" in enterprise networking from 2005 to 2010, well before OpenFlow appeared in 2009. When enterprise admins met OpenFlow for the first time, the controller-switch relationship was already familiar from existing RADIUS and 802.1X deployments. This is one of the reasons OpenFlow was adopted in the enterprise faster than expected compared with earlier research projects.

## 2.2.2 Orchestration platforms (HP OpenView, IBM Tivoli): multi-device workflows

### Mega-NMS in the early 2000s and three problems they solved

From 2000 to 2010, enterprises and service providers invested heavily in mega-platform Network Management Systems (NMS): HP OpenView Network Node Manager (NNM), IBM Tivoli NetView (later Tivoli Netcool Omnibus), and CA Unicenter. These three products dominated the NMS market during this period, with license prices ranging from a few hundred thousand to a few million US dollars for large enterprises.

The three core functions provided by the mega-NMS:

**Auto-discovery through SNMP plus CDP or LLDP.** The NMS scans the entire network range and uses SNMP (RFC 1157, 1990) to read sysDescr, sysName, and ifTable from each device, drawing the topology automatically. Cisco CDP (Cisco Discovery Protocol, proprietary) and LLDP (IEEE 802.1AB, 2005) allow more precise device-adjacency discovery than a pure SNMP sweep.

**Template-based config deployment through CLI scripting.** The NMS lets the operator define a configuration template (for example, "add VLAN 100 named APP-TIER with interface mapping by pattern"), select target devices, and the NMS dispatches CLI commands through SSH or Telnet to each device. Templates were usually written in a proprietary language (HP OpenView used Expect-based scripts, IBM Tivoli used custom Java), not interoperable across vendor NMSs.

**Correlated fault management.** The NMS receives events from multiple sources (SNMP traps, syslog, polling results) and correlates them to avoid alert flooding. For example, a single link down between core and access switch can fail 50 paths. The NMS receives 50 events but raises only one alert about the root link. The correlation logic is complex and requires a custom rule engine.

### Why Orchestration in this period was still a "CLI wrapper"

Despite broad visibility and basic automation, this generation of mega-NMS still belonged in the "CLI wrapper" category in the technical sense. Three reasons:

**No standardized data model.** NETCONF (RFC 4741, December 2006; updated to RFC 6241, June 2011) and YANG (RFC 6020, October 2010) only appeared after these mega-NMS products had dominated the market for several years. Without a standard data model, templates had to be vendor-specific, and porting a template to a new vendor took a great deal of effort.

**CLI fragility.** CLI output changes between firmware versions, sometimes the format of spaces or columns changes without a release note. Templates that parse the output break easily during upgrades. Operators had to test templates against every firmware version the enterprise was running. Maintenance cycles took hours per week.

**No transactional semantics.** A template deployment pushes 100 commands to 20 devices. If the eleventh device fails on the fiftieth command, the entire network state is now half updated and half not. Rollback has to be done manually. There is no atomic commit and rollback as in a database. Configuration drift accumulates.

The mega-NMS vendors tried to solve these three problems through two technology efforts:

**OSSP (Open Services Session Protocol).** A standardization of session-level semantics. It did not succeed because of insufficient vendor adoption.

**WBEM (Web-Based Enterprise Management, DMTF 1996).** A broader framework for management over HTTP and XML, with CIM (Common Information Model) as the data model. It achieved partial adoption in storage (SMI-S) but never dominated networking.

Only with NETCONF plus YANG in the late 2000s and early 2010s did networking finally have a real standard data model. Pre-NETCONF mega-NMSs began to decline. IBM spun off Tivoli as a separate business in 2015, HP spun off OpenView into Micro Focus in 2017, and CA Unicenter rebranded several times.

### Orchestration legacy: the modular plug-in system

Although the specific products declined, the architectural pattern of mega-NMS was inherited:

**Device driver abstraction.** Each vendor device has a driver module (HP called it "Collector", IBM called it "Adapter") that translates proprietary CLI or SNMP into a common data model for the NMS core. This pattern reappears in the modular plug-in architecture of later network control systems (such as the previous Neutron ML2 and today the driver layer of ONOS and OpenDaylight).

**Template-based provisioning.** The mega-NMS template language was not a standard, but the concept "describe the intent, the NMS renders it into commands" is the precursor to Terraform HCL, Ansible YAML, and the OVN NBDB declarative API. The evolution line from declarative configuration intent to imperative commands is clear.

**Fault correlation.** Mega-NMS correlation logic was modernized into today's observability stack (Prometheus alert rules, Grafana, PagerDuty). OVN does not have built-in fault correlation, but it exposes flow and port statistics for upstream observability tools to consume.

## 2.2.3 Hypervisor virtualization plug-ins: pushing edge network control into the server

### The 2009 turning point: VMware vDS and XenServer 5.5

The year 2009 was a landmark in hypervisor networking. Before 2009, hypervisors had simple virtual switches: an L2 bridge in the kernel or in user space, processing packets between the VM and the physical NIC, with no centralized management. Each hypervisor host configured its local vSwitch through GUI or CLI on the host.

In August 2009, VMware released vSphere 4.0 with a new feature: **the vNetwork Distributed Switch (vDS)**. vDS is a virtual switch **spanning multiple ESXi hosts** in the same vCenter cluster, managed centrally from vCenter Server instead of per host. The operator creates a port group on the vDS once, and the port group is automatically available on every host in the cluster. When a VM migrates from host A to host B, the policy of the VM (VLAN, QoS, ACL) follows the VM automatically. There is no need to reconfigure the destination host.

The same year, Citrix XenServer 5.5 (June 2009) integrated Open vSwitch as the default vSwitch, replacing the Linux bridge that Xen used previously. OVS provided native OpenFlow support (the original 1.0 version), with XenServer centralized management through Citrix XAPI and later Citrix CloudPlatform. This integration was a major leap: OVS went from a Nicira research prototype to a production component of a commercial hypervisor product.

### Why hypervisors push edge control into the server

Three technical factors combined to make 2009 the right timing for hypervisor networking to step up:

**Server multi-tenancy at scale.** An ESXi 4.0 host could run 20 to 50 VMs on dense Intel Nehalem CPUs, depending on the workload. Each VM has its own virtual NIC and belongs to a separate VLAN or policy. The number of "ports" on a hypervisor exceeded the number of ports on a traditional ToR switch. Network policy became more complex on the hypervisor than on the physical switch.

**vMotion and live migration.** VMware vMotion (released in 2003) and KVM live migration (mature in 2009) allow VMs to move between hosts without downtime. Network policy must follow the VM and cannot be tied to a fixed port on a physical switch. This was a requirement that the physical network could not meet elegantly.

**The hypervisor is the new trust boundary.** Before virtualization, the trust boundary was the physical host OS. After virtualization, the trust boundary is the hypervisor (the guest OS trusts the hypervisor, and the hypervisor trusts the hardware). Policy enforcement naturally belongs to the hypervisor layer, not to the guest OS or the physical switch.

### The first "single controller plus many distributed switches" model in the enterprise

The vDS and XenServer-with-OVS architecture brought a pattern to the enterprise that would later be a hallmark of SDN: **one controller managing many distributed switches through a private API**.

vCenter Server is the controller, and each ESXi host runs part of the vDS (called the "host proxy switch"), which is the distributed switch. vCenter pushes configuration to every host through the vCenter Agent using a proprietary VMware protocol (not OpenFlow). The operator sees the vDS as a single logical switch; in reality it is 10 to 100 host proxy switches that vCenter keeps in sync.

The same applies to XenServer plus OVS: XAPI (and later CloudPlatform) is the controller, and each XenServer host runs OVS locally as the distributed switch. XAPI synchronizes configuration through SSH or XMLRPC.

This model is SDN by any other name, even though the 2009 vendors did not call it SDN. The difference from later OpenFlow-based SDN is that vDS and XenServer did not use OpenFlow; they used proprietary protocols. But the principle (centralized control plus distributed switches plus API-driven configuration) is identical.

### Legacy into Neutron, OVN, and the modern era

The path from 2009 directly to Neutron in 2012 and OVN in 2015 is a short step. Three concrete inheritances:

**Logical-switch concept.** vDS port group leads to Neutron network leads to OVN `Logical_Switch`. All three are abstractions of "a logical broadcast domain spanning multiple physical hosts". The OVN `Logical_Switch` in NBDB (Part 13.1) has a direct genealogy from the VMware vDS port group.

**Policy following the workload.** vMotion preserves policy. The Neutron security group follows the port. The OVN ACL is attached to a logical port. The pattern "policy attached to the logical workload, not to the physical infrastructure" travels from the hypervisor up to the cloud.

**Integration bridges (`br-int`, `br-ex`).** The pattern in which a hypervisor has multiple internal bridges for different purposes (VM-facing, external uplink, management), connected through patch ports, originated in the Xen and OVS integration of 2009. OVN (Part 13.4) still uses this pattern almost intact.

> **Key point:** The 2009 hypervisor virtualization plug-ins were the first time the concept "distributed virtual switch managed by a centralized controller" entered mainstream enterprise products. OpenFlow 2009 provided an open protocol for that pattern. OVN 2015 formalized the concept into a logical network model with NBDB. The three steps (vDS to OpenFlow to OVN) are a continuous evolution of a single idea, not three disconnected technologies.

## 2.2.4 Legacy: three lines converging into a modular plug-in architecture

In summary, the three lines NAC, Orchestration, and Virtualization plug-ins do not intersect at the level of specific technology, but they converge at the level of ideas in the modular plug-in architecture used by later network control systems. Three clear inheritances:

**From Orchestration to mechanism driver.** In the modular plug-in architecture, the mechanism driver is responsible for "talking to the real device" (a physical switch, a hypervisor vSwitch, a container CNI). This pattern is inherited from the Device Driver of mega-NMS. OVN does not have a mechanism driver in that sense, but `ovn-controller` plus OpenFlow plays the equivalent role: `ovn-controller` translates logical flows (from SBDB) into OpenFlow flow rules installed into local OVS, similar to the way an NMS driver translates a template into CLI commands.

**From Virtualization plug-in to type driver.** The type driver in the modular architecture manages the network type (VLAN, VXLAN, Geneve) and knows how to encapsulate traffic for that type. The genealogy goes from vDS port-group types (VLAN, VLAN trunk, private VLAN) through plug-ins via OpenStack Neutron to OVN `Logical_Switch_Port` types (`vif`, `localnet`, `chassisredirect`, and so on, Part 13.5).

**From NAC to security group.** The cloud security group inherits directly from the NAC group plus ACL model. A user or device belongs to a group, and the group has ACL rules. OVN implements the security group through ACLs attached to a `Port_Group`, which is identical in architectural terms. Forward reference: Part 13.3 (OVN ACL and `Port_Group`) for the concrete implementation.

Forward references to Part 4.1 (OpenFlow 1.2 OXM TLV match) and Part 13.5 (OVN `Port_Binding` types) will analyze similar abstractions implemented at other layers, allowing the operator to recognize the pattern "plug-in replacing hardcoded logic" repeated across many tiers of the SDN architecture.

> **Common misconception:** *"NAC is just SDN applied to the enterprise access layer."* False. NAC (RADIUS and 802.1X plus COPS) and SDN (OpenFlow and OVN) have very different responsibility models. The NAC controller decides only **admission** (allow or reject the device into the network plus assign a VLAN or ACL profile). After admission, the switch operates forwarding on its own using traditional MAC learning plus Spanning Tree. The SDN controller controls **every flow entry** in the forwarding pipeline throughout the lifetime of the connection. NAC is "admission control"; SDN is "forwarding control". Conflating them leads to a wrong estimate of the migration effort from NAC to SDN. Enterprise teams familiar with RADIUS do not automatically know OpenFlow; the controller experience is very different. NAC only socialized the "controller decides policy, switch executes" pattern culturally; it did not inherit the implementation.

> **Common misconception:** *"VMware vDS was the first SDN deployment."* False. vDS (2009) is centrally managed, but the protocol between vCenter and the ESXi hosts is **proprietary internal**, not a standards-track open protocol. There is no programmable match-action table per a public specification. vDS is **proto-SDN** (it has the centralized control-plane pattern), but it is not SDN by the ONF 2011 definition (an open interface between the controller and the forwarding element). This distinction matters because it explains why VMware had to acquire Nicira in July 2012 for 1.26 billion US dollars to obtain NSX. vDS was not open enough and not cross-vendor enough to compete with the OpenFlow ecosystem. Nicira and OVN bridge that gap with the OpenFlow standard for the data plane.
