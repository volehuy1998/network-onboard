# 0.0. How to read the sdn-onboard series

> **Language status:** English (full migration complete, plan v3.12 R1.A run 1, 2026-04-29).

Lab environment: Ubuntu 22.04 LTS (Jammy). Primary references: [sdn-onboard/README.md](README.md), the table of contents for the 20 parts, the Mermaid dependency diagram, and Appendix A (Version Evolution Tracker).

## Learning objectives

After completing this part, you will be able to:

1. **Identify (Remember)** the reading path that fits your background. There are four main paths: Linear (sequential), OVS-only, OVN-focused, and Incident responder.
2. **Explain (Understand)** how the documentation is layered from Block 0 to Block XVII, the dependency relationships between parts, and why some content is a prerequisite for the parts that come later.
3. **Apply (Practice)** the conventions for Key Topic, Guided Exercise, Lab, Trouble Ticket, and version annotations during self-study and reference lookup.
4. **Analyze (Compare)** the existing CCNA and RHCSA knowledge in the series against new SDN concepts so that you can plan your learning time efficiently.

## Prerequisites

The series assumes that you have a solid foundation in the following two areas. If you are not yet confident, review the basic material before starting Block I:

- **Computer networking basics (CCNA 200-301 level):** Ethernet frame structure, 802.1Q VLAN (trunk and access), STP, IPv4 routing, ARP, the TCP and UDP state machines, the three-way handshake, and IP subnetting. If you need to strengthen this area, see the `network-onboard/` series (CCNA in Vietnamese).
- **Linux operating system basics (RHCSA EX200 level):** Managing systemd services, network namespaces, basic iptables and nftables, package management, journalctl, and the Linux process model (fork and exec). If you need to strengthen this area, see the `linux-onboard/` series.

You do not need any prior knowledge of OpenFlow, OVS, or OVN. Everything is built up methodically from the first step.

> **Documentation scope (revision 3, 2026-04-24):** The curriculum focuses purely on standalone OVS, OpenFlow, and OVN. It does not go deep into OpenStack, Neutron, or kolla-ansible. OVN concepts are presented in an upstream-portable way, so they apply to any orchestrator, including OVN-Kubernetes and bare-metal OVN.

## 0.0.1 Where the sdn-onboard series sits in the documentation system

The `network-onboard` repository contains four parallel series that together cover the core knowledge an infrastructure engineer needs today. The structure mirrors the natural skill progression of an infrastructure specialist:

The `linux-onboard` series provides the operating system foundation: file descriptors, epoll, network namespaces, systemd, and iptables. This is the base layer because every SDN component runs on Linux and depends closely on kernel mechanisms.

The `network-onboard` series (Cisco CCNA in Vietnamese) provides traditional networking thinking: L2 switching, STP, VLAN, L3 routing, and TCP state. This is the shared vocabulary needed to approach any networking technology. SDN does not replace these concepts. It redefines how they are controlled.

The `haproxy-onboard` series focuses on the L4 and L7 load balancer layer, a practical application that combines Linux and networking and that lives in user space on the data plane.

The `sdn-onboard` series (this one) goes deep into the control plane layer: how a centralized entity coordinates hundreds of distributed switches and replaces distributed protocols such as STP and OSPF with intent-based logic. SDN is a leap in mindset. It helps you understand why the OVN logical pipeline and distributed flow programming behave so differently from the traditional L2 bridge and OSPF model.

The typical reader is an engineer who already holds RHCSA or CCNA, who operates modern infrastructure (data center fabric, container networking, hypervisor networking), and who needs to master OVS and OVN at the architecture level. The goal is that when a real production incident such as FDB poisoning or a Geneve PMTUD black hole occurs (parts 17 to 19), you can read the logs and understand the root cause.

## 0.0.2 Reading paths

The series contains 16 foundation parts plus 3 advanced parts. Depending on your goal and the time you have, choose one of the four paths below.

### Path 1: Linear, like a university course

Read parts 0 through 13 in order, then 17, 18, and 19. With about 30,000 lines of content, this takes 3 to 5 months at a steady pace of 2 to 4 days per part. This path suits beginners and engineers preparing for in-depth networking certifications such as the networking section of CKA or CKS.

**Advantage:** the knowledge is continuous, with no gaps in the prerequisites. **Disadvantage:** it takes a lot of time, and motivation can drop if you do not have a concrete goal from the start.

### Path 2: OVS-only, focus only on the data plane

Read parts 0 to 4 (SDN history, OpenFlow, and flow programming), then parts 8 to 11 (Linux primer, OVS internals, OVSDB, overlay protocols, and tunnel labs). Skip Block XIII (OVN) and parts 17 to 19.

This path suits engineers who deploy Kubernetes or Docker with CNIs such as Cilium, Calico, or Antrea (plugins that use OVS or eBPF as their data plane). It takes 2 to 3 weeks.

### Path 3: OVN-focused, for engineers who already know OVS well

Read parts 0 to 11 quickly (overlay protocols), then part 13 (OVN foundation), and then parts 17 to 19 (advanced case studies).

This path is for engineers deploying standalone OVN or working directly on OVN incidents in production. Estimated time: 1 to 2 weeks.

### Path 4: Incident responder, jump straight into the real world

When you face a production incident, look up the corresponding parts directly:

- Traffic black hole between chassis, with `ovn-controller` logs reporting "FDB entry poisoned": see part 17 (FDB poisoning case study).
- Connectivity loss after VM live migration: see part 18 (ARP responder and BUM suppression) and part 19 (multichassis binding).
- Large TCP requests time out but ping still works: see part 19 §19.2 (Geneve PMTUD failure).

Each case study (parts 17 to 19) is self-contained, with a topology diagram, a real log timeline, root cause analysis, and the fix.

## 0.0.3 Conventions used in the documentation

The series uses several visual marker systems so you can quickly tell different kinds of content apart.

### Key Topic (the core idea)

Marker: a blockquote starting with `**Key topic:**`. These are the ideas that you absolutely cannot miss. If you skip them, you will misunderstand the entire mechanism that follows. The end of every part has a table that summarizes the Key Topics for fast review.

> **Key topic:** OVN uses Geneve tunnels to carry traffic between chassis instead of VXLAN. Geneve supports an Option field that carries tunnel metadata such as the logical port ID and the datapath ID. This lets OVN encode the entire logical topology into every packet. Traditional VXLAN does not have this capability.

### Guided Exercise, Lab, and Trouble Ticket

Three levels of practice with clear pedagogical intent:

- **Guided Exercise:** provides every step, the commands to run, the expected output, and a detailed explanation.
- **Lab:** provides only the scenario and a high-level goal. You must apply the knowledge yourself to solve the problem.
- **Trouble Ticket:** a real-world scenario from production. You are guided through the workflow: Verify, Isolate, Diagnose, and Fix.

### Version annotation

Marker: a blockquote starting with `**Version note:**`. Used to point out behavior differences between OVS, OVN, or Ubuntu LTS versions. All version notes are summarized in Appendix A of `README.md` for easy lookup.

### Cross-reference format

Internal cross-references use the form `Part X.Y` or `§X.Y`. For example: "Part 19 §19.2 analyzes the PMTUD failure". When you open a file, search for the matching heading.

## 0.0.4 How the series maps to international certifications (CCNA, RHCSA, CKA, CKS)

This series does not re-teach the basics. It builds a strong bridge between traditional theory and modern SDN.

| Topic | CCNA or RHCSA knowledge | What does SDN add? |
|-------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Ethernet framing, MAC learning | INE Network 2 | Part 1: traditional L2 switching versus SDN-based forwarding |
| VLAN, 802.1Q trunking | INE Network 5 | Parts 1 and 17: how VLANs work inside an overlay and the risk of FDB poisoning |
| STP, loop prevention | INE Network 6 | Part 1: how SDN removes STP and uses paths precomputed by the controller |
| TCP three-way handshake | INE Network 10.5 | Part 4: the OpenFlow control channel uses TCP or TLS |
| Linux bridge, veth | linux-onboard | Part 8: how OVS inherits and extends the Linux bridge concept |
| systemd services | RHCSA | Part 9: lifecycle management of `ovs-vswitchd` and `ovn-controller` |
| iptables, nftables | RHCSA | Parts 8.3 and 9.9: connection tracking integrated into the OVS pipeline |

For CKA and CKS candidates, the series strongly complements the networking section (Service, Ingress, NetworkPolicy). Understanding overlay mechanics, VXLAN and Geneve encapsulation, and how CNIs operate at the lower layer makes you confident with Kubernetes infrastructure.

## 0.0.5 Study advice

**Self-check rule:** after each part, look at the Key Topics table. If you cannot recall at least 70 percent of the content without opening the file, that is a signal to read the part again. SDN knowledge is layered. A weak layer below makes the layers on top wobbly.

Do not hesitate to go back to the prerequisites if you struggle. Conversely, if a part feels too easy, move faster or switch to one of the shorter paths to use your time well.
