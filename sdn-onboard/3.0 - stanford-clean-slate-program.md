## Context: why the Internet needed a clean slate in the mid-2000s

In the early 2000s, several leading academic research groups in the United States and Europe arrived at a common conclusion. **The Internet architecture, designed in the 1970s and 1980s by Vint Cerf, Bob Kahn, Jon Postel, and other pioneers, had reached its natural limits in evolvability.** The core protocols (IP, TCP, BGP, OSPF) still worked. But every time a new feature was needed (mobility, security, quality of service, multicast), the effort to modify or extend the protocols faced strong resistance from the huge installed base.

One influential paper at this time was **"Overcoming the Internet Impasse through Virtualization"** by Larry Peterson (Princeton), Scott Shenker (UC Berkeley), and Jonathan Turner (Washington University). It was published in IEEE Computer in April 2005 (Volume 38, Issue 4, pages 34 to 41). The paper stated the problem clearly. The Internet had reached an impasse. It could no longer evolve, because every router vendor was locked into the existing protocols. The proposed solution was virtualization of the infrastructure. Many parallel architectures could run on the same physical substrate. Each architecture would have its own routing and forwarding. Researchers could test new architectures without affecting production traffic.

This idea of "virtualizing the Internet" led to two parallel programs. The NSF (National Science Foundation) launched the Future Internet Design (FIND) program in 2005 with a budget of 20 to 30 million United States dollars per year for academic research on Internet architecture. NSF also funded GENI (Global Environment for Network Innovations), a national testbed that later became the platform for OpenFlow experiments from 2010 to 2015.

In parallel, Stanford University started its own program named **Clean Slate Design for the Internet**. It was funded mainly by the Stanford Computer Science Department, with participation from NSF and an industry consortium. The program officially started in late 2006 with Nick McKeown as Principal Investigator and Guru Parulkar as Executive Director.

The two programs (NSF FIND and Stanford Clean Slate) had significant overlap in people (many researchers participated in both) and in ideas, but they differed in scope. FIND was a funding mechanism for scattered projects across many fields. Clean Slate was a focused program at one institution with dedicated physical infrastructure.

## 3.0.1 Stanford Clean Slate: funded 2006 to 2012, goals

### Organizational structure

The Clean Slate Design for the Internet Program at Stanford (cloud.stanford.edu/cleanslate, archived versions available via the Internet Archive) started in late 2006 and ran until around 2011 to 2012. Nick McKeown, Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at Stanford, was the lead Principal Investigator (PI). Guru Parulkar, a senior research engineer who later became the Executive Director of the Open Networking Research Center (ONRC, a spin-off of Clean Slate in 2011), served as Program Director.

The program had three main research directions:

**Direction 1, redesign of the Internet architecture.** Rebuild the TCP/IP protocol stack with modern ideas (mobility-first, secure by default, content-oriented). Notable projects included HIP (Host Identity Protocol), Content-Centric Networking (later picked up by Van Jacobson and the team at PARC), and Triad (Triadic Addressing).

**Direction 2, redesign of data center networks.** Apply clean-slate principles specifically to the data center. Projects included PortLand (in collaboration with UCSD), SEATTLE (a flat L2 design with Geneve-style encapsulation), and Jupiter (in collaboration with Google starting in 2009).

**Direction 3, software-defined networking.** In a later phase (2007 to 2009), this direction emerged as the dominant one. It produced Ethane (2007), NOX (2008), and OpenFlow (2009). At first it was only one part of a broader direction, but it quickly took over in attention and impact.

### Funding sources: NSF, DARPA, and an industry consortium

The Clean Slate Program received funding from several sources:

- **NSF**, through the FIND program (the main federal source).
- **DARPA** (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency), with several projects focused on network security and availability.
- **Industry consortium**: Cisco, Deutsche Telekom, DoCoMo (Japan), Ericsson, NEC, NTT (Nippon Telegraph and Telephone), and Xilinx (an FPGA vendor, important for hardware prototyping). Each industry member contributed several hundred thousand United States dollars per year for early access to research results, with no exclusive intellectual property rights.

This industry consortium was particularly important for OpenFlow later, because it gave Stanford hardware test partners (Cisco and NEC shipped switches with OpenFlow firmware from 2010 to 2011) without having to build everything from scratch.

The total budget was estimated at 3 to 5 million United States dollars per year, over 5 to 6 active years. Roughly 20 to 40 researchers (faculty, postdocs, PhD students, and research engineers) participated at any given time.

## 3.0.2 Key researchers and their roles

### The four core roles

**Nick McKeown** (Professor, Stanford Electrical Engineering and Computer Science) was the lead PI. Before Clean Slate, McKeown was already known for research in switching fabric and hardware networking. He was a co-author of the textbook "High-Performance Switches and Routers" (Wiley 2001, with HJ Chao). His paper on iSLIP arbitration (IEEE Networking 1999) was deployed in the Cisco 12000 and in many commercial silicon products. McKeown brought hardware architecture expertise and hardware prototyping practice to Clean Slate, particularly through the NetFPGA platform that the McKeown group developed starting in 2005.

**Guru Parulkar** (Executive Director, 2007 to 2011) was responsible for program management and industry liaison. Parulkar had been a professor at Washington University in St. Louis before moving to Stanford. He had deep experience with industry partnerships and program execution. Parulkar's role was especially important in securing industry funding and in keeping a balance between academic goals (publications, student training) and industry deliverables (working prototypes, deployable specifications). After 2011, Parulkar moved on to lead the Open Networking Research Center (ONRC) and then ON.Lab (2012).

**Scott Shenker** (Professor, UC Berkeley and ICSI, the International Computer Science Institute) was the theory anchor. Shenker had decades of contributions to networking theory, starting with congestion control work in the 1980s (the Jacobson-Shenker-Floyd congestion control algorithm), then Content-Centric Networking and policy abstraction. Shenker's role in Clean Slate was theoretical foundation. When other researchers prototyped a new protocol, Shenker was often the person who asked "why is this approach correct, and not another?" and who turned intuition into a formal argument that could be published.

**Martin Casado** (PhD student at Stanford, advised by McKeown) was the lead implementer during the period when OpenFlow was born. Part 2.4 covered Casado in detail: SANE in 2006, Ethane in 2007, NOX in 2008, PhD thesis in November 2007. This part does not repeat the detail. Instead, it emphasizes Casado's transition from research to commercial work. From 2007 to 2009, Casado was still active in Clean Slate as a PhD student, while also being a part-time co-founder of Nicira. After his thesis defense in 2007, Casado moved full-time to Nicira in 2008.

### Other important contributors

Beyond the four core roles, many other people played important roles in Clean Slate:

**Hari Balakrishnan** (MIT professor, Stanford collaborator) was a co-author of the OpenFlow CCR paper of April 2008. He brought a transport-protocol research perspective.

**Jennifer Rexford** (Princeton professor, formerly at AT&T Research) was a co-author of the OpenFlow CCR paper. She connected Clean Slate with the 4D Project (Part 2.3) and helped bring 4D ideas into the practical OpenFlow implementation.

**Justin Pettit** (research engineer at Stanford, later at Nicira as co-founder) was a co-author of the Ethane paper and a lead implementer of the OpenFlow protocol in Open vSwitch.

**Ben Pfaff** (at Nicira, then Red Hat and the OVN project) designed and built Open vSwitch. OVS is one of the most important software artifacts from the Clean Slate era. It later became the primary data plane for OVN and for every Linux-based SDN deployment.

**Teemu Koponen** (PhD student at Helsinki, later at Nicira) was a co-author of NOX. He then led Onix, the distributed version of NOX, presented at OSDI 2010.

This was a rare ensemble in research history. Many of these people later became industry leaders. Casado is now a venture capitalist at a16z. Pettit and Pfaff lead OVN. Rexford is Dean of Princeton SEAS. But during the Clean Slate years, they worked closely as one coherent team.

> **Key point.** Clean Slate succeeded not just because of technology, but because of a combination of theory (Shenker), architecture (McKeown), implementation (Casado, Pettit, Pfaff), and program management (Parulkar). Without any one of these roles, OpenFlow might not have made it to the level of an industry standard. Successful academic research at scale needs a "full stack" team, not just one outstanding individual.

## 3.0.3 Deployment on the Stanford campus: the first real-world testbed

### Gates Computer Science Building, 2008 to 2009

In late 2008 and early 2009, the Stanford Computer Science Department deployed OpenFlow on the production network of the Gates Building, a four-story building containing offices for many CS faculty and students. The deployment included:

- **8 to 10 HP ProCurve 5400 switches** with prototype OpenFlow firmware provided by HP Labs. HP had been an industry partner of Clean Slate from the start. The HP ProCurve team in Palo Alto was located near Stanford and collaborated directly with the McKeown team.
- **One NOX controller** running on a dedicated Linux server in the McKeown lab, in the basement of the Gates Building.
- **Several hundred real users**: faculty, postdocs, graduate students, and some administrative staff of the CS department. This was not an isolated testbed. The traffic was real users using the Internet for email, web, video conferencing, and research work.

The deployment played the role of "eat your own dog food". If OpenFlow did not work, the Stanford CS faculty and students themselves would feel the impact directly. This was an important commitment. It proved the team believed in the technology enough to use it for themselves.

### Lessons from the deployment

The 4 to 6 month deployment (2008 to 2009) provided important feedback for OpenFlow specification refinement:

**Lesson 1: controller latency matters for TCP.** The first-packet model added 5 to 10 milliseconds of latency. With a TCP handshake of 3 round-trips, the total user-perceived setup latency was 20 to 50 milliseconds. This was acceptable for browsers but slow for VoIP call setup. Aggressive flow-entry caching was needed so that subsequent connections did not pay the first-packet latency.

**Lesson 2: controller availability is critical.** When the NOX controller crashed (which happened several times during the deployment), switches kept forwarding along existing flows (fail-open), but they could not establish new flows. Every new TCP connection failed until the controller restarted. This led to the requirement for controller high availability (multiple redundant controllers) in future specifications.

**Lesson 3: flow table size is limited.** The HP ProCurve 5400 had about 1500 to 3000 flow entries in its hardware table. With the production traffic of the Gates Building (tens of thousands of active connections), the flow table filled up quickly and rules had to be evicted aggressively. The default `idle_timeout` of 60 seconds turned out to be too long. A shorter value of 5 to 10 seconds was needed.

**Lesson 4: debugging is hard.** When a packet was dropped or misrouted, tracing the path through the chain of switch, controller, policy, and installed flow was difficult. Tools such as `ofproto/trace` (later available in OVS, see Part 9.11) were needed to simulate the packet path without actual traffic.

Three of these four lessons (availability, table size, debugging) became known design issues of SDN that were addressed in later evolution: multi-controller support in OF 1.2, the group table in OF 1.3, and `ofproto/trace` in OVS tooling.

### GENI: scaling to a national testbed

After the Stanford deployment proved OpenFlow viable, NSF funded the GENI (Global Environment for Network Innovations) program to scale OpenFlow to a national testbed from 2010 to 2015. GENI connected OpenFlow switches at about 30 universities across the United States over the Internet2 backbone (a high-speed research network). Students and researchers could reserve a slice of GENI, deploy their own OpenFlow controller, and test new protocols.

GENI was the at-scale "production test" of OpenFlow before industry adoption. From 2010 to 2013, hundreds of papers were published based on GENI experiments, validating the ability of OpenFlow to handle real Internet-scale traffic. When industry players such as Google B4 (2013) and Microsoft SWAN (2013) deployed OpenFlow in WAN production, they had GENI as a precedent and proof of concept.

## 3.0.4 "OpenFlow: Enabling Innovation in Campus Networks", ACM CCR April 2008

### Eight authors from five institutions

The most influential paper for the start of OpenFlow was published in ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review, Volume 38, Issue 2, in April 2008. It had 8 authors from 5 institutions:

- **Nick McKeown** (Stanford)
- **Tom Anderson** (University of Washington)
- **Hari Balakrishnan** (MIT)
- **Guru Parulkar** (Stanford)
- **Larry Peterson** (Princeton)
- **Jennifer Rexford** (Princeton)
- **Scott Shenker** (UC Berkeley)
- **Jonathan Turner** (Washington University in St. Louis)

Note that this list includes several authors who were not part of the Stanford Clean Slate Program (Anderson at UW, Balakrishnan at MIT, Peterson at Princeton, Turner at WashU). This was deliberate. The paper was planned as a marketing launch of the OpenFlow concept for the broader academic community. Top-tier authors from many institutions were needed to add credibility.

### Three contributions of the paper

The paper had 6 main pages (pages 69 to 74 in CCR 38(2)). It was compact but very carefully structured:

**Contribution 1: OpenFlow is the minimum viable abstraction for programmable forwarding.** The paper argued that researchers had tried Active Networking (Part 2.1) with an ambitious goal (arbitrary code per packet) and had failed because it was too complex. They had then tried ForCES (Part 2.3) with an internal CE/FE split and had failed because the scope was wrong. OpenFlow chose a middle approach. The "match plus action plus priority" abstraction was strong enough to cover real use cases, and simple enough to deploy on commodity hardware (HP ProCurve, NEC switches, Linksys routers).

**Contribution 2: an architecture of controller, flow table, and secure channel.** The paper formalized the architecture with three components:

- **Controller**: a software entity (NOX or custom), where policy lives.
- **Flow table**: in the switch, where packets are matched and actions are applied.
- **Secure channel**: the protocol between controller and switch (this was before the OpenFlow 1.0 specification, so the paper used the generic term).

This architecture seems simple, but the paper defined the responsibilities and interactions between components clearly. This let multiple vendors build interoperable switches and let multiple researchers build interoperable controllers.

**Contribution 3: a deployment roadmap in campus networks.** The paper argued that the campus is the most convenient place to deploy the first production OpenFlow. A campus has a mix of research demand (faculty want to experiment), friendly operators (campus network admins are often faculty colleagues, willing to take risk), and available budget (university IT often has a research budget). The paper called on the academic community to deploy OpenFlow on their own campuses.

### Reaction: hardware vendors picked it up

The CCR paper of April 2008 had a fast and clear impact. In the 6 to 12 months after publication:

- **HP** started shipping prototype OpenFlow firmware for the ProCurve 5400 (2008 Q3 to Q4).
- **NEC** developed the NEC IP8800 switch with native OpenFlow support (2009 Q1).
- **Netgear** released a consumer switch with OpenFlow support (research-grade quality).
- **Stanford** and UC Berkeley started shipping NetFPGA boards with an OpenFlow reference implementation for researchers.

By the end of 2009, the hardware ecosystem was sufficient to support the initial OpenFlow 1.0 specification. The CCR paper of April 2008 was the trigger that made this ecosystem start to form.

> **Key point.** The CCR paper of April 2008 was not a specification (the specification was OpenFlow 1.0, published on December 31, 2009). It was a **manifesto** that made industry start to support the technology. Successful academic papers usually have to do two things: prove that the idea is technically feasible, and convince industry to invest. The CCR paper of April 2008 did both. This became a template for later SDN innovations (for example, the P4 paper in CCR July 2014).

## 3.0.5 Nicira founded in August 2007: the commercial arm

### Compressed timeline

Part 2.4 §2.4.4 covered Casado's PhD thesis and the transition to Nicira in detail. This part gives only a condensed timeline of events relevant to the Block III context:

- **August 2007.** Nicira Networks was founded in Palo Alto. The founding team was Martin Casado (CTO), Nick McKeown (co-founder, part-time), Scott Shenker (co-founder, part-time), Justin Pettit (engineer), and Ben Pfaff (engineer). Series A funding came from Andreessen Horowitz and NEA, about 20 million United States dollars.

- **2007 to 2009.** Focus on enterprise security in the Ethane model. The product was not commercially successful because the market was not ready.

- **2009 to 2011.** Pivot to data center network virtualization. Nicira NVP (Network Virtualization Platform) was released in 2011 with an overlay-based approach (a VXLAN precursor).

- **July 2012.** VMware announced the acquisition of Nicira for **1.26 billion United States dollars** (announcement on July 23, 2012). This was the largest networking startup deal in history at that time.

### Meaning for OpenFlow and SDN

Nicira was founded in August 2007, the same period when OpenFlow began to be formalized. There was a mutual relationship between the two:

**Benefit for OpenFlow.** Nicira provided a commercial vehicle for the Stanford ideas. Nicira engineers continued to work on the OpenFlow specification, on Open vSwitch, and on the NOX controller. They brought industry resources (engineers, budget, marketing) into the research project.

**Benefit for Nicira.** Nicira kept access to research output from Stanford through McKeown and Shenker, who were part-time co-founders. Nicira's product line depended on the progress of OpenFlow.

**Meaning after the VMware acquisition in July 2012.** VMware's acquisition of Nicira effectively brought Stanford ideas into a commercial product used by hundreds of thousands of enterprise customers. NSX (the brand name for NVP after 2013) became the leading network virtualization platform for vSphere. This was the endpoint of the chain Ethane, OpenFlow, Nicira, NSX. It is a research-to-commerce chain that is rare in networking history.

A forward reference to Part 5.1 (Hypervisor overlays, the Nicira NVP to VMware NSX to OVN lineage). That part will analyze the NVP architecture in depth and its impact on present-day OVN. In particular, after 2015 Pettit and Pfaff left VMware, joined Red Hat, and started the OVN project. They brought the Nicira architecture back into open source, which formed the foundation for Block XIII (OVN foundation) of this curriculum.
