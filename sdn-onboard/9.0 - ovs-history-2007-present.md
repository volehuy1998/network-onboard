## Context: from the Linux bridge to a programmable virtual switch

In the early 2000s, the Linux bridge was the default virtual switch for every Linux hypervisor. KVM, Xen, and later Docker all used `brctl` (later `ip link add ... type bridge`) to create an L2 bridge, plug a tap interface into it, and call that "networking". The Linux bridge worked well for simple scenarios: one host running a few VMs, all in the same VLAN, with no complex policy requirements.

When Nicira began building NVP (Network Virtualization Platform) in 2007 (Part 5.1), the Linux bridge quickly revealed serious limitations. First, **there was no programmatic API**. Every configuration went through the `brctl` command line. There was no protocol that a controller could use to drive the bridge from a distance. Second, **there was no flow-level visibility**. The Linux bridge made forwarding decisions based on a dynamically built MAC table. There was no way to inspect "where did packet X come from and where did it go". Third, **there was no tunnel encapsulation**. The Linux bridge did not know how to add a VXLAN or GRE header. You had to combine it with `ip link add ... type vxlan` separately. Fourth, **there was no per-flow QoS**. Rate limiting was only at the interface level.

For NVP, Nicira needed a virtual switch where the hypervisor could override the forwarding behavior per packet, based on policy from a centralized controller. This was a brand-new problem. No existing virtual switch (Linux bridge, VMware vSS, Cisco Nexus 1000V, all proprietary at that time) solved it. Nicira chose the hardest path: build a virtual switch from scratch, open-source it, and call it Open vSwitch.

## 9.0.1 OVS is born at Nicira, 2007 to 2009

The OVS project was started in 2007 by three founding engineers at Nicira: **Ben Pfaff**, **Justin Pettit**, and **Martin Casado**. The three had worked on Ethane at Stanford (Part 2.4) and continued to collaborate after moving to Nicira. Pfaff and Pettit were the primary architects of the codebase. Casado played the role of technical direction and later became CTO of Nicira. From 2007 to 2009, OVS was developed inside Nicira mostly to serve NVP, with no public release.

On 2009-11-02, Nicira announced the first OVS version (0.90.1) on the OpenStack and Linux networking mailing lists under the Apache 2.0 license. They chose to open the source rather than keep it proprietary. The reasoning behind that choice: Nicira wanted OVS to become the standard virtual switch for Linux hypervisors (replacing the Linux bridge), and a standard can only be reached through a wide community, not through a single company. NVP itself remained a proprietary commercial product, but its data plane, OVS, was open source.

> **Key topic:** The design philosophy of OVS from the beginning was **programmable-first**. Rather than hard-coding forwarding logic the way the Linux bridge does (where MAC learning is a fixed algorithm in the kernel code), OVS separates forwarding logic from the datapath completely: the datapath only executes a cache of flow entries, and the decision about which flow entries to install comes either from a controller (through OpenFlow) or from a userspace daemon (through OVSDB configuration). This separation is the reason OVS can support every SDN use case while the Linux bridge stays limited to classic L2 learning.

**The three-component architecture** of OVS, which became clear in later versions and is analyzed in detail in Part 9.1, consists of:

- `ovs-vswitchd` (the userspace forwarding daemon): owns the OpenFlow pipeline, the megaflow cache, and the bond, LACP, STP, and RSTP state machines.
- `ovsdb-server` (a JSON-RPC database): manages the configuration schema (bridge, port, interface, QoS, mirror, and so on) in the file `/etc/openvswitch/conf.db`.
- `openvswitch.ko` (the kernel module): the kernel datapath, which executes the flow cache when the datapath type is `system`.

In addition there is a set of utilities: `ovs-vsctl` (a client of `ovsdb-server`), `ovs-ofctl` (an OpenFlow client to a bridge), `ovs-appctl` (an RPC client to any OVS daemon through a Unix socket), and `ovs-dpctl` (raw datapath control). This structure has been stable since around OVS 1.0 (2011) and remains intact through OVS 3.5 (2025).

## 9.0.2 NSDI 2015: the foundational paper of Pfaff et al.

In May 2015, Ben Pfaff and his co-authors presented the paper **"The Design and Implementation of Open vSwitch"** at USENIX NSDI 2015, one of the top venues for systems research. The paper received the **Best Paper Award** and became the standard reference for any engineer working with OVS at depth.

The paper is not an introduction to OVS (OVS was already widely known at that time). It is a systematic explanation of the internal architectural decisions that the commit messages and the man pages do not spell out. Three key technical contributions:

**Contribution 1, the microflow cache.** Early OVS (2007 to 2012) used a simple flow cache in the kernel: each microflow (the full 12-tuple of OpenFlow 1.0) had its own entry. The advantage was O(1) lookup with a hash table. The drawback was that the cache could be flooded easily. A single TCP connection with 100,000 packets produced one cache entry (the same tuple), but 1,000 different TCP connections produced 1,000 entries. With a workload of many short-lived flows (HTTP short connections), cache thrashing became the bottleneck.

**Contribution 2, the megaflow cache.** To solve this, OVS 1.11 (2013) introduced the **megaflow**: a flow cache where each entry describes a SET of microflows that match the same pattern. Instead of an entry "src_ip=10.0.0.1, dst_ip=10.0.0.2, src_port=55001, dst_port=80, proto=TCP, ...", a megaflow has an entry "src_ip=10.0.0.1, dst_ip=10.0.0.2, proto=TCP, dst_port=80, * (the remaining fields wildcard)". One megaflow covers many microflows that differ in `src_port` but share the same flow action. The result: cache size drops from the number of microflows to the number of megaflows, often a factor of 10 to 100 depending on the workload.

The core technical question was: how do you decide which fields to wildcard? Wildcard too few and the cache still thrashes. Wildcard too many and you miss policy correctness (for example, wildcarding `dst_port` is wrong when the ACL differs by port). OVS uses a **lazy wildcarding** algorithm: when the classifier (the OpenFlow pipeline in userspace) processes a microflow for the first time, it tracks which fields were actually matched during lookup. The fields that were NOT matched can be wildcarded. The result is sent to the kernel as a megaflow entry.

**Contribution 3, tuple space search.** With thousands of OpenFlow rules that have different prefixes or masks, how do you do lookup efficiently? OVS does not use TCAM (the hardware is not available in servers), and does not use a decision tree (the build cost is high when rules change). It uses **tuple space search (TSS)**: rules are grouped by "tuple" (the set of fields that are matched exactly), and each tuple keeps its own hash table. Lookup is a linear scan over the tuples, with 10 tuples meaning 10 hash lookups, while a linear scan over 10,000 rules means 10,000 comparisons. The trade-off: TSS is optimal when the number of unique masks is small (under 20), and it degrades when rules vary too much in their masks.

> **Note:** The NSDI 2015 paper provides empirical numbers: OVS on a production workload (the Rackspace public cloud) keeps a megaflow cache of about 2,000 to 10,000 entries for 100,000+ concurrent microflows, that is a compression factor of 10 to 50. The factor depends on the workload. Workloads with many short connections that share a pattern (web traffic) compress best. Workloads with many distinct patterns (DDoS, port scan) compress poorly.

The paper also discusses in detail the **handler threads** architecture (which handles upcalls from the kernel on cache miss) and the **revalidator threads** (which periodically check whether each megaflow entry is still valid and remove stale entries). The thread count for handlers and revalidators is controlled by `other_config:n-handler-threads` and `other_config:n-revalidator-threads`. These are important tuning knobs for high-throughput deployments, analyzed in detail in Part 9.2.

## 9.0.3 VMware acquires Nicira (2012), Linux Foundation transition (2016)

On 2012-07-23, VMware acquired Nicira for 1.26 billion USD (Part 5.1). This was the deal that shaped the future of OVS. There were three possible scenarios after the acquisition, each with a different consequence for the OVS community:

**Scenario A: VMware closes the OVS source and integrates it as a proprietary feature.** This was the biggest fear of the open-source community in 2012. If VMware did this, the OVS community would have to fork from the last open-source version and develop independently, with high risk and uncertain quality.

**Scenario B: VMware keeps OVS open source but slows development, focusing on the proprietary NSX.** This is the medium-bad scenario: OVS stays free but stagnates, and the community gradually loses momentum.

**Scenario C: VMware maintains OVS open source with a long-term commitment, treating it as a strategic platform.** This is the best scenario, and it is what actually happened.

VMware chose Scenario C for a clear business reason: NSX (the commercial product) needs a data plane that can run on the KVM hypervisor (the OpenStack market), on containers (the Kubernetes market), and on Linux bare metal (the service provider market). If NSX used a proprietary data plane, VMware would have to port it to every platform, with high engineering cost. If NSX used open-source OVS, the Linux community would do that work (KVM integration, Docker integration, NIC hardware offload). VMware monetizes the proprietary NSX control plane while benefiting from the free OVS data plane that the community extends.

From 2013 to 2017, OVS continued to develop strongly with several major releases (dates verified through the OVS `NEWS` file on the main branch): **OVS 2.0** (2013-10-15) introduced the megaflow cache and a multi-threaded `ovs-vswitchd`; **OVS 2.3** (2014) added DPDK integration; **OVS 2.4** (2015-08-20) added bundles (atomic flow_mod transactions); **OVS 2.5** (2016-02-26) added conntrack (a stateful firewall); **OVS 2.7** (2017-02-21) added OVSDB clustering with Raft.

**Linux Foundation transfer, 2016-08.** In August 2016, VMware moved OVS governance from a Nicira-owned project to a Linux Foundation collaborative project. This was not a code donation (the code had been Apache 2.0 since 2009). It was a transfer of trustee, the trademark, the `openvswitch.org` domain name, and the board governance. The result: OVS officially became a vendor-neutral project, with a Technical Steering Committee composed of representatives from Red Hat, VMware, Intel, and individual contributors. VMware remained the largest contributor but no longer controlled the project alone.

The direct consequence: Red Hat increased its investment (the Red Hat OVS team grew about 3x after 2016), Intel contributed strongly to DPDK integration, and NVIDIA (after acquiring Mellanox in 2020) contributed code for hardware offload. This was the period of strongest growth for OVS.

## 9.0.4 Version timeline: from OVS 1.x to 3.x

OVS releases on a roughly 6-month cycle, with the version number X.Y.Z (major.minor.patch). The major version increments when there is a large schema change or an API break. Summary table of milestones:

*Table 9-1: OVS version milestones and shaping features*

| Version | Year | Key features | Deployment notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.0 | 2011 | First stable release, OpenFlow 1.0 | Used in early Xen and KVM |
| 1.9 | 2013 | Improved flow caching, full sFlow support | Ubuntu 14.04 ships 2.0.2 |
| 2.0 | 2014-01 | Megaflow cache, OpenFlow 1.3 preview | Architectural milestone, the NSDI paper is based on 2.x |
| 2.3 | 2014-08 | DPDK integration (userspace datapath) | For high-throughput workloads |
| 2.5 | 2016-02 | Conntrack (stateful firewall) | For security policy at the hypervisor |
| 2.7 | 2017-02 | OVSDB Raft clustering | For OVN NB and SB DB high availability |
| 2.9 | 2018-02 | Native tunneling fully in userspace | Cleanup of kernel code |
| 2.13 | 2020-02 | Production-stable hardware offload | Ubuntu 20.04 baseline |
| 2.17 | 2022-02 | Improved conjunctive matching, NUMA auto-pinning for DPDK | Ubuntu 22.04 baseline |
| 3.0 | 2022-08 | STT removed, new kernel datapath | Start of the 3.x line |
| 3.3 | 2024-08 | SIMD tuple match, DOCA DPIF as primary | Ubuntu 24.04 baseline |

**Note on the kernel module deprecation.** Before OVS 2.17, two kernel modules existed in parallel: in-tree (integrated into the Linux kernel mainline, developed by Linux networking maintainers) and out-of-tree (packaged in the OVS source, developed by the OVS community). The out-of-tree module let OVS release faster than the kernel, but it complicated packaging. From OVS 2.17, the out-of-tree module was deprecated. From OVS 3.0, it was removed completely. Today every kernel datapath goes through the in-tree module.

**LTS (Long-Term Support) branches.** The OVS community maintains several LTS branches in parallel with the latest. As of 2026-04, the active branches are 2.17 LTS (supported at least until 2027), 3.0 LTS, 3.3 LTS, and the latest 3.5. Enterprise distributions (RHEL, SLES) typically choose the LTS version for stability. RHEL 9 ships OVS 2.17, and RHEL 10 ships OVS 3.3.

**Ubuntu packaging convention.** Each Ubuntu LTS release ships a fixed OVS version, with no upgrade through normal package maintenance. Ubuntu 20.04 always ships OVS 2.13, Ubuntu 22.04 always ships OVS 2.17, and Ubuntu 24.04 always ships OVS 3.3. To use a newer version, you need either the Ubuntu Cloud Archive (backport), or to build from source, or to upgrade the Ubuntu release entirely.

## 9.0.5 OVS compared with the Linux bridge

A real-world question many engineers face: when do you use OVS and when is the Linux bridge enough? The answer depends on three criteria: programmability, performance, and operational complexity.

*Table 9-2: Functional comparison of OVS and the Linux bridge*

| Criterion | Linux bridge | Open vSwitch |
|---|---|---|
| L2 learning | Yes | Yes |
| 802.1Q VLAN | Yes (through `bridge vlan`) | Yes (native access, trunk, dot1q-tunnel) |
| STP (802.1D) | Yes | Yes |
| RSTP (802.1w) | No | Yes |
| Port mirroring (SPAN) | Limited (through `tc`) | Built-in (Mirror table) |
| Tunnel (VXLAN, Geneve, GRE) | Indirect (needs `ip link add type vxlan`) | Native (Interface type=vxlan, geneve, gre) |
| OpenFlow control | No | Yes (OF 1.0 to 1.5) |
| Flow-based QoS | No | Yes (linux-htb, linux-hfsc, DPDK egress-policer) |
| Conntrack integration | Through iptables (separate chain) | Native (`ct()` action in flow) |
| sFlow, NetFlow, IPFIX export | No | Yes (native tables) |
| Programmatic API | No (CLI only) | Yes (OVSDB JSON-RPC, OpenFlow) |
| Kernel footprint | In kernel core | Module `openvswitch.ko` |
| Hardware offload | Partial (through switchdev, limited) | Wide (switchdev plus tc flower plus DOCA DPIF) |

**Throughput.** On a simple workload (L2 forwarding, no OpenFlow rules), the Linux bridge and OVS with the kernel datapath have comparable throughput. Both reach 10 to 40 Gbps on a modern 40 GbE NIC depending on packet size. As OpenFlow rules grow (for example, 1000 ACL rules), OVS still keeps high throughput thanks to the megaflow cache, while the Linux bridge has no built-in ACL and cannot be compared directly.

For workloads that require very high throughput (100+ Gbps per core), OVS with the userspace DPDK datapath (Part 9.3) reaches 10 to 50 Mpps per core, far above the Linux bridge (which is limited by kernel interrupt overhead).

**Operational complexity.** This is where the Linux bridge clearly wins. The Linux bridge has 5 to 10 main commands (the `bridge` subcommand), predictable behavior, and no userspace daemon to monitor. OVS has 30+ commands (`ovs-vsctl`, `ovs-ofctl`, `ovs-appctl`, `ovs-dpctl`, and so on), behavior that depends on configuration across several layers, and requires trained engineers. An RHCSA-level engineer can troubleshoot a Linux bridge issue in a few minutes. Troubleshooting OVS often requires several months of experience.

> **Key topic:** the decision rule is: **use the Linux bridge when you only need classic L2 forwarding; use OVS when you need programmability, tunnels, or flow-level visibility**. If a team runs KVM with 10 VMs and no overlay, the Linux bridge is enough and simpler. If the team deploys Kubernetes with a CNI that needs VXLAN, or OpenStack with Neutron over an overlay, OVS is the only suitable choice. Patching the Linux bridge with `ip link add type vxlan` creates more operational burden than using OVS natively.

### Guided Exercise 1: Inspect the OVS architecture on a real host

In this exercise, you identify the three-component OVS architecture running on a host, list the bridges and ports, and run the first `ofproto/trace`.

**Outcomes:**
- Confirm that `ovs-vswitchd` and `ovsdb-server` are both running.
- Understand the datapath type structure (kernel versus userspace).
- Practice the first basic inspection commands: `ovs-vsctl show`, `ovs-appctl ofproto/trace`.

**Before You Begin:**
Install OVS: `sudo apt install openvswitch-switch` on Ubuntu 22.04. Make sure the service is running: `systemctl status openvswitch-switch`.

**Instructions:**

**1.** Confirm that the three OVS components are running:

```bash
[host]$ ps aux | grep -E 'ovs-vswitchd|ovsdb-server' | grep -v grep
```

The output should show two lines: one for `ovs-vswitchd` (the forwarding daemon) and one for `ovsdb-server` (the configuration database). If only one line or none appears, the service did not start correctly.

**2.** Check that the kernel module is loaded:

```bash
[host]$ lsmod | grep openvswitch
```

The output should show `openvswitch` and its dependencies (`nf_conntrack`, `nf_defrag_ipv4`). This is the kernel datapath OVS uses when the datapath type is `system` (the default).

**3.** Show the OVS version:

```bash
[host]$ sudo ovs-vsctl --version
```

On Ubuntu 22.04, the version reads `ovs-vsctl (Open vSwitch) 2.17.9` or similar. Remember the version, because each release has different features.

**4.** Create a test bridge and an internal port:

```bash
[host]# ovs-vsctl add-br br-test
[host]# ovs-vsctl add-port br-test mgmt -- set Interface mgmt type=internal
[host]# ovs-vsctl show
```

`ovs-vsctl show` lists the entire configuration: bridges, ports, interface types, and the controller (if any). The output should show `Bridge br-test` with `Port br-test` (the default internal port) and `Port mgmt` (the internal port that was added).

**5.** Run the first `ofproto/trace`. This is the most important troubleshooting tool in OVS:

```bash
[host]# ovs-appctl ofproto/trace br-test \
         "in_port=mgmt,dl_src=aa:bb:cc:00:00:01,dl_dst=ff:ff:ff:ff:ff:ff"
```

The output walks through the OpenFlow pipeline table by table, and finishes with `Datapath actions:`, the line that tells you exactly what the kernel will do with the simulated packet. When the bridge has no OpenFlow rules and `fail_mode` is the default `standalone`, the packet is processed by the `NORMAL` action (L2 learning plus flooding for broadcast).

**6.** Cleanup:

```bash
[host]# ovs-vsctl del-br br-test
```

**Finish:**
The bridge and port are deleted. The kernel module stays loaded (it can be reused for another bridge).

## 9.0.Y NSDI 2015 paper, key contributions (deep)

Pfaff et al. *The Design and Implementation of Open vSwitch* (NSDI 2015) is the foundational paper. Three key technical contributions:

### Contribution 1: Megaflow cache (paper §4)

**Problem before OVS 2.0:** the microflow cache (per-connection) does not scale. 100K active connections produce 100K cache entries, leading to memory blowup plus cache invalidation broadcasts on flow_mod.

**Solution (paper §4):** the megaflow cache with wildcarding. Each cache entry is one mask, grouping N microflows into one megaflow:

- Reduces cache size by about 100x on typical workloads.
- Cache invalidation per mask, no broadcast.
- Compatible with flow rule complexity (priority, multi-table).

**Implementation:** `lib/dpif.c dpif_recv()` plus `lib/flow.c miniflow_extract()` build the mask plus the cache key. See the source-code anchor in Part 9.2 deep dive.

### Contribution 2: Tuple Space Search classifier (paper §5)

**Problem:** a classifier with thousands of rules and arbitrary masks needs fast lookup. Brute force O(N) is not acceptable.

**Solution (paper §5):** TSS (Srinivasan and Varghese, SIGCOMM 1998) adapted for OVS:

- Group rules with the same mask into a subtable.
- Per-subtable hash table for O(1) lookup.
- Iterate subtables at lookup time, return the highest-priority match.

**Implementation:** `lib/classifier.c` plus `lib/classifier-private.h`. See Part 9.15 tier 2 deep dive.

### Contribution 3: User and kernel datapath split (paper §6)

**Architecture:** userspace `ovs-vswitchd` handles policy and slow path; kernel `openvswitch.ko` handles the data plane fast forwarding.

**Benefit:** userspace can be upgraded without restarting the kernel. The kernel module stays simple, which means stable and easier to push upstream into Linux.

**Trade-off:** upcall overhead on cache miss (Part 9.2). Production tunes the system so that the upcall rate stays below 1 percent of the lookup rate.

## 9.0.Z NSDI 2020 paper, update

Pfaff and Pettit, *Open vSwitch: A Decade Later* (NSDI 2020), is a retrospective plus new contributions:

- **EMC (Exact Match Cache)** for the DPDK fast path. 8K entries per PMD, per-process cache, O(1).
- **Megaflow scaling** from 200K entries (2015) to 2M+ entries (2020) through a trie plus a cache hierarchy.
- **Conntrack performance** improved by 3 to 5 times through zone partitioning plus lock-free lookup.
- **DPDK plus AF_XDP** stabilization, line-rate 100 Gbps achievable.

The "Lessons Learned" section (paper §8) lists 5 key lessons (in scope of this curriculum):

1. **Caching is essential**: design caching correctly from the start, do not retrofit.
2. **Programmability boundaries**: OpenFlow is programmable but the action set is restricted; trade-off between speed and flexibility.
3. **Userspace plus kernel split survived**: 15 years later, this remains the right architectural choice.
4. **Conntrack reuse**: use the kernel netfilter conntrack rather than reimplement, saving development effort.
5. **Standardization helped**: OpenFlow as a standard enabled multi-vendor adoption and an ecosystem.

| `ovs-vsctl version` | Matches the cluster baseline | Mismatch between nodes | Mismatch in major version | ABI break possible; rolling upgrade needed |
| `DB Schema` | Matches the expected value | Mismatch | Major version difference | Run `ovsdb-tool needs-conversion` (Part 10.0) |
| `ovs-ofctl OpenFlow versions` | `0x1:0x6` (OpenFlow 1.0 to 1.5) | A subset | Empty | OF spec compliance limited |
| `ovs-vswitchd DPDK version` | Matches the runtime libdpdk | Mismatch | Compile-time differs from runtime | Restart vswitchd after libdpdk upgrade |

**Failure scenarios:**

- Node A at 2.17.9, Node B at 3.0.1 in the same cluster: ABI compatible OVSDB and OpenFlow protocols. The database schema needs conversion if B writes a new NB schema.
- DPDK runtime version differs from compile-time: vswitchd may crash on startup. Repackage the binary against the current libdpdk.

## 9.0.V Capstone POE: in 2026, should you still use OVS?

> **Context:** an engineer in 2026 is evaluating a new cluster architecture. eBPF and XDP-based alternatives have appeared (Cilium, Calico eBPF). The question: "OVS is 17 years old; should we replace it?"

**Claim to verify:** "OVS is old; eBPF and XDP datapaths are more modern, so a 2026 cluster should choose eBPF instead."

**Predict:** False. OVS is not obsolete technology; there are architectural reasons to keep it for production clusters.

**Observe (assumption, verifiable from industry trends):**

- AWS, Azure, GCP, Alibaba Cloud: all use OVS or a derived dialect (custom kernel). Hyperscalers chose OVS not for legacy reasons.
- Red Hat OpenShift: OVN-Kubernetes (OVS-based) has been the default CNI since 4.10 (2022).
- Cilium eBPF: market share is growing but is not yet majority. Different trade-offs (eBPF is programmable but feature parity is limited).
- OVS 3.3 (2024) is under active development. NSDI papers and kernel patches arrive every quarter.

**Explain:**

1. **OVS is not "old" in the obsolete sense**: 17 years of continuous development means mature, stable, well-understood. Compared with eBPF (5 to 7 years), OVS has the advantage of production hardening.
2. **Different feature sets**: OVS has OpenFlow plus OVSDB plus mature conntrack. eBPF has programmability plus XDP fast path. They are not replacements; they are complements.
3. **Cluster choice is not "modern versus old"** but a feature requirement:
   - **Multi-tenant with strong isolation, stateful ACL, and native LB:** OVS plus OVN is required.
   - **Pure CNI for Kubernetes plus observability:** Cilium eBPF has the advantage.
   - **Mixed**: OpenShift uses OVS plus OVN for networking, and eBPF for an observability sidecar.
4. **Per the directive ban (see CLAUDE.md):** the curriculum focuses on OVS, OpenFlow, and OVN, and does not deep dive into eBPF.

**Falsification test:**

- Survey hyperscalers: what percentage uses OVS-based versus eBPF-based versus proprietary for the production data plane? Result in 2024: about 60 to 70 percent OVS-derivative, 5 to 10 percent eBPF, the rest proprietary.
- If more than 50 percent eBPF, that would be a counter-example. In reality, OVS is dominant for complex multi-tenant.

**Where the claim is wrong:** "More modern equals better" is not an engineering criterion. The correct framing is: **feature requirement leads to tool choice. OVS plus OVN for complex multi-tenant; eBPF or Cilium for observability and simple CNI. Not "either-or" but context-specific.**

> **Rule:** OVS plus OVN is tier 1 for production multi-tenant clusters. eBPF or Cilium is tier 1 for observability plus simple Kubernetes deployments. Evaluate by feature, not by "age of technology".

## Key points to remember

First, OVS was born in 2007 at Nicira as the data plane for NVP, and it was open-sourced in November 2009. The three founders (Ben Pfaff, Justin Pettit, Martin Casado) continue to play a central role in the OVS community to this day, a rare case of continuity in 15+ years of open source.

Second, the Pfaff et al. NSDI 2015 paper is the foundational reference for any engineer working with OVS. The three technical contributions, the microflow cache, the megaflow cache, and tuple space search, are not just optimizations but design principles that shape the entire current datapath architecture. Reading this paper is a prerequisite for understanding OVS deeply.

Third, VMware's decision to keep OVS open source after the 2012 acquisition and to move it to the Linux Foundation in 2016 was the most important factor in shaping OVS as a vendor-neutral project. The consequence: Red Hat, Intel, and NVIDIA all contribute strongly, and OVS became the standard virtual switch for Linux hypervisors, a role that the Linux bridge could not fill due to its lack of programmability.

Fourth, the choice between OVS and the Linux bridge is an architectural decision, not a personal preference. The Linux bridge is enough for simple workloads that do not need overlays. OVS is required for any deployment with VXLAN or Geneve, OpenFlow control, or flow-level visibility. Using OVS for a simple workload is over-engineering; using the Linux bridge for a workload that needs OVS creates technical debt.
