## Context

From the mid 1980s to the late 2000s, the architecture of the networking industry barely changed in principle. Every switch, router, and firewall was a closed box that combined three roles: forwarding packets (the data plane), computing routes (the control plane), and providing the operator interface (the management plane). All three roles ran on the same CPU, the same firmware, and the same proprietary operating system from a single vendor. The model worked well when the Internet was small and traffic was mostly client-server in the north-south direction.

Around 2005, three pressures appeared at once. The hyperscale data centers of Google, Facebook, and Microsoft started to reach tens of thousands of servers. Virtualization (VMware ESX in 2001, KVM in 2007) created a new networking entity: the mobile VM. Multi-tenant cloud services (AWS EC2 launched on 2006-08-24, per AWS News) needed to isolate thousands of customers on the same physical infrastructure. Under these three pressures, the closed-box model started to show clear limits. This part analyzes six dimensions of those limits, three technical (Spanning Tree, the 4096 VLAN cap, chassis scale) and three operational (CLI per device, configuration drift, change velocity), to set up the parts that follow which explain how SDN appeared as the answer to each one.

## 1.0.1 The traditional model: one device equals control, data, and management

The "switch as a closed box" architecture was shaped by three generations of dominant equipment from 1984 to 2010. Cisco Systems (founded in 1984 by Leonard Bosack and Sandy Lerner) shipped the Catalyst series with IOS, a monolithic operating system that ran on the device's internal CPU. Juniper Networks (1996, Pradeep Sindhu) built Junos on a FreeBSD base but kept the same vertically integrated mindset. Arista Networks (founded 2004 by Andy Bechtolsheim and Kenneth Duda) shipped EOS, a more open operating system on a Linux kernel, but the control logic still lived inside each box.

Three planes operated in parallel on the same device:

The data plane forwarded packets at line rate (10, 40, or 100 Gbps) using a dedicated ASIC. The ASIC is not programmable at the protocol behavior level. It only looks up forwarding tables (the MAC table for an L2 switch, the FIB for an L3 router) and pushes the packet out the matching port. The lookup table is populated by the control plane.

The control plane ran the routing and switching protocols on the device's CPU. On a Catalyst 3750 switch, that CPU is a PowerPC 405 or an ARM Cortex around 600 MHz with a few hundred megabytes of RAM, per the Cisco datasheet for the Catalyst 3750 Series. The same CPU has to run OSPF for internal routing, BGP for external peering, STP to prevent L2 loops, and LACP for link aggregation. Each device computes the topology on its own and decides paths on its own. It only learns what its neighbors decide through the messages the protocols allow them to exchange.

The management plane is the CLI over a console or SSH plus SNMP for monitoring. The administrator logs into one device at a time. There is no central view.

The direct consequence of this model: **every policy change has to be applied manually on every device**. A new access list rule across 50 access switches means 50 SSH sessions and 50 copy-and-paste actions, and you have to hope you do not make a typo each time.

> **Common misconception:** "A centralized control plane means SDN removes the device's control plane."
>
> **Reality:** SDN moves the policy decision (what is allowed where, with what QoS) out of the device, but it keeps the fast local forwarding mechanism on the ASIC. In OpenFlow, the controller installs flow entries on the switch. The switch matches each packet on its own and forwards it according to the entry without asking the controller for every packet. This is the difference between reactive and proactive flow programming, which we analyze in detail in part 3.1 (the OpenFlow 1.0 specification).

## 1.0.2 Vendor lock-in and the innovation bottleneck

The closed-box model created a chain of economic and technical lock-in between the customer and the vendor. Three layers of lock-in operated at the same time.

The first layer is proprietary silicon. Cisco developed in-house ASICs (Cisco Silicon One since 2019, with UADP for Catalyst before that), and Juniper has Trio (per Juniper documentation, the Trio chipset). A customer who wanted to switch to another vendor had to replace the hardware end to end, a capital cost similar to rebuilding the data center.

The second layer is proprietary software. An IOS configuration does not run on Junos. An automation script written for Arista EOS does not translate to Cisco NX-OS. The operations team has to keep a separate skill set for every vendor, which leads to the "one vendor, one team" pattern often seen in large enterprises.

The third layer, which has the deepest consequence, is product innovation on the vendor's roadmap. When the customer needs a new feature, they must wait for the vendor to put it on the roadmap, typically with an 18 to 24 month cycle from the request to commercial release. Features that break the vendor's revenue model, for example the ability to run on a commodity whitebox without high-end silicon, may never make it onto the roadmap at all.

The Ethane paper (Casado, Freedman, Pettit, Luo, McKeown, Shenker, SIGCOMM 2007, Kyoto, 2007-08-27 to 2007-08-31) records the motivation directly: "business productivity can be severely hampered by network misconfigurations". It cites a Yankee Group report that 62 percent of downtime in multi-vendor networks is caused by human error, and 80 percent of the IT budget goes to maintenance and operations. This is one of the most authoritative records of the hidden cost of the old model. It is also the premise for Ethane to propose a centralized controller, the idea that later became the core of OpenFlow (part 3.1).

The slow device replacement cycle made things worse. A Cisco Catalyst 6500 bought in 2005 was usually amortized over 7 to 10 years. The firmware features the vendor committed to during that period locked the enterprise into that roadmap. When technology moved fast (virtualization in 2001, cloud in 2006, containers in 2013), the physical network infrastructure could not keep pace with the application tier.

## 1.0.3 The east-west traffic explosion (2005 to 2010) in hyperscale data centers

If §1.0.1 and §1.0.2 are about architecture, this section is about traffic. The biggest shift of the 2000s was the reversal of traffic direction: from mostly north-south (Internet client to server) to mostly east-west (server to server inside the data center).

Google estimates that the share of internal traffic grew sharply when search and Gmail moved to the MapReduce and Google File System (GFS) architecture. Per Singh et al., "Jupiter Rising: A Decade of Clos Topologies and Centralized Control in Google's Datacenter Network" (SIGCOMM 2015, pages 183 to 197, DOI 10.1145/2829988.2787508), the Jupiter network reached 1.3 Pbps bisection bandwidth in 2015, 100 times the 2005 Firehose 1.1 generation. That growth did not serve external clients. It served the shuffle between MapReduce tasks inside the same data center.

Facebook published its "fabric" architecture in 2014 (Facebook Engineering, "Introducing data center fabric, the next-generation Facebook data center network", post dated 2014-11-14). The post explains the reason: machine-to-machine traffic (server to server, inside the data center) had grown well past machine-to-user traffic (data center to Internet), with internal traffic estimated to be several times the external traffic. Roy et al., "Inside the Social Network's (Datacenter) Network" (SIGCOMM 2015, DOI 10.1145/2785956.2787472) measured the Facebook network directly and confirmed that most packets go cross-server inside the same cluster.

Three concrete drivers behind the east-west growth:

The first is distributed storage. GFS, HDFS, and Ceph split data into replicas across many nodes. Every write produces 2 or 3 copies across the network. A 1 GB write from a client is actually 3 GB of internal traffic for a 3-way replication.

The second is batch analytics. The MapReduce shuffle moves data between every map task and every reduce task in the cluster, generating in-phase traffic across the entire fabric.

The third is the service mesh. A single user request (for example, opening the Facebook feed) triggers 50 to 100 internal RPC calls between services (user profile, friend graph, feed ranking, ads, photos), all inside the same data center. None of this is visible from the north-south view.

The architectural consequence is that the traditional 3-tier tree (access, distribution, core, per the Cisco Three-Tier Design Guide) cannot keep up. Server-to-server traffic in the same access switch crosses one hop, but server-to-server traffic between two different access switches has to go up to the core and back down, which creates a hairpin and oversubscribes the core. The Clos (leaf-spine) architecture solves this with full bisection bandwidth, and it is the foundation for the VXLAN and overlay work that follows in blocks XI and XII.

## 1.0.4 Three technical limits before SDN: STP, VLAN, chassis scale

### Spanning Tree Protocol: 40 to 50 percent of capacity blocked

Radia Perlman invented the Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) at Digital Equipment Corporation in 1985, and it was standardized as IEEE 802.1D in 1990 (IEEE Standards Association, 802.1D specification). STP solves the L2 loop problem by proactively blocking redundant links and keeping a single path between any two switches. The algorithm picks a root bridge, computes the shortest path to the root, and any link not on that path is set to the Blocking state.

STP is simple and self-healing when the topology changes. Its weakness shows up in the modern data center. With a leaf-spine topology of 4 spines and 16 leaves in full mesh (each leaf connected to all 4 spines), STP blocks 3 of the 4 uplinks on each leaf to avoid a loop. Only 1 of 4 = 25 percent of the uplink capacity is usable; 75 percent of the physical links are wasted. With a fully meshed router topology, the blocked share is typically 40 to 50 percent depending on the structure. That investment is locked in but does not produce real bandwidth.

Rapid STP (IEEE 802.1w, 2001) cut convergence time from 30 to 50 seconds down to 1 to 2 seconds, but it did not solve the blocked-link problem. Multiple Spanning Tree (MST, 802.1s) lets each VLAN have its own tree and improves load distribution, but each tree still blocks links. Both RSTP and MST were folded into IEEE 802.1Q-2014.

The real solution came from outside IEEE. TRILL (RFC 5556, "Transparent Interconnection of Lots of Links", IETF 2009) uses IS-IS routing to forward L2 across multiple parallel paths. ECMP over an L3 overlay (VXLAN, part 11) bypasses STP entirely by replacing L2 flooding with L3 routing under encapsulation. OpenFlow goes further: the controller computes the path for each flow directly and installs it on the switch, with no need for any distributed convergence protocol.

### VLAN 4096: a 12-bit tag hits the cloud wall

IEEE 802.1Q defines a 12-bit VLAN tag, which gives at most 4094 usable VLANs (IDs 0 and 4095 are reserved). RFC 7348 §3.1 (Mahalingam et al., "Virtual eXtensible Local Area Network (VXLAN): A Framework for Overlaying Virtualized Layer 2 Networks over Layer 3 Networks", IETF 2014) records the issue verbatim: "A 12-bit VLAN ID is used in the Ethernet data frames to divide the larger Layer 2 network into multiple broadcast domains. This has served well for many networks, but in larger multi-tenant environments accelerate the need for larger VLAN limits."

The 4094 cap is fine for an enterprise with dozens or hundreds of departments. It breaks in the public cloud: AWS, GCP, and Azure need to isolate networks for hundreds of thousands or millions of tenants on the same physical infrastructure. Each tenant needs at least one private broadcast domain. Some tenants need several broadcast domains for tiers (web, app, database).

VXLAN solves this with a 24-bit VXLAN Network Identifier (VNI), which provides 16,777,216 segments (RFC 7348 §1, confirmed in the plain-text output). Geneve (RFC 8926) extends this further with a variable-length TLV. Encapsulation details are covered in block XI (Overlay Networks).

### Chassis scale: backplane oversubscription

At the individual device level, chassis switches hit a backplane capacity ceiling. A Cisco Catalyst 6513 (13 slots) has a total bus capacity of 720 Gbps per the Cisco Catalyst 6500 datasheet. With 13 line cards, each with 48 ports of 10 GbE, the nominal port bandwidth is 13 times 480 = 6240 Gbps, but the backplane only carries 720 Gbps. The 8.7 to 1 oversubscription ratio means that when every port sends at full rate, 87 percent of the packets have to drop or queue.

For north-south traffic, oversubscription is acceptable because all ports rarely run at full rate at the same time. For east-west traffic in the data center, many parallel flows (MapReduce shuffles, storage replication) can saturate many ports at the same time, and the backplane becomes the bottleneck.

The leaf-spine architecture avoids this problem by removing the large chassis. Replacing one 13-slot chassis with 13 independent 1U switches connected through a full-mesh spine eliminates the single backplane choke point. Oversubscription is spread evenly across the fabric.

## 1.0.5 Three operational limits: per-device configuration, configuration drift, change velocity

The three operational limits below complement the three technical limits in §1.0.4. They are not problems of capacity or speed. They are problems of human cost and time.

### Per-device CLI configuration through screen scraping

Before 2012, network configuration automation depended mostly on `expect` scripts and screen scraping: typing CLI commands over SSH and then parsing the text response. Tools such as Ansible (released on 2012-02-20, per the Ansible GitHub releases) and Puppet (2005, Luke Kanies) have modules for network devices, but inside those modules the work is still an `expect` chain that sends IOS or Junos commands. There was no structured API exposed by the device for Ansible to call.

The consequence is that pushing a policy change to 100 switches takes time linear in the number of devices, plus the risk of a race condition when 10 of the 100 fail halfway through. NETCONF (RFC 6241, 2011) and YANG (RFC 6020, 2010) provided a standard data model, but adoption in enterprises in 2015 was below 20 percent, per Gartner reports from that time. It took the rise of SDN and cloud networking (OpenStack Neutron in 2012) for the network API to become widely adopted.

### Configuration drift

When the configuration of 100 switches is applied manually or through `expect` scripts, even a few unrecorded emergency hotfixes cause the actual state on the devices to drift away from the planned state in the documentation. This is configuration drift.

Drift is not dangerous when it is small and isolated. It is dangerous when an incident hits after months of accumulated drift: the engineer does not know which firewall rule is correct and which is legacy waiting to be cleaned up. The Mean Time To Resolution (MTTR) for incidents of this kind often runs from many hours to many days.

A centralized source of truth, where the SDN controller holds the authoritative copy of the policy and pushes it down to the devices, removes drift completely because the devices do not hold their own state to drift away from. This is one of the clearest operational reasons to move to SDN.

### Change velocity

Change velocity is the speed at which the operations team can deploy network changes safely. Before 2010 the typical cycle in a large enterprise was:

Change request, then review by the Change Advisory Board, then maintenance window (usually outside business hours, on the weekend), then rollout in batches of 5 to 10 switches, then verify, then roll back if something fails.

This cycle usually took 2 to 3 weeks for an average change, based on the experience of many enterprise network operations teams recorded in public post-mortems (for example, the Etsy Network Engineering Blog, 2015). Meanwhile, AWS EC2 (launched on 2006-08-24, per the AWS News page "Announcing Amazon Elastic Compute Cloud, Beta") set a new expectation: a new VM in a few minutes. The gap between "deploy a server in minutes" and "change the network in weeks" became a barrier to every application automation initiative.

SDN promises to close that gap by letting the application call an API to request a network, provision a new tenant, create a segment for a batch job, and tear it all down when the job is done. SDN northbound APIs such as the OVN NBDB (part 13) and NETCONF and YANG (part 5.0) later formalized this model into first-class primitives.

### ▶ Guided Exercise 1: Count the CLI commands needed to create VLAN 100 on a 20-switch topology

**Context:** Suppose you administer a data center with 20 Cisco Catalyst 9300 switches running IOS-XE. The development team requests VLAN 100 with the name `APP-TIER` on all 20 switches. Each switch needs an uplink trunk that carries VLAN 100. On the 2 core switches, you also need a Switched Virtual Interface (SVI) `Vlan 100` with the IP gateway addresses `10.100.0.1/24` and `10.100.0.2/24` set up as active and standby for HSRP.

**Step 1, Predict:** Before you do the math, write down on paper the total number of CLI commands you guess you will need across the 20 switches.

**Step 2, Observe (the actual count):**

On each access switch (18 of them), the minimum is 4 commands:
```
configure terminal
vlan 100
 name APP-TIER
 exit
```
Plus the trunk uplink commands: `interface GigabitEthernet1/0/48` plus `switchport trunk allowed vlan add 100` plus `exit` = 3 commands. Total per access switch: 7 commands. 18 switches times 7 = **126 commands**.

On each core switch (2 of them), add the SVI and HSRP block:
```
interface Vlan 100
 ip address 10.100.0.1 255.255.255.0   (or .2 for core 2)
 standby 100 ip 10.100.0.254
 standby 100 priority 110              (110 for active, 100 for standby)
 standby 100 preempt
 no shutdown
 exit
```
One core switch: 4 (VLAN) plus 3 (trunk) plus 7 (SVI plus HSRP) = 14 commands. Two cores times 14 = **28 commands**.

Add commit and save: `copy running-config startup-config` times 20 = **20 commands**.

**Total: 126 plus 28 plus 20 = 174 commands** typed manually over SSH or console across 20 separate sessions.

**Step 3, Explain:** 174 is only the minimum. In practice, every SSH session also needs login (username and password or TACACS+), `enable` to enter privileged mode, and `show running-config` to verify before and after the change, which raises the total to 250 to 300 actions. At a pace of one switch every 3 to 5 minutes when everything goes well, the entire change takes 60 to 100 minutes, and that is before the review and rollback plan. The number does not scale at all when the topology grows to 200 switches or when the policy gets more complex (QoS, ACL, routing).

Compare your initial guess to the actual number. Students with operations experience usually guess 40 to 80 commands (50 to 60 percent short). Students with no production experience usually guess 10 to 20 (almost 90 percent short). The gap reflects the real reason cloud operators moved to OpenFlow and SDN: not because traffic grew, but because operational cost grew linearly with the number of devices and that no longer scaled at hyperscale.

A quick comparison with SDN: an OpenFlow controller receives a single API call `POST /networks/{id}/vlans` with the payload `{"vlan_id": 100, "name": "APP-TIER", "scope": "all-switches"}`. One API call, and the controller pushes the necessary flow entries to every switch under management. The contrast is 1 versus 174, and that is before you account for drift and rollback.
