## Learning objectives

After finishing this part, you can:

1. **Describe (Understand)** ForCES as the IETF standardization effort from 2001 to 2010 that splits a monolithic router into a Control Element and a Forwarding Element communicating through the standard protocol RFC 5810.
2. **Distinguish (Analyze)** the intra-box scope of ForCES from the inter-box scope of OpenFlow. Both respect the same control-data separation principle but solve different problems.
3. **Explain (Understand)** the 4D Project as the 2005 academic breakthrough that proposed the clearest network-wide control from a centralized decision plane and is the direct ancestor of Ethane and OpenFlow.

## Context: two different efforts with the same goal

After the ATM era ended (Parts 2.0 and 2.1) and the enterprise converged on the modular plug-in (Part 2.2), the mid 2000s saw two parallel research and standardization efforts trying to restructure network control. Both arrived at the same conclusion in principle (separate control from data), but their implementation and outcomes differed greatly.

**ForCES** (Forwarding and Control Element Separation) is an IETF effort chartered in 2001. The aim was to standardize the internal split between the control part and the forwarding part of a router. Commercial goal: allow one vendor to build a Control Element and another vendor to build a Forwarding Element, with both connected through a standard protocol. Internal scope: within a single physical device (it could be a large chassis router with multiple cards, or a small stack router).

**The 4D Project** is an academic effort by Princeton and CMU, published mainly in 2005 as an ACM SIGCOMM CCR paper. 4D did not try to standardize directly. It proposed a **new design blueprint** for network control at the inter-box, inter-AS scale. Network-wide scope: the entire enterprise or provider network, not a single device.

The two efforts differ in scope and method but complement each other. ForCES provides a standard for anyone who needs to implement a protocol between CE and FE. 4D provides the philosophy for anyone who needs to argue why the split matters. When OpenFlow arrived in 2009, it did not use the ForCES protocol (although it could have), but it used the 4D thinking almost intact. The ForCES legacy faded; the 4D legacy is very strong.

## 2.3.1 ForCES: the IETF standardization effort 2003-2010

### Chartering and goals

IETF chartered the ForCES Working Group at the end of 2001 under the Routing Area. The motivation came from an observation: enterprise and service-provider routers in the late 1990s were getting larger (a chassis with 16 or more line cards and hundreds of ports), with internal control CPUs running routing protocols and forwarding ASICs running packet processing. The two components share a chassis but are in fact two independent computers communicating across a backplane, with proprietary protocols per vendor.

If the protocol between Control Element (CE) and Forwarding Element (FE) could be standardized, the commercial benefits would include:

- A new vendor could build an FE (competing with Cisco or Juniper ASICs) without having to write its own routing-protocol stack.
- A niche vendor could build a CE with unique features (for example, a routing protocol for a niche use case) and reuse a commercial off-the-shelf FE.
- Enterprises could mix and match, choosing CE from vendor A with FE from vendor B.

The WG worked on three main deliverables over almost ten years:

**RFC 3654, Requirements for Separation of IP Control and Forwarding** (Hormuzd Khosravi, Todd Anderson, November 2003). This document defined the requirements: actual bandwidth between CE and FE must be high enough (expected thousands of routing updates per second), latency must be low (so as not to slow route convergence), and reliability must be higher than wire-level routing protocols (because the FE depends on the CE for forwarding decisions).

**RFC 3746, Forwarding and Control Element Separation (ForCES) Framework** (Lily Yang, Ram Dantu, T. Anderson, Ram Gopal, April 2004). This is the architecture document. It defines CE, FE, CE Manager, and FE Manager as four separate roles, communicating through the ForCES Protocol (not yet detailed at this point; that came in a later RFC).

**RFC 5810, Forwarding and Control Element Separation (ForCES) Protocol Specification** (Avri Doria, Jamal Hadi Salim, Robert Haas, Hormuzd Khosravi, Weiming Wang, Ligang Dong, Ram Gopal, Joel Halpern, March 2010). The concrete protocol document. By this point, nine years separated the charter from the final RFC, a sign of slow WG operations and internal conflict of interest.

### ForCES CE/FE versus OpenFlow controller/switch

In the abstract, ForCES CE/FE and OpenFlow controller/switch look very similar: one side computes policy and decides, the other side enforces. But on closer inspection, three important differences emerge:

**Physical scope.** ForCES CE/FE live in the same physical box and communicate through an internal transport (could be Ethernet inside the chassis, or PCIe, or shared memory). The OpenFlow controller is fully separate from the box and communicates through TCP or TLS over an IP network. With ForCES, the CE-to-FE round trip is microseconds. With OpenFlow, the controller-to-switch round trip can be milliseconds for WAN, hundreds of microseconds for LAN.

**Trust model.** In ForCES, CE and FE share the same owner; trust is implicit. The protocol's authentication relies on physical adjacency (PCIe cannot be MITM-attacked from outside). In OpenFlow, controller and switch may have different owners (for example, an MSP managing a controller for a customer); the protocol uses TLS plus certificate-based authentication.

**State granularity.** A ForCES FE has a structured data model through Logical Function Blocks (LFBs), each of which represents a processing function (classifier, meter, scheduler, shaper). The CE sends commands that change LFB state. This semantics is much more complex than an OpenFlow flow entry, but it is also more rigid (LFBs are pre-defined in an XML schema).

The Logical Function Block model is a unique contribution of ForCES. Each LFB is described by an XML schema (a precursor to YANG), with clear attributes and events. The CE can discover LFBs in the FE through a query and understand the function of each LFB. This is the precursor to the NETCONF and YANG data model, and in the abstract, the LFB model is more solid than the OpenFlow 1.0 flat flow table. However, this complexity is part of why ForCES is hard to adopt: it requires a runtime XML parser and schema validator, all heavier than the simple binary OpenFlow protocol.

## 2.3.2 Control Element versus Forwarding Element: three components plus CE/FE Manager

The ForCES Framework (RFC 3746) details four roles in a ForCES deployment:

**Control Element (CE).** Runs routing protocols (OSPF, BGP, IS-IS), signaling (LDP, RSVP), and the management plane (SNMP, NetFlow collection). The CE computes the route table, ACL policy, and QoS classes, and pushes them down to the FE.

**Forwarding Element (FE).** Runs packet processing. It includes the ingress classifier, lookup engine, transformations (TTL decrement, IP header modification), egress scheduler, and shaper. The FE does not run routing protocols; it only knows how to execute commands from the CE.

**CE Manager.** A meta-component that manages the CE topology. If the deployment has redundant CEs (active plus backup), the CE Manager owns the failover logic. One CE Manager can manage many CEs.

**FE Manager.** Similarly, manages the FE topology. In a large chassis router, there are many FEs (each line card is an FE), and the FE Manager keeps state in sync among them when the CE updates.

This two-manager model is significantly more complex than OpenFlow. OpenFlow has only controller and switch, no meta layer. The simplicity helped OpenFlow be adopted quickly. The complexity slowed ForCES down.

In thinking-pattern terms, the ForCES CE is a precursor to the OpenFlow controller, and the ForCES FE is a precursor to the OpenFlow switch. However, the implementation lineage is not direct: no OpenFlow implementation inherits the ForCES codebase. The teams that built OpenFlow (NOX 2008, Stanford Open vSwitch) read the ForCES documents but chose to build from scratch with a simpler protocol.

## 2.3.3 Why ForCES did not gain commercial acceptance

ForCES did not disappear immediately; the WG was officially closed in 2015 after 14 years of activity. But commercially, adoption was almost nonexistent. No router vendor shipped a ForCES-compliant CE-FE interface to the market. No open-source reference implementation reached production quality. Three main reasons, drawing on William Stallings's survey in *Foundations of Modern Networking* (Pearson 2016) and the analysis by Thomas Nadeau and Ken Gray in *SDN: Software Defined Networks* (O'Reilly 2013):

### Reason 1: solving the wrong-scope problem

ForCES standardizes the internal interface of a router. But **vendor lock-in in networking is mostly not at the internal-router level**. It is at the network-wide level (you cannot migrate switch vendor across the entire network, you cannot interoperate the enterprise control plane with the service-provider control plane). Vendor Cisco does not care whether their FE runs with a Juniper CE; they care whether the customer continues to buy Cisco hardware instead of Juniper. ForCES solved a problem that the industry did not prioritize.

If ForCES had succeeded, it might have opened the door for FE silicon startups to compete with Cisco ASICs. But this market was too narrow and the capex too high to be attractive to VCs. There was no economic incentive driving adoption.

### Reason 2: lack of an open-source reference implementation

OpenFlow had a major advantage in that the NOX controller (Nicira 2008) and Open vSwitch (Nicira 2009) were both open-source and production-quality from an early stage. Researchers and startups used them for free, contributed fixes, and created a mass ecosystem.

ForCES had no equivalent. A few academic prototypes (Linux kernel modules in ForCES papers, MeshFlow from Chinese universities) existed, but none were maintained to production. When a protocol has no quality reference implementation, no matter how good the specification is, adoption is hard. Operators cannot easily "install ForCES in a lab to try".

### Reason 3: lack of disruptive vision in marketing

ForCES was marketed as "coexist with the current control plane, add flexibility". There was no radical change manifesto. The industry and academia were looking for something new, and "add flexibility to a Cisco router" was not exciting enough.

OpenFlow succeeded largely because of its disruptive marketing: "separate everything, rebuild from scratch, dream up new network architectures". For hyperscaler operators wanting to build a custom data center, this manifesto resonated. For enterprise operators wanting to keep using Cisco, ForCES sounded more appealing in theory but ultimately no one bought it.

**Historical lesson for modern SDN:** standardization is not strong enough on its own. It needs (a) a high-quality open-source ecosystem, (b) a clear architectural-change manifesto, and (c) a problem statement aligned with industry priorities. OpenFlow had all three. ForCES had none.

## 2.3.4 The 4D Project: Rexford, Greenberg, Maltz, and colleagues

While ForCES moved slowly at IETF, a small group of researchers from Princeton and Carnegie Mellon published an 11-page paper in ACM SIGCOMM CCR in October 2005 with much greater impact than the paper's size implied. The paper is "A Clean Slate 4D Approach to Network Control and Management" by Albert Greenberg (AT&T Research), Gísli Hjálmtýsson (Reykjavík University, formerly AT&T), David A. Maltz (Carnegie Mellon), Andy Myers (CMU), Jennifer Rexford (Princeton), Geoffrey Xie (Naval Postgraduate School), Hong Yan (CMU), Jibin Zhan (CMU), and Hui Zhang (CMU).

### The "clean slate" manifesto

The paper opens with a manifesto bold for 2005: *"The current Internet architecture couples the dissemination of state with path selection and traffic forwarding. This coupling leads to complex interactions between routing protocols, difficulty in enforcing policies, and challenges in network management. We propose a clean slate approach..."*

This manifesto matters for three reasons:

First, the group stated clearly that **the management plane and the control plane have been mixed together** for the entire 30-year history of the Internet. Operators configure BGP policy, ACLs, and QoS classes manually per device; routers run OSPF and BGP distributively. There is no clear boundary between "the policy the operator wants" and "the mechanism that the protocol enforces".

Second, **distributed control protocols have limits in complexity and correctness**. OSPF and BGP have hit a complexity ceiling: operators understand only part of them, tracing tools are hard, and misconfiguration accounts for the majority of network outages (Kempf et al. "Analysis of a Large Scale Configuration Database" SIGCOMM 2006 estimates 60 to 80 percent of outages from misconfig).

Third, **a clean-slate approach is feasible** if you accept a paradigm shift. The group proposed: do not improve existing distributed protocols; redesign from scratch with centralized decisions. The risk is large, but the reward is also large.

These three points are the foundational ideas for every later SDN approach. OpenFlow comes directly from 4D; ONOS comes from 4D; OVN follows the same philosophy of "centralized configuration intent, distributed enforcement".

### The influence of AT&T Research and Princeton

The 4D group are not random researchers. Albert Greenberg and Jennifer Rexford are two of the leading names in 2000s networking research, with many SIGCOMM and INFOCOM papers and direct industry impact. Greenberg worked at AT&T Research (where the privilege of access to production-backbone data lets you evaluate ideas). Rexford worked at AT&T before moving to Princeton in 2005. Both have credibility for the "clean slate" manifesto to be heard by both academia and industry.

David Maltz (CMU) and Hui Zhang (CMU) are also senior researchers with long-standing work. Maltz later moved to Microsoft, contributing to VL2 (Microsoft's DC network architecture, SIGCOMM 2009). Rexford and Maltz also advised Google during 2008-2012 when Google designed Jupiter.

The presence of these top figures turned the 4D paper from "a typical CCR paper" into "a manifesto noticed by the community". The paper was cited widely in academia and read by industry key people. Martin Casado (Stanford, later Nicira) has acknowledged in retrospectives many times that Ethane was directly influenced by 4D.

## 2.3.5 The four planes: Decision, Dissemination, Discovery, Data

The core architectural contribution of the 4D Project is dividing network functionality into four clear planes, each named with the letter D:

### D1: Decision Plane, logically centralized

The decision plane consists of centralized entities (which can be physically distributed but are logically centralized) responsible for computing policy for the entire network. The decision plane receives:

- **Network-wide goals** from the operator (for example, "minimize latency for tenant X", "ensure tenant Y has 100 Mbps QoS").
- **Network state** from the Discovery plane (topology, link capacity, current flows).

The decision plane outputs **forwarding state** specific to each device: flow-table entries, ACL rules, QoS-class assignments. The decision logic can be a global optimization (such as an ILP solver), a heuristic (shortest path with custom weights), or learned (a machine-learning model).

The spirit of the Decision plane is: **the operator says "what" at the high level, and the Decision plane handles "how" at the low level**. This is declarative-to-imperative translation, similar to a SQL query planner or a Kubernetes scheduler.

### D2: Dissemination Plane, a reliable channel that pushes state down to devices

The Dissemination plane is the transport channel between the Decision plane and the devices (switches, routers). Requirements: reliable, ordered, low latency, and secure. 4D does not specify a concrete implementation; it could be TCP or TLS, SCTP, or a custom protocol.

This differs from a traditional distributed protocol (OSPF LSA flooding, BGP update). Dissemination is point-to-point from the Decision plane to the device; no convergence logic is needed. The device only receives state; it does not discover neighbors or compute on its own.

The Dissemination plane is the precursor to the OpenFlow channel that came later. The OpenFlow specification §6.1 (1.0.0) describes a secure channel with properties (reliable, ordered, TLS-optional) that nearly match the 4D Dissemination requirements.

### D3: Discovery Plane, collects topology and state from devices

The Discovery plane runs in the opposite direction. Devices send state (link up or down, traffic counters, error rates) up to the Decision plane. The Decision plane uses this state as input for policy computation.

Discovery has two modes: **event-driven** (the device sends a notification when state changes, for example, link flap) and **polling** (the Decision plane queries periodically, for example, every 5 seconds). Both modes are usable, and real deployments mix the two as needed.

In OpenFlow, the Discovery plane is implemented through `PORT_STATUS` async messages (event-driven link status) and `FLOW_STATS` Request/Reply (polling statistics).

### D4: Data Plane, pure forwarding

The Data plane only forwards packets according to the state that the Decision plane has pushed down. It does not decide paths on its own, does not run routing protocols, and does not cache topology information on its own.

This is the largest simplification compared with legacy networks, where every router runs OSPF or BGP and decides on its own. 4D pushes all complexity to the Decision plane and keeps the data plane minimal. ASIC vendors are happy because forwarding logic is simple. Operators are happy because devices are more predictable.

### The key principle

The 4D paper highlights one principle: **the Decision plane has a network-wide view; the other planes do not**. The Data plane only knows local state (which port is up, which flow entries exist). The Discovery plane only knows the state it collects. The Dissemination plane is just transport.

Only the Decision plane has visibility across the entire network and is therefore the only place that can compute globally optimal decisions. This is the difference-maker compared with the distributed approach.

Forward reference to the SDN controller (Part 3.1 OpenFlow 1.0, Part 4.x OpenFlow evolution): all modern SDN controllers inherit the 4D model directly. OpenFlow controller equals Decision plane. OpenFlow channel equals Dissemination plane. OpenFlow async message equals Discovery plane. OpenFlow switch equals Data plane.

## 2.3.6 4D legacy: a strict separation between the control plane and the management plane

After 2005, the 4D paper did not directly produce a commercial product (the 4D group focused on academic publication, not on starting a company). But the 4D thinking spread through three clear lineage channels:

**Lineage 1: 4D to Ethane to OpenFlow.** Martin Casado (Stanford, Nick McKeown's PhD student) read the 4D paper in 2005 and pursued the idea in Ethane (SIGCOMM 2007). Ethane is in essence an implementation of the Decision plane plus Dissemination plane plus Discovery plane for enterprise Ethernet. OpenFlow 1.0 (2009) further generalized Ethane into an open protocol. Part 2.4 will analyze Ethane in detail and show the direct lineage.

**Lineage 2: 4D to NOX to Nicira NVP.** The NOX controller (2008, Nicira researchers, Part 7.0) was the first implementation of a "network operating system" based on 4D principles. NOX later evolved into Onix (SIGCOMM 2010) and Nicira NVP (the 2011 commercial product, becoming VMware NSX after the 2012 acquisition).

**Lineage 3: 4D to SDN textbooks to general SDN awareness.** The textbooks by Göransson, Black, and Culver, by Stallings, and by Nadeau and Gray all dedicate many pages to 4D as an example of clean-slate thinking. The generation of networking engineers who learned SDN from 2013 to 2020 mostly encountered the 4D paper in their first week of study.

**Why 4D succeeded in influence while ForCES failed**: three main reasons.

**Reason 1: clear narrative.** 4D had the "clean slate" manifesto and four memorable D letters, creating a strong mnemonic. The ForCES specification is hundreds of pages of RFC, with no manifesto attracting academic or media attention.

**Reason 2: short, readable paper.** The 4D paper is only 11 pages and reads in 30 to 40 minutes. The ForCES requirements plus framework plus protocol total several hundred pages, which no one reads in full except WG members.

**Reason 3: top authors from academia and industry research.** Greenberg and Rexford are leading researchers whose thesis students continued working in the same direction. The ForCES WG had many vendor consultants but no big-name academic champion.

The final lesson: in tech innovation, **idea distribution matters as much as the technology**. 4D and ForCES are both good ideas, from the same period. The difference is that 4D was distributed well (narrative, brevity, prestigious authors), and ForCES was distributed poorly (rigid WG process, verbose documentation, no champion author). When designing a new SDN feature today, you must think about both technical content and distribution strategy.

> **Key point:** 4D is not a protocol; it is a design philosophy. OpenFlow and OVN do not "implement 4D" in the programming sense; they "inherit 4D" in the thinking sense. This is the reason learning SDN requires reading the 4D paper: not to write code, but to understand why SDN design decisions look the way they do today.
