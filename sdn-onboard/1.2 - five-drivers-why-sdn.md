## Context

Part 1.0 answered the question "what was wrong with the traditional network?" at the architectural level. Part 1.1 went deep into four concrete pain points at the data center layer. This part takes a step back and asks "why were those pain points big enough to force the networking industry to redesign itself?". It summarizes the answer into five high-weight drivers that the academic and industrial communities later agreed are the main ones.

The five-driver classification comes from the classic textbook by Paul Göransson, Chuck Black, and Timothy Culver, *Software Defined Networks: A Comprehensive Approach* (2nd edition, Morgan Kaufmann 2017), chapter 2.5. Each driver has its own conditions for triggering the move to SDN. No single driver alone is enough to cause the shift. It is the resonance of all five together. Understanding this relationship helps you avoid the common mistake of answering "what does SDN solve?" with a single one-line answer. SDN is a multi-dimensional answer to a multi-dimensional question.

---

## 1.2.1 Driver 1: data center bandwidth growth from video, big data, and microservices

### Three workload waves between 2005 and 2015

Data center bandwidth demand does not grow linearly. It grows in steps, with each step driven by a new workload wave. From 2005 to 2015, three waves applied pressure in sequence:

**Wave 1: video streaming (2005 to 2010).** YouTube launched on 2005-02-14 (YouTube company history, acquired by Google in 2006-10), and Netflix started its streaming service in 2007-01 after the DVD-by-mail era (Netflix corporate timeline). Video is a special workload: each user session takes 1 to 5 Mbps sustained for tens of minutes to hours, very different from the earlier web browsing pattern (small bursts, long idle gaps). By 2010 video already accounted for about 50 percent of global Internet bandwidth, per the Cisco Visual Networking Index Report 2010. Bandwidth grew not only at the data center egress to the Internet but also internally: CDN edge nodes cached video and synchronized with the origin, generating data center to data center traffic at scale.

**Wave 2: big data analytics (2006 to 2012).** Hadoop release 0.1.0 (2006-04, Doug Cutting and Mike Cafarella at Yahoo!), Apache Hadoop 1.0 (2011-12). The MapReduce shuffle between map tasks and reduce tasks creates all-to-all traffic inside the data center, with peaks reaching 90 percent of the link bandwidth for the few minutes of the shuffle stage. The Facebook Hadoop cluster in 2012 already held 100 PB of data and was growing at 500 TB per week (Jeff Rothschild, "Scaling Facebook's Data Warehouse", HPTS 2013 talk). Each byte written into the cluster generated 2 to 3 bytes of internal traffic because of HDFS 3-way replication.

**Wave 3: microservices and containers (2013 to 2018).** Docker 0.1 was released on 2013-03-13 (Docker blog, initial release), and Kubernetes 1.0 on 2015-07-21 (Kubernetes release notes). Microservice architectures break a monolithic application into tens or hundreds of services that run independently and communicate over RPC or HTTP within the same cluster. A single user request opening the Facebook feed triggers 50 to 100 RPC calls between internal services (Roy et al., SIGCOMM 2015). All this traffic stays inside the data center and does not go out to the Internet, multiplied by billions of requests per day.

### The traditional 3-tier architecture cannot keep up

The traditional Cisco Three-Tier Design (access, distribution, core) was sized for north-south, client-server traffic, not for east-west, server-to-server traffic. When two servers in the same rack talk to each other, traffic enters the access switch and leaves through another port on the same switch without going up. When two servers in different racks talk to each other, traffic has to climb to the distribution layer and come back down to another access switch, creating a hairpin hop.

With a 3-tier topology that has 4:1 oversubscription at the distribution and 2:1 at the core (typical 2010 enterprise ratios), east-west traffic is heavily throttled. Google estimates in the Jupiter SIGCOMM 2015 paper that the traditional 3-tier network can only meet about 10 percent of the real east-west bandwidth need of a hyperscale data center. The solution is the Clos leaf-spine architecture with full bisection, but that architecture has too many ECMP paths and too much per-switch routing configuration to manage by hand. This leads directly to the need for SDN centralized control.

### Driver 1 in the 2026 reality

Bandwidth demand is still growing, but it has shifted shape. AI training (2020 onwards) is the new bottleneck. A GPU cluster training an LLM consumes all-reduce bandwidth between GPUs that can reach terabits per second per node, far past CPU workloads. NVIDIA InfiniBand and other dedicated fabrics have appeared for the AI tier. For CPU workloads, bandwidth is now sufficient at most cloud pods today, and driver 1 is no longer the top bottleneck it was in 2010.

## 1.2.2 Driver 2: agility, from weeks to minutes

### A new benchmark set by the cloud

AWS EC2 launched in beta on 2006-08-24 (per the AWS News announcement) and set a new benchmark for the entire industry: "provision a VM in a few minutes through one API call". Before 2006, getting a new server in a traditional data center followed a standard process:

1. Quarterly capacity planning for server purchases (6 to 12 weeks lead time).
2. Server arrives at the data center, rack-and-stack by the data center team (1 to 2 days).
3. Network team configures the VLAN, port access, subnet, and firewall rule (3 to 7 days, possibly with a maintenance window).
4. Storage team provisions a LUN and zones the SAN (2 to 3 days).
5. OS install plus software stack plus handover to the application team (1 to 2 days).

The total time from "I need a server" to "the server is ready to deploy the app" was typically 2 to 4 weeks in the enterprise, and 6 to 8 weeks in government or finance with strict change management. AWS EC2 cut the entire process to 2 to 5 minutes for the VM, and 15 to 30 minutes for a full VPC plus EC2 plus EBS setup.

### The gap between compute and the network

Compute scaled rapidly thanks to virtualization (VMware ESX in 2001, KVM in 2007) reaching production at hyperscale. The network could not keep up because it still depended on manual configuration on physical switches. An application that needed a new VLAN or a new firewall rule still had to wait for the network team, which pushed the deployment cycle back to 1 to 2 weeks instead of the hours that compute allowed.

This gap created the "shadow IT" phenomenon: development teams bypassed the network team and bought cloud services directly to get agility. In 2012 Gartner estimated that 30 to 40 percent of IT spending in large enterprises was shadow IT, mostly cloud consumption that did not go through the CIO. The network team was caught in the middle: they knew the problem and the solution (automation), but they did not have a standardized tool.

### SDN provides the tool

SDN brings two enablers that let the network catch up with agility:

**The northbound API**: replace the CLI with a REST or gRPC API with a clear schema, so that the application can provision the network on its own without going through the network team. The OVN NBDB (part 13) exposes a schema for logical switches, logical routers, ACLs, and load balancers. The OpenFlow controller exposes an API for flow entries. NETCONF and YANG (part 5.0) provides a vendor-neutral version for devices.

**A declarative intent model**: instead of describing every step (add VLAN 100 to switch X, configure the SVI on core Y, add HSRP Z), the application only describes the end state ("network tier APP-TIER needs subnet 10.100.0.0/24 with an HA gateway"). The controller computes the concrete steps. This is the spirit of Intent-Based Networking. Part 6 (revision 3) does not have a separate chapter on IBN, but the declarative spirit is preserved in the OVN NBDB and in Kubernetes NetworkPolicy.

With these two enablers, network agility approaches compute agility. A Terraform manifest that declares a VPC plus subnet plus security group on AWS triggers an API call to the VPC service and creates the full topology in 30 seconds, with no SSH session at all.

> **Key topic:** Agility is not an extra feature. It is a model change. When the network can be requested through an API just like compute, the entire CI/CD pipeline can spin up and tear down environments on its own, opening the door to DevOps and GitOps practice at scale. SDN does not create DevOps, but SDN is a necessary condition for DevOps to apply down to the network layer.

## 1.2.3 Driver 3: policy-driven operations replacing CLI-driven operations

### Separating intent from mechanism

Traditional network configuration forces the operator to think at the mechanism level: "type `switchport access vlan 100` on port GigabitEthernet1/0/24 of switch X". The intent behind that action might be "the host on this port belongs to tenant A" or "this host is allowed to access application Y". The intent is buried in the mechanism and no one can read it back from the configuration.

When the environment changes, for example tenant A is renamed to tenant B, or application Y moves to a new host, the operator has to revisit the mechanism and update each place. There is no formal mapping between business-level intent and device-level mechanism, so updates are easily missed and easily wrong.

Three concrete consequences:

**Documentation drift**: the design documentation records the intent ("VLAN 100 = APP-TIER for project X"), and the device configuration records the mechanism. When another operator changes the mechanism without updating the documentation, the two versions drift apart. After 2 to 3 years, the documentation becomes untrustworthy and operators only trust the running config.

**Security policy uncertainty**: ACLs on the firewall are written by IP subnet, but how is the subnet mapped to the tenant? Many enterprises maintain a "tenant mapping spreadsheet" by hand, and it does not stay in sync with the firewall config. A compliance audit (PCI-DSS, SOC 2) often finds a mismatch between the policy intent and the actual firewall rule, costing many weeks of investigation.

**Troubleshooting overhead**: when a packet is dropped, the engineer has to trace from the packet (mechanism level: source IP, destination IP, port) up to the intent (business level: which user, which request, which policy) through several layers of interpretation. OVN provides `ovn-trace` (part 13) which shows the packet's path through the logical pipeline and brings the intent closer to the packet.

### A northbound API with a declarative model

The SDN controller exposes an API that lets the operator declare intent:

```
POST /v1/logical-networks
{
  "name": "app-tier-prod",
  "owner": "team-backend",
  "subnet": "10.100.0.0/24",
  "security_group": "allow-db-access",
  "compliance_tag": "pci-dss-restricted"
}
```

The controller translates the intent into a mechanism: it creates a `Logical_Switch` in the OVN NBDB, creates ACLs whose match expressions correspond to the security group, and tags them with compliance metadata. The mechanism is now a deterministic derivation of the intent and is no longer an independent source of truth. When the intent changes, the controller re-derives the mechanism on its own, without depending on the operator remembering to update it.

Cisco DNA Center and Juniper Apstra (part 7.3) commercialized this idea under the name Intent-Based Networking. OVN takes a simpler approach and exposes the NBDB as the official intent store. Both approaches respect the same principle: policy must live separately from device configuration, and the controller holds the mapping.

## 1.2.4 Driver 4: multi-tenancy isolation for the public cloud

### Three dimensions of isolation

Multi-tenancy is not just "every tenant has its own ID". The public cloud must provide three dimensions of isolation at the same time:

**Network isolation**: tenant A and tenant B cannot see each other's packets. Even when two VMs run on the same physical hypervisor, the traffic must be logically isolated as if they were in two different data centers.

**Address space overlap**: tenant A may use the subnet 10.0.0.0/16 for their VPC, and tenant B may also use 10.0.0.0/16 without a conflict. This requires either encapsulation or L3 NAT at the tenant boundary so that the tenant address space is separated from the underlay address space.

**Policy independence**: tenant A's security group must not affect tenant B. If tenant A opens port 22 for `0.0.0.0/0`, only tenant A's VMs are affected, with no spillover to other tenants.

These three dimensions are necessary for a commercial public cloud. Without dimension 2 (address overlap), the cloud cannot let the tenant choose their own subnet, and the experience drops sharply. Without dimension 3 (policy independence), the tenant cannot trust the cloud because a misconfiguration by another tenant could affect them.

### Why VLAN is not enough

Part 1.1 §1.1.2 already pointed out the 4094 VLAN limit for the public cloud. Even if scale were not a concern, VLAN does not provide all three dimensions:

- **Address overlap through VLAN: not directly.** A single L2 VLAN domain does not allow two hosts with the same IP. To get overlap, you need NAT or routing, which adds complexity.
- **Policy independence through VLAN: only partial.** ACLs on the switch can split policies by VLAN, but they scale poorly with millions of rules per tenant.

VXLAN solves the three dimensions at once: a 24-bit VNI for scale, encapsulation for address overlap (the inner header belongs to the tenant, the outer header belongs to the underlay independently), and an SDN controller that pushes per-tenant policy without interfering with other tenants.

### The OVN logical model embodies this driver

OVN (part 13) organizes multi-tenancy around the concept of `Logical_Switch`: each tenant has one or more logical switches, and each logical switch is an independent broadcast domain (logically, not requiring its own physical VLAN). Logical switches are connected by `Logical_Router`s to route between subnets of the same tenant. ACLs are attached to logical switches or port groups inside that tenant and do not affect other tenants.

When a packet leaves a VM in tenant A and goes through a Geneve tunnel to another chassis, the option field of the Geneve header carries the logical port ID of the destination VM. The receiving `ovn-controller` uses the option field to know which tenant the packet belongs to and which pipeline must process it. This is the byte-level mechanism that realizes driver 4.

> **Key topic:** Multi-tenancy is the driver that absolutely cannot be broken in an SDN design. An SDN controller for shared infrastructure that does not guarantee strict isolation is commercially worthless. Every design decision in OVN (per-tenant NBDB partitioning, Geneve options for logical context, per-tenant ACL evaluation) must be verified through the lens of "can a different tenant see or affect anything?".

## 1.2.5 Driver 5: cost reduction through commodity hardware and whitebox

### The disaggregation philosophy

Before 2011 a physical switch was a tightly integrated box: vendor-specific silicon, proprietary ASIC firmware, and a proprietary NOS. All from one vendor, on one roadmap, at a premium price. The disaggregation movement started with the Facebook Open Compute Project (OCP), launched in 2011-04 with the server effort and extended to networking in 2013 with the OCP Networking subproject. The goal was to separate hardware from software so that they could be mixed and matched.

Three layers of disaggregation:

**Silicon commoditization**: merchant silicon such as Broadcom Trident, Tomahawk, Cisco Silicon One, and Intel Tofino lets many OEMs and ODMs build switches with similar capabilities and compete on price. ODMs in Guangdong and Taiwan (Celestica, Edgecore, Delta) build whitebox switches with the same Broadcom silicon that Cisco and Arista use, and sell them at 40 to 60 percent less because they do not charge a brand premium.

**Open NOS**: Cumulus Linux (acquired by Nvidia in 2020), SONiC (Microsoft open-sourced in 2016), and FBOSS (Facebook internal, open-sourced in 2017) provide a NOS that runs on multiple silicon families. SONiC is especially successful at hyperscale because Microsoft is behind it, and the ecosystem now has more than 40 contributing vendors.

**SAI (Switch Abstraction Interface)**: a layer between the NOS and the silicon, similar to a HAL for the Linux kernel. With SAI, the NOS does not need to know whether the chip is Broadcom or Mellanox; it just calls the SAI API. The silicon vendor writes a SAI adapter for its own chip. This is the SDN principle applied at the silicon layer: separate control (NOS) from the data plane (silicon) and connect them through a standard API.

### The economics of whitebox

A Cisco Nexus 93180YC-EX with 48 by 25G plus 6 by 100G has a list price of about $25,000 to $30,000 (Cisco GPL 2022). An equivalent whitebox (Edgecore AS7726 or Celestica DX010) with the same Broadcom Trident 3 silicon costs about $8,000 to $10,000, a 60 to 70 percent saving, before NOS license costs (SONiC is free, Cumulus is about $2K per year per switch, Cisco bundles it into the hardware price).

At hyperscale, with hundreds of thousands of switches, this saving adds up to billions of dollars per year. Facebook, Microsoft, LinkedIn, and Alibaba all moved most of their fleet to whitebox plus SONiC between 2015 and 2020. Google took its own path: in-house custom silicon (the Jupiter ASIC), not merchant silicon, because the scale is large enough to amortize the silicon design cost.

This driver is not just about CapEx savings. Whitebox gives the operator full control of the stack: firmware update timing, the feature set, security patches, no dependence on the vendor's roadmap. At cloud scale, the ability to add a small feature (for example, change the ECMP hash function) in a few weeks instead of waiting for a vendor release is a major advantage.

### Driver 5 in the 2026 reality

Cost reduction has shifted shape. Hyperscalers such as Google, AWS, and Meta no longer run pure merchant silicon. They invest in custom silicon optimized for their own workloads (AI, microservices, security). AWS Nitro Cards (since 2017) are an example: custom ASIC plus FPGA placed inside the server, offloading the virtualization network stack from the host CPU and reducing CPU overhead from 20 to 30 percent down to under 1 percent. Meta MTIA (2023) is a custom chip for AI inference. Google TPUs are custom accelerators placed alongside the in-house Jupiter switch ASIC.

The whitebox movement is not dead. It has shifted to tier 2: smaller cloud providers (whose scale does not justify custom silicon) and large enterprises continue to use SONiC plus whitebox. The whitebox share of the data center networking market is estimated at 20 to 25 percent in 2025, per Dell'Oro Group, up from nearly 0 percent in 2010.

## 1.2.6 2026 update: which drivers are still active, which have shifted shape

Reviewing the situation 18 years after SDN began commercial use (from Nicira's founding in 2008 to 2026), the urgency of each driver has shifted noticeably:

**Driver 1 (bandwidth)** is still active but the focus has moved. CPU workload bandwidth is now sufficient. GPU and AI training are the new bottleneck. InfiniBand and RDMA over Converged Ethernet (RoCE) have taken over the fabric role. Traditional SDN is not enough for latency-critical AI training, but it still plays a role in control plane management.

**Driver 2 (agility)** has completed its mission in the cloud but not in the enterprise. AWS, GCP, and Azure have an API for every network resource, but many enterprises still use the per-device CLI. The shift is slow because of operational inertia. Kubernetes NetworkPolicy plus the CNI pushes one more step, but the enterprise legacy is still slow.

**Driver 3 (policy)** has evolved into commercial IBN (Cisco DNA, Juniper Apstra) and the open OVN NBDB. Intent-based routing and zero-trust security through a policy engine are the new standard. Note: many real IBN implementations are config generators in disguise, and true declarative behavior is still limited.

**Driver 4 (multi-tenancy)** is the strongest of the five. The cloud keeps growing. Every cloud tenant, every Kubernetes namespace, and every microservice is a "tenant" of the network. This driver has not shifted shape. It has only expanded its scope.

**Driver 5 (cost)** has shifted shape: from "merchant silicon replaces chassis vendor" (2008 to 2015) to "custom silicon for the workload" (since 2018) at the hyperscaler. For the enterprise and tier 2 cloud, whitebox plus SONiC remains the leading choice.

In summary: the five drivers have not been replaced. They have evolved. SDN as a field did not stop at OpenFlow 1.0. ### ▶ Capstone Block I Lab / Trouble Ticket 1-1: migrate a 3-switch topology to a VXLAN overlay

**Context:** You have a 3-switch pilot data center with three Cisco Catalyst 9300s connected in a triangle, with 2 to 3 hosts on each switch. The current configuration: VLAN 100 spans all 3 switches through trunks, and STP runs to prevent loops. Management asks you to assess the real effort to migrate to a VXLAN overlay on OVS, and to draw a comparison table of operational cost.

**Prerequisites:** You have read parts 1.0 and 1.1, and installed Mininet 2.3 plus OVS 2.17 per part 0.1.

**Step 1, set up the traditional topology (baseline):**

Build a Mininet topology with 3 OVS switches in a triangle, each switch with 2 hosts, all on VLAN 100:

```
$ sudo mn --custom triangle.py --topo triangle --switch ovs --controller none
```

(The `triangle.py` file builds a 3-switch triangle topology with 6 hosts.) Configure the trunk and STP on each switch. Count the CLI commands needed and assume manual entry (no automation). Record the count as X1.

**Step 3, set up the VXLAN overlay topology:**

On the same physical topology, switch to VXLAN. Each OVS switch becomes a VTEP. Build Geneve tunnels between the switches through a `br-tun` bridge. Configure flows that put the host onto VXLAN VNI 10000, and provide L2 cross-VTEP connectivity through broadcast replication inside the tenant. Count the commands and record the count as X2.

**Step 4, measure (Observe):**

| Metric | Traditional VLAN plus STP | VXLAN overlay |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|
| CLI commands to set up | X1 = ? | X2 = ? |
| Time to add a new host | ? minutes | ? minutes |
| Scale ceiling before hash collision | 4094 VLANs | 16M VNIs |
| STP-blocked links | 1 to 2 (because of the triangle) | 0 (L3 underlay ECMP) |
| Time to change the VLAN order | 5 to 10 minutes per switch | One API call, 1 second |

**Step 5, explain (compare your prediction with the real result):**

Compare X1 and X2 against your prediction in step 1. The actual result usually falls in this range: traditional takes 30 to 50 commands to set up, VXLAN takes 15 to 25 commands plus a one-time bootstrap. So X2 is meaningfully smaller than X1, and the **opposite hypothesis is rejected**: VXLAN has a real operational cost advantage. More importantly, when you add the 100th host to the VLAN, the traditional cost continues to grow linearly while VXLAN barely changes because the logical topology is decoupled from the physical one.

Compare with the five drivers:
- Driver 1 (bandwidth): traditional is blocked by STP; VXLAN uses ECMP on the underlay.
- Driver 2 (agility): traditional is manual per switch; VXLAN is API-driven.
- Driver 4 (multi-tenancy): traditional is capped at 4094 VLANs; VXLAN supports 16M VNIs.

The trouble ticket ends with a concrete migration recommendation: phase 1, set up VXLAN in parallel; phase 2, migrate traffic; phase 3, decommission the old VLAN.
