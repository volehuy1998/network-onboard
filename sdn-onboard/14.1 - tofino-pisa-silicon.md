## 14.1.0 Opening drama: from "fixed-function ASIC" to Tofino silicon in 2016

Between 2010 and 2013, the silicon switch market was dominated by **Broadcom** (Trident, Tomahawk series) and **Marvell** (Prestera). These ASICs were "fixed-function": the parser was hard-coded for a known set of protocols (Ethernet, VLAN, IPv4, IPv6, MPLS, GRE), and the match-action tables were fixed with specific fields. To add support for VXLAN (2014), the industry had to wait two years for a new ASIC generation. New protocols kept coming (Geneve in 2015, NSH in 2016, SRv6 in 2017), and the industry hit a bottleneck: **the silicon roadmap was slower than the protocol roadmap**.

In 2013, **Pat Bosshart** (Intel, ex-Broadcom) along with **Martin Izzard** (AMD) and **Nick McKeown** (Stanford) published the SIGCOMM 2013 paper **"Forwarding Metamorphosis: Fast Programmable Match-Action Processing in Hardware"**. The paper proposed **PISA** (Protocol-Independent Switch Architecture), a new ASIC design with three programmable blocks:

- **Programmable Parser:** a state machine that can parse any header sequence per a P4 program.
- **Match-Action Pipeline:** several stages (12 in the original design) with a generic match-action unit.
- **Programmable Deparser:** reorders the headers as specified.

The paper showed that PISA can deliver line rate equivalent to a fixed-function ASIC with only 10 to 20 percent silicon area overhead. In 2015, **Barefoot Networks** (founded by McKeown, Izzard, Bosshart, and Changhoon Kim) announced the **first-generation Tofino ASIC**: 6.5 Tbps full-duplex, 65 ports of 100 GbE, 12 plus 12 match-action stages, 16 nm TSMC process. This was the first commercial ASIC programmable in P4.

From 2016 to 2023, Tofino went through three generations: T1 (6.5 Tbps, 2016), T2 (12.8 Tbps, 2020), T3 (25.6 Tbps, announced in 2021 but cancelled). Google, Microsoft, and Alibaba deployed Tofino in hyperscale datacenters: INT telemetry (Google B4), the SilkRoad load balancer (Microsoft), and the NetCache distributed key-value cache (Microsoft). The story did not end well. On 3 January 2023, Intel announced the **end of life of the entire Tofino line**: no T4, no successor roadmap. Tofino market share never reached the volume needed to justify a continued silicon roadmap.

This Part 14.1 dives into the **Tofino silicon architecture**: the three pipeline blocks, the four per-stage resource pools, the compile constraints of `p4c-tofino`, and the migration path after end of life. It closes with an exercise on resource allocation and a capstone POE on "after the Tofino end of life, what is the future of P4 hardware?"

> **Common misconception:** *"The Tofino end of life means P4 is dead."*
>
> **Reality:** The Tofino end of life is one hardware vendor stopping its silicon roadmap. The P4 ecosystem still has: (a) BMv2 software reference, (b) DPDK backend and eBPF backend (Linux kernel), (c) the AMD Pensando DPU with P4Runtime support (Elba, Giglio), (d) research testbeds (Stanford, Cornell, CMU using legacy Tofino through 2027). P4 as a data-plane description language continues to grow, with fewer hardware vendors and more software targets.

## 14.1.1 PISA reference architecture

PISA silicon organises packet processing into three pipeline groups: **Ingress Parser, Ingress Match-Action (MA) Pipeline, Traffic Manager and Packet Buffer, Egress Match-Action Pipeline, and Egress Deparser**. In Tofino T1, each MA pipeline has 12 sequential stages. A packet enters from a port. The SerDes (serialiser-deserialiser) converts the 25G, 50G, or 100G electrical signal into bytes. The parser parses the header. The packet flows through 12 ingress stages, enters the Traffic Manager (packet buffer plus queueing plus multicast replication), flows through 12 egress stages, the deparser reassembles the header, the SerDes converts back to signal, and the packet exits the port. The total measured port-to-port latency is 397 nanoseconds for Tofino T1 at a 1.25 GHz clock (Barefoot whitepaper 2016).

Each stage is a **Match-Action Unit**, a self-contained compute block with four resource pools. (1) **TCAM** (Ternary CAM) for masked match. Each Tofino T1 stage has about 48 TCAM entries times 128 bits. (2) **SRAM** for exact match. Each stage has about 80 SRAM blocks times 1024 entries times 128 bits. (3) An **ALU bank**. Each stage has about 32 ALUs for arithmetic, logic, and hash operations in actions. (4) **PHV containers**. The Packet Header Vector holds the parsed headers. There are 192 containers in total, shared across the 12 stages, with widths of 8, 16, and 32 bits. All these resources are shared between the ingress and egress pipelines, so the P4 programmer has to budget for both sides when writing the program.

The Packet Buffer (also called Traffic Manager in Barefoot documentation) is a large RAM of about 20 MB SRAM between the ingress deparser and the egress parser. This is where packets wait when the output port is congested, where multicast replication happens (one packet in, N packets out), and where priority scheduling executes. The Packet Buffer is not P4-programmable; the control plane configures it through an API (queue mapping, ECN threshold, drop policy). However, the P4 programmer can **assign a queue ID** in the last ingress stage through metadata, which influences which queue the packet enters in the Packet Buffer.

## 14.1.2 Tofino generations 1, 2, and 3

**Tofino 1 (T1, 2016 to 2017):** aggregate switching capacity 6.5 Tbps (Barefoot announcement) with up to 65 ports of 100 GbE, 32 ports of 200 GbE, or 16 ports of 400 GbE. 16 nm TSMC process, 1.25 GHz clock, about 200 W power. 12 plus 12 stage pipeline. Used in switches by Edgecore (Wedge 100BF-32X), Arista (7170 Series), and the Barefoot Deep Insight reference design. Note: many documents say "3.3 Tbps", which is half-duplex; full-duplex (both directions) is 6.5 Tbps.

**Tofino 2 (T2, 2020):** aggregate switching capacity 12.8 Tbps, 7 nm TSMC process, 1.35 GHz clock. 20 plus 20 stage pipeline (double the T1 count, allowing more complex P4 programs). About 350 W power. Maximum port configuration: 32 ports of 400 GbE or 64 ports of 200 GbE. Used in the Intel Tofino 2 reference board and a few ODM switches (Inventec, Edgecore). Improvements over T1: more PHV containers (256 versus 192), larger stages (TCAM and SRAM per stage doubled), and support for egress mirror to ingress (recirculation).

**Tofino 3 (T3):** roadmap announced in 2021 but never produced commercially in volume. Target: 25.6 Tbps. Development stopped when Intel decided on the end of life in January 2023. Some engineering samples exist inside Intel, but were never released commercially.

> **Technical note:** "Tbps" above is the **aggregate switching capacity** (total full-duplex bandwidth across all ports). The per-port throughput is a different metric. T1 reaches 100 Gbps per port at line rate for a simple P4 program. The number drops if the P4 program is complex and traverses many stages. **Packets per second (PPS)** is a third metric. T1 reaches about 4.8 Bpps (billion packets per second) with 64-byte packets. This number is fixed regardless of the P4 program (PISA guarantees line rate).

> **Common misconception:** *"Tofino T2 is faster than T1 because of the higher clock."*
>
> **Reality:** T1 clock is 1.25 GHz, T2 clock is 1.35 GHz, a difference of 8 percent. The actual throughput doubles (6.5 to 12.8 Tbps) mainly because of (a) a smaller process node (16 nm to 7 nm) that allows more transistors per die, (b) double the number of ingress plus egress stages (12 to 20), (c) improved SerDes (25G to 50G PAM4), and (d) larger die size. Not the clock rate. This is a common pattern in silicon: when the process node shrinks, more computation can be packed per die, without necessarily raising the clock (a higher clock often raises power consumption too much).

## 14.1.3 Per-stage resources and compile constraints

When a P4 programmer writes a program for Tofino, the `p4c-tofino` compiler (in the Barefoot SDE, not open source) has to solve the **resource allocation problem**: each table in the P4 source has to be placed in one or more stages, subject to the constraint that the per-stage TCAM, SRAM, ALU, and PHV budget is not exceeded. This is an **NP-hard scheduling problem**. The compiler uses heuristics and may report "does not fit" if no solution is found.

Four common compile errors that a Tofino programmer needs to know: (1) **PHV allocation failure**, when the total bits of header fields exceed 192 containers times the size on T1, or 256 on T2. The fix: keep fewer fields across the pipeline, use less metadata, or pack fields. (2) **Stage-depth exceeded**, when the P4 program has too many dependent tables (table B needs the result of table A). The compiler cannot parallelise, and the chain exceeds 12 stages. The fix: refactor the control flow to reduce dependencies. (3) **Action budget per stage**, when each stage allows only N actions to run in parallel. The fix: split a table into smaller tables on different stages. (4) **TCAM overflow**, when total ternary entries exceed the TCAM capacity. The fix: switch the match type to exact or LPM, or shrink the table size.

The table-to-stage mapping has to follow the **control-flow order** of the P4 source. If the P4 source has `t1.apply(); t2.apply();`, then t2 has to live in a stage greater than or equal to the stage of t1. If `if (t1.apply().hit) { t2.apply(); }`, then t2 depends on the match result of t1, so the compiler has to place t2 at least one stage after t1 to give the data time to propagate through registers. An optimisation that the compiler performs: if two tables are independent (no shared field, no control-flow dependency), the compiler can place them on the same stage (table fusion), reducing pipeline depth.

Registers and counters are allocated differently. A pure counter (increment only) can share an ALU slot with an action, but a register (read-modify-write each packet) needs its own per-stage ALU, because each packet must read the old state, compute, and write the new state in one clock cycle. A register in P4 takes 1 to 2 ALU slots per stage depending on width. The programmer has to plan register access carefully; too many registers per stage will cause compile failure.

## 14.1.4 Intel acquisition and the end-of-life roadmap

Barefoot Networks was founded in 2013 by Nick McKeown (Stanford), Martin Izzard, Pat Bosshart, and Changhoon Kim. The company raised $130M before being acquired. **Intel acquired Barefoot Networks on 10 June 2019**, with no officially announced price but estimates between $500M and $650M based on industry sources. After the deal, Barefoot became the **Intel Connectivity Group** under DCG (Data Center Group), continuing to develop Tofino 2 and 3 and the P4 and P4Studio ecosystem.

**On 3 January 2023**, Intel announced the **end-of-life plan for the Tofino line**. The notice stated: (a) no Tofino 4 or successor generation, (b) Tofino 1 and 2 continue in production at least through 2027 with last time buy at end of 2026, (c) SDE maintenance (bug fixes, security patches) through 2028. The official reason: "strategic realignment to focus on high-growth areas". The technical reason was not announced, but industry speculation: Tofino did not reach the volume needed to sustain the line, competition from Broadcom Tomahawk took the hyperscale segment, and Intel refocused on Ethernet Controllers (the 800 Series NIC) and the IPU (Infrastructure Processing Unit, the DPU class).

The migration roadmap for P4 customers after end of life has three branches. (1) **Stay on Tofino.** Intel guarantees supply through 2026, fitting research and specific niches (for example, telecom time-synchronisation use cases and research testbeds at Stanford and Cornell). (2) **Move to AMD Pensando DPU.** Pensando supports P4 through P4Runtime on its ARM-based DPU (Elba, Giglio); the throughput is lower (about 200 Gbps per card) but tightly integrated with the host CPU, well suited for smart NIC deployments. (3) **Move to NVIDIA BlueField.** BlueField 3 does not natively support P4. It uses the DOCA SDK with a Flow API that has different semantics from P4. The programmer has to rewrite the logic. Choices (1) and (2) preserve the P4 investment, (3) discards it.

**Impact on the P4 ecosystem from 2024 to 2026:** the P4 consortium recognised this risk by 2023. The reaction: more investment in the **eBPF backend of `p4c`** (for the Linux kernel data plane), the **DPDK backend** (for userspace networking), and the **PSA-eBPF** standard (the PSA architecture running on eBPF). P4 will continue to live through CPU-based network programming, even as the hardware acceleration tier shrinks. The value of learning P4 today is to understand the data-plane programming model (which generalises to XDP, DPDK rte_swx, NVIDIA DOCA Flow), not just to code for Tofino.

**Predict (POE step 1):** Before compiling a simple P4 program (L2 forwarding) for Tofino, predict:
- Stages used: about 2 to 4 stages (an L2 lookup table plus a forward action, no deep parsing needed).
- TCAM utilisation: near 0 percent (L2 exact match uses SRAM hash, not TCAM).
- SRAM utilisation: linear in the table size; 256 entries is roughly 2 SRAM blocks, less than 5 percent of the 80 blocks per stage.
- PHV containers: about 10 to 20 containers (Ethernet 112 bits plus standard metadata of about 80 bits, fitting into 8 times 16-bit plus 2 times 32-bit containers).
- Compile time: less than 30 seconds for a simple program (BMv2 less than 1 second).

**Observe (POE step 2):** Actual results (with Option A, Tofino hardware plus SDE):
- The `Table allocation report` confirms stages 2 and 3 (2 stages used), 0 percent TCAM, about 2 to 5 percent SRAM, about 10 PHV containers.
- `jq '.tables[] | {name, stage}' context.json` lists each table and its stage placement.
- The binary file `tofino.bin` is generated successfully, less than 1 MB in size.

**Explain (POE step 3):** What this exercise teaches.

First, **the 12 ingress stages plus 12 egress stages of Tofino create a hard constraint**. A complex P4 program may not fit in 12 stages and may fail to compile with a "Cannot fit" error. Production P4 development requires a compile-iterate-optimise loop to fit the resource budget. With a simple L2 program that uses only 2 to 4 stages, there are still 8 to 10 stages available for more complex business logic (ACL, routing, load balancing, telemetry).

Second, **TCAM is expensive** (limited number of blocks, about 48 per stage on T1) compared with **SRAM, which is cheap** (more blocks, about 80 per stage on T1). Exact match goes to SRAM (hash lookup, O(1)). Ternary or wildcard match goes to TCAM (hardware CAM lookup, where each bit in the entry can match 0, 1, or don't care). Good P4 design minimises TCAM use where possible, preferring exact match for MAC and IP, and reserving TCAM for ACLs with wildcard masks.

Third, after Intel's end of life in 2023, the P4 programmable hardware ecosystem is in an uncertain state. The AMD Pensando DPU and NVIDIA BlueField DOCA Pipelines are alternatives that have programmable elements, but the semantics are different from pure P4. Learning P4 on BMv2 plus the eBPF backend still has conceptual value (understanding the match-action model, the PISA abstraction), even as the production silicon path narrows.

**Falsification test:** Try to compile a very complex P4 program (for example, 50 ACL tables with wildcard match plus 20 registers plus a chain of dependencies between tables) for Tofino T1. Prediction: the compile will fail with a "TCAM overflow" or "stage depth exceeded" error. Verification: turn on the `--verbose 2` flag and read the specific error message (for example, "table `t42` cannot fit in remaining stages"). This experiment confirms that **the compiler is an NP-hard solver**: not every P4 program will compile on a hardware target.

## 14.1.5 Comparison: Tofino PISA versus fixed-function ASIC (Broadcom) versus DPDK CPU

Three modern data-plane options compared across seven dimensions:

| Dimension | Broadcom Trident or Tomahawk (fixed) | Intel Tofino PISA (programmable) | DPDK CPU (x86) |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------|
| Throughput | 12.8 Tbps (Tomahawk 4), 25.6 Tbps (Tomahawk 5) | 6.5 Tbps (T1), 12.8 Tbps (T2) | 40 to 80 Gbps per core |
| Per-packet latency | 300 to 500 ns (fixed pipeline) | 397 ns (T1, verified by Barefoot) | 2 to 10 microseconds (5 to 25 times slower) |
| Protocol flexibility | Fixed: Ethernet, VLAN, IPv4, IPv6, MPLS, GRE, VXLAN | Programmable: any protocol through P4 | Programmable: any through C code |
| Silicon area overhead | Baseline | Plus 10 to 20 percent versus fixed (PISA paper) | Not applicable (general CPU) |
| Development tooling | Vendor SDK (proprietary) | `p4c` open source plus SDE proprietary | DPDK C API (open) |
| Deployment scale | Hyperscale (Google, Meta, Microsoft spine) | Limited (research and specific use cases) | Hypervisor, edge, DPU offload |
| Market status in 2026 | Growing (Tomahawk 5 shipping) | End of life 2023, Pensando and BlueField alternative | Growing (DPU, XDP, eBPF) |

**When to choose what:**

- **Broadcom fixed** for: hyperscale spine-leaf, standard workload (Ethernet, VXLAN, MPLS), low cost per port.
- **Tofino PISA** (legacy) for: research networks, INT telemetry, line-rate stateful load balancing. Only if a deployment is already in place from before 2023.
- **DPDK CPU** for: hypervisor data plane (OVS DPDK), SmartNIC offload, flexible scale-out through software.

**Important point:** A fixed-function ASIC is not strictly worse than PISA in every aspect. For the majority of hyperscale use cases (pure L2 or L3 forwarding plus VXLAN), Broadcom Tomahawk is enough and cheaper. PISA wins when the problem demands a custom protocol or stateful processing at line rate, but does not win when the problem is standard forwarding.

### Comparison with OVS plus OVN

OVS plus OVN (Part 9, Part 13) is the software data plane for hypervisor workloads. The question: when does a deployment need P4 or Tofino instead of OVS?

**OVS plus OVN serves well:**
- East-west overlay (Geneve tunnel between VMs and pods), 10 to 40 Gbps per hypervisor.
- Stateful ACL plus conntrack through the `ct()` action (Part 9.24).
- Multi-tenant isolation through datapath zones.

**P4 or Tofino adds value when:**
- Fabric-level telemetry (INT, every packet carries a timestamp plus queue depth across the spine-leaf fabric).
- Stateful load balancing above 100 Gbps on a single device (SilkRoad class).
- A new protocol that OVS does not yet support (for example, an SRv6 variant or proprietary tunnelling).

**Combined pattern:** "Tofino at the spine plus OVS at the hypervisor". Tofino handles INT and load balancing, and OVS handles overlay and tenant isolation. They do not exclude each other.

## 14.1.6 Key takeaways

**Six core points:**

1. **PISA is an ASIC design pattern** (Bosshart, SIGCOMM 2013), not a product. PISA equals Programmable Parser plus Match-Action Pipeline plus Programmable Deparser. Silicon overhead is about 10 to 20 percent compared with fixed-function.
2. **Tofino is the first commercial implementation of PISA** (Barefoot 2015). Generations: T1 (6.5 Tbps, 2016), T2 (12.8 Tbps, 2020), T3 (cancelled in 2023).
3. **Each stage has four resource pools:** TCAM (about 48 blocks on T1), SRAM (about 80 blocks on T1), ALU (about 32 per stage), PHV containers (192 on T1, 256 on T2). The budget is shared between ingress and egress.
4. **`p4c-tofino` is an NP-hard solver:** it allocates tables to stages without exceeding the budget. Four common compile errors: PHV allocation, stage-depth exceeded, action budget, TCAM overflow.
5. **The Packet Buffer sits between the ingress and egress deparsers** and is not P4-programmable. The control plane configures queueing, multicast replication, and priority. P4 only assigns the queue ID through metadata.
6. **Intel ended the life of Tofino in 2023.** Last time buy 2026 to 2027, SDE maintenance through 2028. Three migration paths: (a) keep Tofino for research and specific use cases, (b) Pensando DPU with P4Runtime, (c) NVIDIA BlueField DOCA (different semantics from P4, requiring a logic rewrite).

### Capstone POE: After the Tofino end of life of 2023, what is the future of P4 hardware?

> **Lab verification status:** This POE is based on publicly documented deployments and vendor roadmaps from 2024 to 2026. Wait two to three more years for the ecosystem to settle before treating the data as definitive.

**Goal:** Evaluate four post-Tofino P4 hardware options and recommend a path for an organisation that wants to continue investing in P4.

**Scenario:** Organisation Y deployed 20 Tofino T1 switches starting in 2019 for INT telemetry plus stateful load balancing. Tofino reached end of life in 2023, with last time buy in 2026. The CTO asks the network team: "What do we move to after 2027?"

**Four options:**

1. **Stay on Tofino through end-of-life support (2028).** Buy spare T1 and T2 stock up to the 2026 limit, run through 2028, then retire.
2. **Migrate to AMD Pensando DPU** (Elba, Giglio): supports P4Runtime, throughput about 200 Gbps per card, integrated with the x86 host.
3. **Migrate to NVIDIA BlueField DOCA:** does not support P4 natively. Uses the DOCA Flow API (different semantics) and requires logic rewrite.
4. **Retreat to software P4:** use BMv2 (research) plus DPDK backend plus eBPF backend for hypervisor workloads.

**Predict:** Evaluate the four options against three criteria (capability parity, migration cost, long-term viability).

**Observe (reasoning):**

**Option 1 (Stay on Tofino):**
- Capability: 100 percent parity (same hardware).
- Migration cost: low (no migration).
- Long-term viability: **high risk** after 2028. Spare parts limited, firmware not updated, security patches limited.

**Option 2 (Pensando DPU):**
- Capability: 60 to 70 percent parity. Throughput of 200 Gbps is about 30 times lower than Tofino's 6.5 Tbps. Suitable for per-hypervisor offload, not suitable for fabric-wide INT.
- Migration cost: medium. P4Runtime compatibility preserves the code. The hardware topology is different (the DPU lives in the server, not in a top-of-rack switch).
- Long-term viability: AMD has committed to a 5- to 7-year roadmap. Viable if AMD continues support (no discontinuation signal yet).

**Option 3 (BlueField DOCA):**
- Capability: 40 to 50 percent parity. The DOCA Flow API differs from P4 semantics, requiring a full rewrite. Throughput is higher than Pensando (about 400 Gbps on BlueField 3).
- Migration cost: high. Rewrite the code and retrain the team.
- Long-term viability: NVIDIA has committed strongly to BlueField (AI datacenter focus). Viable, but the ecosystem is incompatible with P4.

**Option 4 (Software P4):**
- Capability: 100 percent in concept (every P4 program runs on BMv2), but throughput is 0.1 to 5 percent of Tofino.
- Migration cost: low. P4 code is reused.
- Long-term viability: software is always available. But it does not replace hardware-accelerated use cases (INT plus load balancing above 100 Gbps).

**Explain:** Recommendation by use case.

- **If Tofino is used for fabric-wide INT:** combine (1) and (4). Stay on Tofino through 2028 while developing a software P4 pipeline for DPDK and eBPF. As hardware phases out, software backfills. Accept reduced telemetry granularity (sampling instead of every packet).

- **If Tofino is used for stateful load balancing:** combine (1) and (2). Stay on Tofino for the existing workload, deploy Pensando DPU for new workload. Five to ten years of runway.

- **If Tofino is used for research:** (1) plus (4). The research network uses BMv2 plus eBPF plus limited legacy Tofino through 2028. Stanford, Cornell, and CMU follow this pattern.

- **Do not recommend (3)** for an existing P4 shop: rewriting in DOCA Flow effectively throws away the P4 investment. Only consider (3) for a greenfield deployment without P4 history.

**Finish:** A universal principle is **"hardware lock-in is a bigger risk than hardware feature"**. Investment in software skills (BMv2, eBPF, DPDK) preserves value across hardware generations. Investment in hardware-specific skills (Tofino P4Studio) can be wiped out when a vendor discontinues the product. This pattern applies broadly: Cisco IOS versus Linux networking, proprietary ASIC versus merchant silicon, and so on.
