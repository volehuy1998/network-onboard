## 12.0.1 History: Charles Clos 1953

Charles Clos (Bell Labs) published "A Study of Non-Blocking Switching Networks" in 1953. This was a seminal paper on telephone switching. The idea was to build one large non-blocking switch out of many small multi-stage switches.

A 3-stage Clos topology has the form Ingress to Middle to Egress. Every ingress connects to every middle, and every middle connects to every egress. The ratio M >= N guarantees non-blocking behavior.

Sixty years later, Google, Facebook, and Microsoft re-invented Clos for the data center. Leaf-spine is the 2-stage variant.

---

## 12.0.2 3-tier legacy (access, aggregation, core)

Pre-2010 DC topology:

```
                  [Core Router]
                  /     |     \
         [Aggr1] [Aggr2] [Aggr3]
          / \     / \     / \
        Access switches (top of rack)
          |    |     |     |
         Servers
```

Problems:

- **North-South dominant.** The core becomes a bottleneck because modern workloads are East-West dominant.
- **Large STP domain**, fragile.
- **High oversubscription** (20:4:1) leads to packet drops at peak.
- **Does not scale horizontally.** You must upgrade the aggregation or the core.

---

## 12.0.3 Leaf-Spine (Clos 2-stage)

Modern DC topology from the 2010s:

```
       [Spine 1] [Spine 2] [Spine 3] [Spine 4]
          │    ╲ ╲    ╲  ╱  ╱ ╱   │
          │ full-mesh connectivity │
          │    ╱ ╱    ╱  ╲  ╲ ╲   │
       [Leaf 1] [Leaf 2] [Leaf 3] [Leaf N]
         │ │     │ │     │ │     │ │
        Servers  Servers  Servers  Servers
```

- Every leaf connects to every spine (full mesh).
- **ECMP** distributes traffic across all spines.
- **No STP.** L3 routing is used (BGP unnumbered or OSPF).
- **Predictable latency.** The maximum is 3 hops between any two servers.

**Horizontal scaling:** add a spine to grow bandwidth, add a leaf to grow port count.

## 12.0.4 Facebook F4/F16

**F4 (2014):**
- 4-layer Clos with a pod architecture.
- Scales to hundreds of thousands of servers in one building.

**F16 (2019):**
- Simplified architecture, smaller building blocks (Minipack, based on Broadcom Tomahawk 3).
- Higher-radix spine, fewer tiers.
- 400G or higher spine links.

Facebook chose Broadcom merchant silicon plus FBOSS (Facebook Open Switching System, Linux-based).

---

## 12.0.5 Google Jupiter

Google published Jupiter at SIGCOMM 2015 ("Jupiter Rising: A Decade of Clos Topologies and Centralized Control in Google's Datacenter Network").

- **Bisection bandwidth 1.3 Pbps**, the largest DC fabric in 2015.
- **Custom Google silicon.**
- **Centralized control plane** through B4, one of the earliest production SDN systems.
- **ECMP plus weighted traffic engineering.**

Jupiter set the stage for the industry to accept large-scale leaf-spine Clos with centralized control.

---

## 12.0.6 Bisection bandwidth and oversubscription

**Bisection bandwidth:** the total bandwidth across a cross-section bisection. This is a key metric for fabric capacity.

**Oversubscription ratio:** (server-facing bandwidth) divided by (uplink bandwidth) per leaf.

- 1:1 (non-blocking): a leaf has 400G of server-facing bandwidth and 400G of uplink.
- 3:1 (common enterprise): 1200G of server-facing and 400G of uplink.
- 6:1 (legacy, avoid).

High oversubscription causes East-West traffic drops at peak. Modern cloud DCs push toward 1:1 for bursty workloads.

---

## Key points to remember

First, the Clos topology from 1953 is still the foundation for modern DC fabric. Leaf-spine is the 2-stage variant.

Second, 3-tier legacy does not scale for East-West dominant traffic. Leaf-spine solves this with ECMP plus L3 routing.

Third, hyperscalers (Facebook F16, Google Jupiter) push the architecture to its limits, with petabit bisection and custom silicon.

Fourth, bisection bandwidth and oversubscription ratio are the main metrics when designing fabric. Aim for 1:1 for cloud workloads.
