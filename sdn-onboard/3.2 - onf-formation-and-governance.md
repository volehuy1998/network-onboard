## Context: from a Stanford lab to an industry consortium

Parts 3.0 and 3.1 described the Stanford Clean Slate Program, the Gates Building deployment of 2008 to 2009, the CCR paper of April 2008, and the OpenFlow 1.0 specification published on December 31, 2009. By early 2011, OpenFlow had a hardware base (HP ProCurve, NEC IP8800), a software stack (NOX controller, Open vSwitch, Mininet emulator), and academic support (the GENI testbed at national scale). But there was still one important gap: **who is the official body that maintains the specification?**

The Stanford Open Networking Laboratory was an academic group. It had no legal standing to maintain a production-grade specification for the long term. When industry asked "when will the OpenFlow 1.1 specification ship, who has the authority to approve it, and is there a binding commitment if a vendor implements the specification and the specification then changes?", Stanford had no formal answer. An independent organization was needed, with:

- Legal standing (as an incorporated non-profit).
- A formal process to approve specifications.
- Clear liability about intellectual property (members agreeing to an IP policy such as royalty-free or reasonable terms).
- Stable funding (not dependent on the academic grant cycle).
- Industry visibility (members who are major buyers and users of network equipment).

This was the context for the formation of ONF. The key difference from earlier standards bodies was that ONF was started **by operators, not by vendors**. The six founders were all large consumers of network equipment (Deutsche Telekom, Facebook, Google, Microsoft, Verizon, Yahoo!). Cisco, Juniper, and HP were not on the founder list. The operator-led model was unusual and had significant meaning for the dynamics of the industry that followed.

## 3.2.1 ONF formation: the press release of March 21, 2011

### The official press release

On March 21, 2011, the **Open Networking Foundation** was officially founded with a press release published at the website onf.org (later redirected to opennetworking.org). The opening of the press release reads as follows:

> *"MENLO PARK, CA, Today, leaders from the world's largest companies that own, operate or build computer networks, data centers and campus networks have joined forces in a newly formed nonprofit organization called the Open Networking Foundation (ONF) to promote a new approach to networking called Software-Defined Networking (SDN)."*

According to the press release, ONF was a "nonprofit industry consortium to promote Software-Defined Networking and the OpenFlow protocol". The mission had three points:

**Mission 1.** Advocate for and promote the adoption of SDN with OpenFlow as the foundational protocol.

**Mission 2.** Lead the OpenFlow specification through its evolution (from version 1.1 onward).

**Mission 3.** Provide technical working groups to develop complementary specifications (OF-Config, the Northbound API, Optical Transport extensions).

### Motivation for separating from Stanford

The press release also addressed the question of why ONF separated from Stanford:

> *"The Open Networking Foundation, independent from any single vendor, will organize activities around SDN, with the initial focus on the development and standardization of an important SDN protocol: OpenFlow."*

The key phrase is "independent from any single vendor". From day one, ONF declared that it did not belong to any vendor. This was very important for operator confidence. If OpenFlow were standardized by Cisco or HP (both vendors), operators would worry that the specification would be bent to favor that vendor. An operator-led ONF removed this concern.

Stanford also formally transferred ownership of the OpenFlow specification to ONF through a license agreement at this time. Both the existing 1.0 specification and the upcoming 1.1 specification (released on February 28, 2011, the same week as the ONF press release) were transferred. The copyright notice on later versions reads "Copyright Open Networking Foundation" instead of "Copyright Stanford University".

### Archive location of the press release

The original press release is no longer available at opennetworking.org because the website has been restructured several times. The Internet Archive Wayback Machine preserves a snapshot at:

```
https://web.archive.org/web/20110322*/opennetworking.org
```

Visit the snapshot of March 22, 2011 or near it to see the original press release plus the full member list.

## 3.2.2 Founding members and motivation

### The six founding operators

The press release of March 21, 2011 lists six founding members at the Board level:

**Deutsche Telekom** (Germany): a major telecommunications provider in Europe. It operates T-Systems (its IT services arm) and is a buyer of OpenFlow technology in the telco WAN backbone.

**Facebook** (United States): a social networking operator with hyperscale data centers. Facebook had invested heavily in in-house network engineering. It later launched the Open Compute Project (also in 2011) for server hardware.

**Google** (United States): a web services operator with the largest data center fabric in the world. Google had production experience with SDN-like systems before the OpenFlow specification existed (the Jupiter fabric, the B4 WAN). ONF membership formalized Google's engagement with the broader industry community.

**Microsoft** (United States): a cloud and consumer-services operator. The Azure data center network and the Microsoft research network were both targets for SDN.

**Verizon** (United States): a telecommunications provider with a backbone WAN and a wireless core network. Verizon FiOS fiber and 4G LTE networks were both candidates for SDN.

**Yahoo!** (United States): a web services operator. In 2011 Yahoo was still a major player, before its decline from 2013 to 2016.

### Seventeen early-adopter members

Beyond the six Board founders, the press release also lists 17 "early adopter" members, representing vendors and supporting companies:

- **Silicon and ASIC vendors**: Broadcom, Marvell.
- **Switch vendors**: Brocade, Ciena, Cisco (yes, Cisco joined as an early adopter even though it was not a founder), Dell Force10, Ericsson, Extreme Networks, HP, IBM, Juniper, NEC, Netgear.
- **Software and integration**: Citrix, Riverbed, VMware.
- **ISP and infrastructure**: NTT (Nippon Telegraph and Telephone).

A particular detail to notice: **Cisco and HP**, the two vendors with the earliest OpenFlow implementations, both joined as early adopters rather than as founders. This reflected the operator-led intent of ONF accurately. Operators led, vendors followed. Vendors had no Board seats. Only operators had Board seats in the original structure.

### Operators forced vendors to participate

Why did vendors join even without Board seats? The commercial reason was simple. **The six founding operators were among the top 10 buyers of network equipment in the world.** If these operators demanded OpenFlow-capable equipment, vendors could not refuse. Any vendor that did not support OpenFlow would lose business from these six operators, and would lose potential business from dozens of operators that would follow them.

This was a rare example of an operator-driven standard in networking history. Most earlier standards bodies were vendor-driven. IEEE 802.1 was chaired and mainly funded by Cisco, Juniper, and HP. Feature decisions all went through a vendor lens. IETF had broader participation (academic, vendor, and operator), but vendor engineers made up most of the active contributions.

ONF inverted this dynamic. The Board of operators decided the technical direction. Vendors contributed and implemented. If a vendor disagreed with the direction, the operator members could decide not to buy that vendor's products. This power dynamic pushed the networking industry into an operator-centric era from 2011 to 2015, with many innovations driven by hyperscaler needs.

> **Key point.** An operator-led standard is rare but powerful. When 6 of the top 10 buyers push in the same direction, vendors have to follow regardless of preference. This let OpenFlow be adopted faster than alternative standards (ForCES, early OPENSIG) that did not have operator backing. The lesson for standards work is that who is paying matters more than who is designing.

## 3.2.3 ONF technical committees and working groups

### Governance structure

ONF governance has three tiers, defined in the bylaws published at github.com/opennetworkingfoundation/legal:

**Board of Directors (11 seats).** Represents the operator members. Decides strategic direction, approves the budget, and sets membership policy. The original Board was 6 founders plus 5 seats for additional operators that joined. The Board meets quarterly.

**Technical Steering Committee (TSC).** Elected by the Board. Responsible for detailed technical direction. The TSC reviews work items from the Working Groups, approves specification releases, and arbitrates technical disputes. The TSC has 7 to 9 members, a mix of operators and neutral technical experts.

**Working Groups (WGs).** This is where the actual technical work happens. Each Working Group focuses on a specific topic, has a chair and a co-chair, and has members from any ONF member (operator or vendor). WGs meet bi-weekly or monthly by conference call, with face-to-face plugfests and member meetings each quarter.

### The main Working Groups, 2011 to 2018

The most important Working Groups, by deliverable:

**Configuration and Management (the OF-CONFIG WG).** Develops the OF-Config specification, a protocol that lets a controller configure the management plane of an OpenFlow switch (not the data plane). OF-Config is based on NETCONF and YANG. It lets operators configure switch properties such as ports, controllers, and certificates through a standardized protocol. OF-Config 1.0 was released in 2012, version 1.2 in 2014.

**The Forwarding Abstractions Working Group (FAWG).** Develops abstractions on top of the OpenFlow flow table. The key deliverable is the TTP (Table Type Patterns) specification, ONF TS-017 (August 2014). It lets a controller query a switch about its supported pipeline structure. After 2015 the FAWG split. One part moved to the P4 Language Consortium (which ran in parallel from 2014 to 2018). The other part stayed at ONF for Flow Objectives.

**The Northbound Interfaces WG (NBI WG).** Develops APIs between the SDN controller and applications above. The major deliverable was the Intent-based NBI (2014 to 2016), a high-level policy API. It never reached widespread adoption because of competition with vendor-specific APIs from Cisco and VMware.

**The Optical Transport WG.** Extends OpenFlow for optical switching (ROADM, OTN). The key deliverable is the Transport API (T-API), versions 2.1 and 2.4, for SDN-driven optical transport. Adoption was limited to a few major telecom operators, mainly AT&T and Deutsche Telekom.

**The Security WG.** Develops the security aspects of OpenFlow, including the TLS requirement, topology discovery security, and controller authentication. Deliverables were small but impactful, notably the recommendation that TLS be mandatory for production deployments.

**The Testing and Interoperability WG.** Organized the "Plugfest" events, where vendors brought equipment to test interoperability with multiple controllers and switches. Plugfests took place 2 to 3 times a year from 2012 to 2017, hosted at ONF member facilities. They produced interoperability reports published to ONF members.

### Approval process

A new specification goes through this process:

1. **WG draft.** WG members develop a draft specification through meetings and collaborative edits in shared documents.
2. **TSC review.** When the draft is stable, the TSC reviews it for correctness, compatibility with existing specifications, and scope appropriateness.
3. **Member vote.** The draft specification is submitted for an ONF membership vote. Members have voting rights based on their tier (operator and vendor tiers).
4. **Publish.** After approval, the specification is published at opennetworking.org with a version number and an ONF copyright.

This process differs from IETF (rough consensus on a mailing list, no formal vote) and from IEEE (a formal ballot with strict procedure). The ONF process is a hybrid: formal enough to add credibility, fast enough to keep up with the pace of technology.

## 3.2.4 Specification ownership: transfer from Stanford to ONF in 2011

### License agreement and IP transfer

In March 2011, when ONF was founded, the Stanford University Open Networking Laboratory formally transferred the OpenFlow specification IP to ONF through a license agreement. Details:

- **Specification 1.0.0** (December 31, 2009): copyright held by Stanford, transferred under an assignment agreement. Full rights for ONF to publish, modify, and evolve.
- **Specification 1.1.0** (February 28, 2011, only weeks before the ONF founding): initially held under Stanford copyright, transferred at the same time.
- **Future specifications (1.2 and later).** Developed entirely under ONF, with ONF copyright from the beginning.

The license agreement specified a royalty-free policy. ONF members had the right to implement the specification without royalties, and ONF could not charge a license fee for basic implementation. This was a difference-maker compared with proprietary protocols. Vendors implementing OpenFlow did not have to pay anyone.

Stanford retained the right to participate in ONF activities and to continue research on the specification (non-exclusively). McKeown and Parulkar attended ONF Board meetings for many years after 2011.

### Continuity of version numbering

The transfer did not disrupt versioning. The version numbers continued without a break:

- OF 1.0.0 (December 2009, Stanford), then OF 1.0.1 errata (October 2010, Stanford).
- OF 1.1.0 (February 2011, Stanford), then OF 1.2.0 (December 2011, ONF).
- OF 1.3.0 (April 2012, ONF), and so on, through OF 1.5.1 (March 2015, the last ONF revision).

The wire protocol number was also continuous: 0x01 (1.0), 0x02 (1.1), 0x03 (1.2), 0x04 (1.3), 0x05 (1.4), 0x06 (1.5). Switches that implement multiple versions can negotiate through the Hello message and fall back to a common version if controller and switch support different versions.

### Academic lab to industry consortium: a rare model

Transferring specification IP from an academic lab to an industry consortium, as Stanford did to ONF, is **uncommon**. Most protocol specifications move from academic to industry through one of two paths:

- **Startup path.** The academic lab spins off a startup, which keeps the IP as a commercial product (for example, Nicira with the initial OVS).
- **Standards body path.** The specification is submitted to IETF or W3C, and becomes an RFC or a recommendation through a standard process.

The Stanford to ONF path is rare because:

- There was no startup in between (Nicira was a startup, but it kept the NVP product separately, not the OpenFlow specification).
- There was no traditional standards body involved (neither IETF nor IEEE claimed OpenFlow).
- ONF was founded specifically to host the specification, and it did not pre-exist.

This model was possible because Stanford had a high ambition for academic-to-industry transitions. McKeown and Parulkar drove this in practice. Founding ONF was, in effect, a Stanford decision, with operator backing added later.

## 3.2.5 ONF compared with IETF, IEEE, and OCP: who does what

### Four organizations and their scope of work

Four standards organizations are important for modern networking:

**ONF (Open Networking Foundation).** Mandate: SDN architecture and the OpenFlow and P4 protocols. Detailed scope: SDN control plane, southbound protocols (OpenFlow, P4Runtime), Northbound API attempts. Operator-led governance. Membership fee: 30,000 to 50,000 United States dollars per year, depending on tier.

**IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force).** Mandate: Internet routing protocols and transport layer. Scope: BGP, OSPF, IS-IS, MPLS, RSVP, NETCONF and YANG, QUIC. Open participation (anyone can contribute). Philosophy of rough consensus and running code. No formal membership fee for participation (meeting hosts need a sponsor).

**IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers).** Mandate: physical-layer and data-link-layer standards (L1 and L2). Scope: Ethernet 802.3, VLAN 802.1Q, LACP 802.3ad, Wi-Fi 802.11. Standards committee with formal voting procedure. Membership fee: 900 United States dollars per year for an individual. Vendor membership uses a different tier.

**OCP (Open Compute Project).** Mandate: open hardware specifications. Scope: server chassis, switch hardware, storage, cooling, power distribution. Open community (anyone can contribute designs). Free participation. Facebook launched OCP in 2011, and the scope expanded to include many vendors.

### Overlap and complement

The four organizations have clear scopes and minimal overlap:

- **IEEE**: "what an Ethernet wire looks like" (VLAN tag, MAC frame, QinQ).
- **IETF**: "how packets route through the Internet" (BGP, OSPF, MPLS, transport).
- **ONF**: "where the control logic runs and how it talks to hardware" (SDN architecture, OpenFlow, P4).
- **OCP**: "how the hardware is built" (mechanical, electrical, thermal specifications).

In practice, the organizations overlap when a technology crosses boundaries. For example, SDN for optical networking needs the ONF T-API but also the ITU-T OTN standards (ITU is yet another standards body). A SmartNIC with a programmable data plane needs the P4 specification (ONF) plus OCP hardware specifications plus IEEE Ethernet compatibility.

The complementary relationship between these organizations is a feature, not a bug. Each one handles a different layer of the stack and produces compatible outputs. Operators benefit from interoperable standards across multiple organizations. They avoid lock-in to any single vendor or technology silo.

### Membership comparison

Membership tier overview:

| Organization | Lowest tier | Annual fee | Voting right? |
|--------------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| ONF | Associate | 30,000 United States dollars | Operator tier voting |
| IETF | Individual | Free | Consensus (no formal vote) |
| IEEE | Individual | 900 United States dollars | Formal ballot |
| OCP | Community | Free | Community decision |

Operators prefer ONF and OCP for the operator-led governance and the lower friction. Vendors are comfortable with IETF and IEEE because the rules are historically established. Large organizations (Microsoft, Google, and so on) participate in all four at once and contribute selectively based on priority.

## 3.2.6 The ONF and ON.Lab merger of 2018

### Background on ON.Lab

**ON.Lab (Open Networking Laboratory)** was a non-profit founded in 2012 by alumni of the Stanford Clean Slate Program to maintain the ONOS (Open Network Operating System) controller and the CORD (Central Office Re-architected as Datacenter) reference design. The founders included Guru Parulkar (formerly Executive Director of Stanford Clean Slate) and many other researchers.

ON.Lab and ONF had a tight relationship from the beginning. They shared the same community, the membership overlapped, and ON.Lab software implemented ONF specifications. But organizationally, they were two separate entities with different bylaws and different funding.

After 5 to 6 years of co-existence, the two recognized that consolidating would yield more synergy. ON.Lab was strong in software (the ONOS controller, CORD, production deployments with AT&T and Comcast) but weak in standards legitimacy. ONF was strong in standards legitimacy (the member list, specification authority) but weak in software output (specification only, no implementation).

### The merger of October 2018

On October 10, 2018, ONF and ON.Lab announced a merger to form a "unified organization" focused on Software-Defined Standards and Implementation. The merger completed in Q4 2018 with:

- **Combined leadership.** Guru Parulkar (formerly of ON.Lab) became Executive Director. The Board included operators from both organizations.
- **Combined assets.** ONF specifications, the ONOS codebase, the CORD reference design, and the P4 Language Consortium liaison.
- **New focus areas.** SD-RAN (Software-Defined Radio Access Network) for 5G, and Aether, a private 5G edge cloud.

### State in 2026

As of 2026, the post-merger ONF is a different organization from the 2011 founding:

**Shift in focus.** From a pure OpenFlow protocol leader (2011 to 2018) to broader SDN and 5G edge platforms. The OpenFlow specification is no longer in active development. Version 1.5.1 (March 26, 2015) is the last revision. The P4 Language Consortium merged into ONF (2019), and the P4 specification continues to evolve there.

**The ONOS controller** continues to be maintained, with production deployments at Comcast, Telefonica, AT&T, and many European ISPs. The latest ONOS version is 2.7 (2024), focused on 5G UPF and SD-RAN integration.

**CORD evolved into Aether**, a private 5G plus edge cloud platform for enterprises. Aether combines an OpenFlow and P4 data plane, an ONOS control plane, and Kubernetes orchestration for operators that build private or managed 5G networks.

OpenFlow itself, as a protocol, is in maintenance mode only. No new revision is planned. OVS continues to implement OpenFlow 1.3 as primary, with partial support for 1.4 and 1.5. Hardware vendors ship OpenFlow 1.3 as a baseline and do not add new features.

### OpenFlow 1.5.1 is the final revision, dated March 26, 2015

Full specification evolution timeline:

| Version | Release date | Key feature |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| 1.0.0 | December 31, 2009 | Stanford-led, 12-tuple match, single table |
| 1.0.1 | October 2010 | Stanford errata |
| 1.1.0 | February 28, 2011 | Stanford, multi-table pipeline, group table |
| 1.2.0 | December 5, 2011 | ONF first release, OXM, controller roles |
| 1.3.0 | April 25, 2012 | ONF, meter table, PBB, auxiliary channels |
| 1.3.1 to 1.3.5 | 2012 to 2015 | ONF errata chain for 1.3 (the most deployed version) |
| 1.4.0 | October 14, 2013 | ONF, bundle messages, eviction |
| 1.5.0 | December 19, 2014 | ONF, egress table, TCP flags |
| 1.5.1 | March 26, 2015 | ONF, last revision, packet-type aware |

A 1.6 specification was developed internally in September 2016 but was never published publicly. It was only available to ONF members. The working groups could not reach consensus on the direction of breaking changes for 1.6, and industry attention had shifted to P4. Version 1.5.1 is officially the last revision of the OpenFlow protocol.

Parts 4.0 to 4.6 will analyze each version of the evolution from 1.1 to 1.5.1 in detail, with features and adoption.

### Block III Capstone Guided Exercise: first-flow installation with Mininet and Ryu

**Context.** After studying the OF 1.0 specification in detail and the ONF governance, you will practice installing a simple flow entry on Mininet with the Ryu controller. The goal is to experience flow-based forwarding end to end and to verify the flow entry through the switch CLI.

**Prerequisites:**

- Mininet 2.3, installed per Part 0.1.
- Ryu controller installed: `pip install ryu`.
- Open vSwitch 2.17 (which comes with Mininet).
- A Linux host with sudo access.

**Step 1, start the Ryu controller.**

Open a first terminal and start the Ryu controller with the simple L2 learning application:

```
$ ryu-manager --verbose ryu.app.simple_switch_13
```

The controller listens on TCP port 6653 (the OpenFlow default) and logs every event.

**Step 2, start the Mininet topology.**

Open a second terminal and start Mininet with a 2-switch and 2-host topology, connected to the Ryu controller:

```
$ sudo mn --topo linear,2 --switch ovs,protocols=OpenFlow13 --controller remote,ip=127.0.0.1,port=6653
```

Mininet will connect the switches to Ryu. Ryu should print log messages when the switches connect (OFPT_HELLO, OFPT_FEATURES_REPLY).

**Step 3, test connectivity and observe PACKET_IN.**

In the Mininet CLI, ping from h1 to h2:

```
mininet> h1 ping -c 3 h2
```

Watch the Ryu log. The first ARP and the first ICMP packet trigger PACKET_IN (unknown destination). The Ryu `simple_switch_13` application learns the MAC addresses and installs flow entries. Subsequent packets do not trigger PACKET_IN (the flow is now cached).

**Step 4, inspect the flow entries on the switch.**

Open a third terminal and query the flow table of switch s1:

```
$ sudo ovs-ofctl -O OpenFlow13 dump-flows s1
```

The output shows the flow entries that Ryu installed, with the match fields and actions. Example format:

```
cookie=0x0, duration=5.123s, table=0, n_packets=3, n_bytes=294, priority=1,
  in_port=1,dl_dst=00:00:00:00:00:02 actions=output:2
```

Inspect the entry to understand it: `in_port=1,dl_dst=...` is the match, `actions=output:2` is the action, `priority=1` is the priority, and `n_packets=3` is the counter (3 packets have matched this entry so far).

**Step 5, trigger FLOW_REMOVED through timeout.**

Wait 60 seconds (the default idle timeout of `simple_switch_13`). The flow entry expires and the switch sends OFPT_FLOW_REMOVED to Ryu. Watch the Ryu log for this event.

Re-run the ping from h1 to h2:

```
mininet> h1 ping -c 1 h2
```

PACKET_IN is triggered again (the flow has expired) and Ryu re-installs the entry. This is the full first-flow cycle.

**Step 6, cleanup.**

```
mininet> exit
$ sudo mn -c
```

**Meaning.** This exercise covers the complete flow lifecycle of OF 1.0 and 1.3 (Hello handshake, Features exchange, PACKET_IN, FLOW_MOD, counters, FLOW_REMOVED). This is the foundation for understanding every advanced OpenFlow scenario later.
