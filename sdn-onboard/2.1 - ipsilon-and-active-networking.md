## Context: two branches of thinking that crossed in the mid 1990s

While DCAN and OPENSIG laid the conceptual foundation of "separate control from the switch", the networking industry from 1996 to 1997 saw two branches apply this principle in completely different directions, running in parallel and sometimes in ideological conflict.

The first branch, commercial in nature, centered on Ipsilon Networks, a startup in Sunnyvale California founded by Peter Newman with Silicon Valley VC funding. Ipsilon took the DCAN plus GSMP idea and packaged it into a concrete sellable product: IP Switching. The key difference from DCAN was that instead of letting the IP router and the ATM switch run separately in parallel (DCAN treated ATM as native and IP as an application), Ipsilon merged the two layers into a single product, with IP as the public face and ATM as the data plane hidden underneath.

The second branch was academic and research-driven, centered on the DARPA Active Networks Program. Instead of accepting that the data plane must run a fixed protocol such as ATM cell switching or IP routing, this group of researchers proposed a bolder idea: **every network node can run arbitrary code on every packet**. A packet was no longer passive data forwarded by table lookup; it could carry code, and the switch executed that code along the way.

The two branches had a common root (programmable network) but opposite philosophies. Ipsilon pushed intelligence to the edge (the IP router at the boundary) and kept the core simple (ATM cell switching). Active Networks distributed intelligence across the core, where every switch could compute. History tilted toward Ipsilon (externalized intelligence is still the SDN principle today), but the Active Networks legacy still lives on in P4 and modern programmable data planes, with a different implementation that avoids the original problems.

## 2.1.1 Ipsilon IP Switching: the first commercial debut of GSMP

Ipsilon Networks was founded in August 1994 by Peter Newman, an engineer who had previously worked at Nippon Telephone and Telegraph (NTT) and at Adaptive Corporation. The core idea came from an observation: in a network with both IP and ATM, the routing decision for forwarding-table lookup (IP) is slower than the actual cell-relay operation (ATM fast path). If you can "learn" which flows are long-lived and bypass the IP layer for those flows, overall performance improves significantly.

### The two-phase architecture of IP Switching

Ipsilon proposed a two-phase operating model:

**Phase 1: IP forwarding (for new flows).** When a new, unrecognized flow arrives at an Ipsilon IP switch, packets traverse the normal IP routing path. The switch has an IP route engine CPU that processes the packet in software, looks up the routing table, decides the next-hop, and forwards the packet. The rate is slow (a few tens of thousands of packets per second per CPU), but the path is flexible.

**Phase 2: Cell-relay bypass (for long-lived flows).** When the switch observes a flow that lasts long enough (for example, more than 10 packets in 1 second per the default heuristic), it asks the ATM layer to install a virtual circuit between the input port and the output port and maps the packets of that flow directly to ATM cell relay. After this step, packets bypass the IP route engine entirely and pass through hardware ATM fast-switching at line rate.

Communication between the IP route engine and the ATM switch hardware used GSMP v1.1 (RFC 1987, August 1996), a binary protocol that Ipsilon published as an informational RFC to encourage adoption. RFC 1987 was not a creative contribution to IETF in the standards sense; it simply documented the protocol Ipsilon was already using in its commercial product, in the hope that other ATM vendors would support GSMP so an Ipsilon IP switch could talk to any ATM switch on the market. Newman was the editor of the RFC, with co-authors from Ipsilon: William Edwards, Robert Hinden, Eric Hoffman, Fong Ching Liaw, Tom Lyon, and Greg Minshall.

A companion RFC of the same period that documented Ipsilon's "flow classifier" was RFC 1953 "Ipsilon Flow Management Protocol Specification for IPv4 Version 1.0" (May 1996, Newman, Edwards, Hinden, Hoffman, Liaw, Lyon, Minshall). IFMP was a protocol between two adjacent Ipsilon IP switches to negotiate flow-entry installation. Unlike GSMP (internal between an IP engine and an ATM switch), IFMP was external between independent devices.

### The commercial product: the Ipsilon IP Switch series

Ipsilon shipped two main products before being acquired:

- **IP Switch ESR 5000 series** (1996): combined a Sun SPARC-based IP route engine with ATM switch hardware (Fore Systems ASX-200 engine). Actual throughput was about 1 to 2 Gbps total.
- **IP Switch Gateway Ethernet (IPSG)** (1997): an enterprise-campus gateway. It did not require ATM at the edge but still used ATM in the core.

The company received significant investment and reached revenue in the tens of millions of US dollars in 1996 to 1997, but it fell into a timing trap. Cisco launched "Tag Switching" in September 1996 with an idea similar to IP Switching but without requiring ATM (it used a proprietary label over Ethernet), allowing enterprises not to abandon their existing infrastructure. Toshiba had Cell Switch Router with an architecture close to IP Switching. All three approaches targeted the same market: backbone service providers wanting to leverage fast switching for IP.

### Legacy: the path from IP Switching to MPLS

In July 1997, IETF chartered the MPLS Working Group (Multiprotocol Label Switching). The group was tasked with unifying three competing ideas (Ipsilon IP Switching, Cisco Tag Switching, and Toshiba CSR) into a single standard. The result was RFC 3031 "Multiprotocol Label Switching Architecture" (Eric Rosen, Arun Viswanathan, Ross Callon, January 2001) and RFC 3032 "MPLS Label Stack Encoding" published at the same time.

MPLS won over IP Switching on three key points:

**No ATM requirement.** An MPLS label is a 32-bit header inserted after the L2 header (Ethernet, PPP, or Frame Relay) and before the L3 header. An MPLS router does not need ATM hardware, only a firmware update. Enterprises did not have to replace their core infrastructure.

**Label stacking.** RFC 3032 allows multiple labels to be stacked, with each label carrying a separate dimension of policy (VPN ID, traffic-engineering tunnel, fast-reroute backup path). IP Switching had no stacking and could only distinguish flows at a basic level.

**Traffic-engineering ready.** RFC 3209 "RSVP-TE" (December 2001) lets the operator set up an explicit path through the network for optimization, a use case that service providers especially needed.

Ipsilon was acquired by Nokia in December 1997 for about 120 million US dollars, less than two years after launching its first product. Nokia then nearly stopped developing IP Switching and refocused on mobile networking. By 2000 to 2001, the IP Switching product had disappeared from the market entirely, replaced by MPLS.

The timely lesson from Ipsilon is not that the technology was wrong (GSMP was a good idea). It is **timing and coupling with a declining technology**. Ipsilon locked itself to ATM just as ATM began to decline. Whenever an engineer is considering an SDN technology lock-in to a specific data plane (for example, running only on a Tofino P4 ASIC), the Ipsilon story is a cautionary reference.

> **Key point:** Ipsilon did not die because GSMP was poor. It died because ATM was poor (in the face of Ethernet dominance). MPLS inherited the "label plus control-data separation" idea but removed the ATM dependency, becoming the dominant WAN and service-provider technology for the next 20 years. When OpenFlow appeared in 2009, it did the same with Ethernet: it inherited the control-data separation of GSMP and MPLS, running on the most widely available hardware.

## 2.1.2 Ipsilon to Cisco Tag Switching to MPLS: a five-year lineage

The path of the label-switching idea from Ipsilon 1996 to MPLS 2001 is a classic case study of how the networking industry assimilates innovation. Timeline:

- **08/1994**: Ipsilon is founded; Peter Newman files a patent on IP Switching.
- **05/1996**: RFC 1953 IFMP is published (informational), the flow-management protocol between IP switches.
- **08/1996**: RFC 1987 GSMP v1.1 is published (informational), the GSMP between an IP engine and an ATM switch.
- **09/1996**: Cisco announces Tag Switching, a competing approach using a "tag" (the precursor to the MPLS label) instead of an ATM VPI/VCI.
- **12/1997**: Ipsilon is acquired by Nokia. IETF charters the MPLS WG.
- **1998-2000**: The MPLS WG absorbs ideas from Tag Switching, Cell Switch Router, and IP Switching into a single standard.
- **01/2001**: RFC 3031 MPLS Architecture is published, the complete standard.
- **12/2001**: RFC 3209 RSVP-TE is published, traffic engineering over MPLS.

This progression shows a recurring pattern in networking: a startup proposes a breakthrough idea, a large vendor (Cisco) develops a competing variant, IETF intervenes to unify the work into an open standard, and the startup is acquired or withdraws. A similar pattern played out with Nicira and NVP versus VMware NSX (Part 5.1) 15 years later, and with Google B4 and SDN versus hyperscaler-specific traffic engineering. Modern SDN has to learn how to navigate this pattern, not only to avoid the Ipsilon fate but also to align vendor interests with the absorption process.

## 2.1.3 Active Networking: the DARPA program 1996-2001

While Ipsilon and MPLS both respected the assumption "the data plane has a fixed protocol; the control plane sets up flows in advance", the DARPA Active Networks Program challenged this assumption at the root. The program started in 1996 under the lead of David Tennenhouse (DARPA ISTO, who later returned to MIT as a professor) and Dan Wetherall (MIT), with a budget of tens of millions of US dollars over five years. The goal was to **let users and applications deploy arbitrary code into network nodes, where that code executes on packets passing through**.

### Foundational paper: Tennenhouse and Wetherall, CCR 1996

"Towards an Active Network Architecture" by David L. Tennenhouse and David J. Wetherall (ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review 26(2), 5-18, April 1996) made the manifesto: *"An active network is a network whose nodes perform computations on the user data flowing through them, whereby the user may customize these computations."* The paper distinguished three levels of programmability:

- **Configuration**: the operator tunes parameters (queue size, routing metric) but does not change algorithms.
- **Programmability**: the operator changes the algorithm through a standardized API (the OPENSIG model).
- **Active Networking**: the user (the application) injects code into the network node, and the code runs per packet or per flow.

Active Networking is the most extreme of the three, equivalent to "letting a script run inside the router". This idea was so radical that many academics cautiously considered it "not feasible in production" from the start, but DARPA funding allowed deep experimentation.

### Five main participating groups

The Active Networks program brought together about 10 to 15 research groups from leading US universities. The five biggest contributors were:

**MIT with ANTS** (Active Network Toolkit): a Java framework for running capsules (packets containing bytecode) on a switch. Each capsule contained a header, a Java class, and user data. The node read the class, loaded it into an embedded JVM, and executed a method that processed the data. Wetherall was the principal investigator of ANTS.

**University of Pennsylvania with SwitchWare and PLAN** (Programming Language for Active Networks): a different approach from ANTS. It used a domain-specific language (PLAN) instead of general-purpose Java, with a type system that guaranteed the packet handler would terminate in bounded time. Jonathan Smith was the principal investigator.

**Columbia with NetScript**: a custom scripting language for network configuration and active processing, with emphasis on composition (chaining small modules into a complex pipeline). Andrew Campbell and Henning Schulzrinne were the principal investigators.

**Princeton with RCANE** (Resource Controlled Active Network Engine): focused on resource management, with CPU and memory quotas per capsule to prevent a faulty capsule from consuming all node resources. Andrew Whitaker and David Wetherall (after moving from MIT) led the work.

**BBN with Smart Packet**: research aimed at commercial applicability, targeting the network-management use case rather than per-packet computation. Beverly Schwartz was the principal investigator.

Two seminal papers shared by the program, in addition to Tennenhouse and Wetherall 1996, are "Architectural Framework for Active Networks" by Ken Calvert (Georgia Tech) and others (ACM CCR 1999), which defined a reference model, and RFC 4728 "A Resource-Oriented Abstraction for Multi-Domain Services" (February 2007), which summarized some of the late-program lessons.

## 2.1.4 Two schools: capsules versus programmable switches

Among the Active Networking groups, a key technical debate existed about **where code is deployed and when it executes**. Two opposite schools emerged:

### The capsule approach: code travels with the packet

Characterized by MIT ANTS. Each packet is a self-contained "capsule" that carries the Java bytecode to execute. When the capsule arrives at a node, the node loads the bytecode, executes the `eval()` method, and the method decides where the packet is forwarded (or dropped, or cloned, or whether the payload is modified).

**Strengths**: maximum flexibility. A user can deploy any algorithm by changing the Java class. No out-of-band coordination with the operator is needed; just sending a packet deploys the code. This is "pure" Active Networking in the original Tennenhouse 1996 sense.

**Weaknesses**: three serious problems.

Performance. JVM class loading per packet costs 100 microseconds to a few milliseconds, nowhere near hardware forwarding speed (a few nanoseconds). It can run only at a few thousand packets per second per node, three orders of magnitude below Gigabit line rate.

Security. Arbitrary code runs on the node at high priority. Even with a Java sandbox, a malicious capsule can exhaust CPU or memory, exploit a JVM bug to escape the sandbox, or have colluding capsules create a covert channel through node state. The problem of "security strong enough to let untrusted code run at network speed" was never solved adequately.

Operability. The operator loses visibility into the network's behavior. You cannot predict what the node will do when it receives capsule X because the code in X is decided by the user. Debugging and troubleshooting become nearly impossible.

### Programmable switches: code is deployed separately from the data flow

Characterized by UPenn SwitchWare and PLAN, and by later hybrid approaches. The operator or an authorized user deploys code to the node through a separate management channel, not through the packet. The packet only triggers code already present on the node (for example, a packet with flag X invokes function Y that is already loaded); it does not carry the code.

**Strengths**: solves the two major problems of the capsule approach. Performance: code can be JIT-compiled at load time, cached, and executed quickly when a packet triggers it. Security: the operator controls what code is loaded and audits it before deployment. An untrusted user cannot inject code.

**Weaknesses**: loses the "user-level programmability" that Tennenhouse 1996 declared. The application cannot deploy code on its own; it must go through the operator. Flexibility decreases, closer to the OPENSIG model than to pure Active Networking.

History tilted toward programmable switches. Every modern SDN follows this model. The controller (OpenFlow, OVN) deploys flow rules or logical flows into the switch through an out-of-band control channel, and the packet only triggers rules already installed. P4 follows the same model: a P4 program is compiled and loaded into the switch beforehand, and the packet only triggers the match-action pipeline that has been built. The capsule approach was almost entirely abandoned.

## 2.1.5 Why Active Networking did not commercialize

A summary of three main reasons, drawing on the retrospective by David Wetherall "Retrospective on the Active Network" (2013 keynote at HotNets), Jonathan Smith "Programmable Networks: Selected Challenges in Computer Networking" (ACM Computing Surveys 2013), and the synthesis in Casado's PhD thesis (Stanford, 2007):

**Reason 1: an unbridgeable performance gap.** When Internet backbones reached OC-48 (2.5 Gbps) and OC-192 (10 Gbps) at the end of the 1990s and the start of the 2000s, packet rates rose to 10 to 50 million packets per second per link. JVM-based capsule execution achieved only a few thousand to a few tens of thousands of packets per second. A gap of three to four orders of magnitude could not be closed by software optimization. Only FPGA or custom ASIC could reach line rate, and such hardware was not programmable at the granular level the capsule approach required.

**Reason 2: the security model was not sufficiently sandboxed.** The JVM sandbox was designed for client-side web browsers, where the end user bears the risk if an applet misbehaves. A network node is shared critical infrastructure. A faulty or malicious capsule can affect the traffic of every other user, not just the user who sent the capsule. This trust model had no precedent for being solved successfully. Deploying it in the production multi-AS Internet (where real adversaries exist) became impractical.

**Reason 3: no killer application.** Active Networking could solve some problems, but which problem was important enough that the operator would accept the deployment risk and cost? The use cases commonly cited (per-flow congestion control, adaptive video streaming, distributed caching) all had simpler alternatives: ECN (RFC 3168), adaptive HLS, operator-managed CDN. There was no "killer app" that only Active Networking could solve and that was valuable enough to drive adoption.

The DARPA program officially ended in 2001 when funding ran out. Some groups continued research in a "post-active-networks" direction (Cisco's Active IP Option, MIT's Active Networking 2.0), but none gained commercial traction. The entire idea hibernated for about ten years before re-emerging in the form of P4 in 2014.

> **Key point:** The three reasons Active Networking failed (performance, security, killer app) are direct lessons for SDN. OpenFlow avoided all three by: (a) not executing user code at runtime, only matching pre-compiled match-action rules, keeping line rate; (b) keeping the controller in the operator's trusted domain and not letting external users inject rules; (c) having a clear killer app, namely multi-tenant cloud with OpenStack and Neutron later. Historical lesson: a successful technical innovation needs technology, security model, and business case to line up.

## 2.1.6 Legacy through to P4 (Block VI)

Active Networking hibernated from 2001 until about 2014, when P4 (Programming Protocol-Independent Packet Processors) appeared and reignited the programmable-data-plane idea. The foundational paper "P4: Programming Protocol-Independent Packet Processors" by Pat Bosshart, Dan Daly, Glen Gibb, Martin Izzard, Nick McKeown, Jennifer Rexford, Cole Schlesinger, Dan Talayco, Amin Vahdat, George Varghese, and David Walker (ACM CCR 44(3), July 2014, DOI 10.1145/2656877.2656890) proposed a domain-specific language for designing parser plus match-action pipeline plus deparser.

The core differences of P4 from Active Networking that avoided the three original problems:

**Compile-time instead of runtime.** A P4 program is compiled by the `p4c` compiler into a binary that runs on target hardware (Tofino ASIC, BMv2 simulator, eBPF). There is no runtime interpretation per packet. Performance reaches line rate.

**Operator-controlled deployment.** A P4 program is deployed by the operator as a binary image, similar to a firmware update. Applications cannot inject code per packet. Security stays within the trusted domain.

**A clear business case.** By 2014, when P4 appeared, hyperscalers (Google, Facebook, Microsoft) had clear use cases for a programmable data plane: custom load balancers, INT (In-band Network Telemetry) for visibility, CCA (Congestion Control Assistance). The killer app was no longer hypothetical but a concrete requirement.

Part 6.0 will analyze P4 in detail. For now you only need to note that DARPA's Active Networking did not die outright. The "programmable packet processing" idea was reincarnated with a completely different implementation that inherited the lessons of the 2001 failure.

> **Common misconception:** *"Active Networking is the direct predecessor of SDN and OpenFlow."* False. Active Networking (capsules plus an EE runtime) and SDN (external controller plus match-action) are **two opposite philosophies** in nearly every architectural aspect. Active Networking pushes **intelligence into the packet** (code travels with the data); SDN concentrates intelligence at the controller (the switch stays dumb). OpenFlow 2008 inherited from GSMP, the 4D Project, and Ethane. It did NOT inherit from Active Networking. Only P4 (2014, Block VI) is the reincarnation of the programmable-data-plane idea, and P4 specifically **avoids** the three problems that killed Active Networking (compile-time, operator-controlled, clear business case). Reading these two historical branches as a direct inheritance is a serious genealogical error.

> **Common misconception:** *"Ipsilon's failure means GSMP had a design flaw."* False. Ipsilon failed because ATM lost to Ethernet in the LAN and DC dominance war, not because GSMP was flawed. Evidence: GSMP itself was inherited in principle by Cisco Tag Switching (1996), then standardized by IETF as MPLS (RFC 3031, 2001), and became the dominant WAN and service-provider technology for more than 20 years. A protocol whose specification was reused by three successive generations of technology (Ipsilon to Cisco Tag to MPLS to OpenFlow) cannot be called "flawed by design". Lesson: distinguish product failure (business model, market timing, hardware choice) from protocol failure (design flaw, scalability limit, security hole).
