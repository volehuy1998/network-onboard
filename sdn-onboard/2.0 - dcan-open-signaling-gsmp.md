## Context: why ATM gave rise to the idea of separating control from data

Before Ethernet became the unchallenged standard for campus networks and data centers, the late 1980s and the 1990s were the era that expected Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM) to dominate. ITU-T standardized ATM from 1988 with the goal of replacing traditional TDM for voice, replacing Ethernet for the LAN, and replacing ISDN for broadband, all with a single cell-switching technology that used a fixed 53-byte cell. The ATM Forum (founded in 1991) brought together major manufacturers such as Cisco, Juniper (the predecessor company), Alcatel, and Nortel to promote the technology.

The commercial expectation for ATM was so high that many telecom backbones and a number of large-scale campus networks (including major US and European universities in the mid 1990s) deployed ATM switches at a cost of hundreds of millions of US dollars in dedicated equipment. However, the ATM switch itself had a monolithic architecture similar to the later Ethernet switch: Q.2931 signaling (the protocol that sets up virtual circuits) ran on the CPU inside the switch, tightly coupled to the cell relay hardware, proprietary to each vendor, and hard to extend.

When the Internet exploded in the mid 1990s with HTTP and the World Wide Web, ATM met an unexpected competitor: IP-over-Ethernet was simpler, cheaper, and already had a software ecosystem (Windows, Linux, BSD). ATM started to fade away in the late 1990s, but during that brief window it gave rise to three lines of thinking about programmable networks that SDN later inherited directly: DCAN, Open Signaling, and GSMP. Each line answered the same question ("how do we separate the control function from the forwarding function inside an ATM switch to reduce vendor dependence?") from a different angle.

## 2.0.1 DCAN: Devolved Control of ATM Networks (Cambridge, 1995)

The Cambridge Computer Laboratory research group led by Ian Leslie, with Sean Rooney and Jacobus van der Merwe as the two main researchers, published the DCAN proposal around 1995 to 1997 through a series of Cambridge CL technical reports and presentations at the IEEE Local Computer Networks Conference and the OPENSIG workshop. The name "Devolved Control" was chosen deliberately to contrast with "centralized" or "distributed". "Devolved" means "handed down". In this context it means handing the signaling function from the switch CPU to an external controller.

The DCAN architecture splits the ATM switch into two clear components. The switch hardware keeps only the cell-relay engine: it reads the virtual path identifier and the virtual channel identifier (VPI and VCI) inside each cell, looks up the forwarding table, and sends the cell out the matching port. The control responsibilities, including virtual circuit setup and teardown, Q.2931 signaling, and QoS parameter management, are devolved to a host computer outside the switch, connected to the switch through a dedicated control channel. In a monolithic ATM, the operator had to use a vendor-specific CLI on the switch CPU to create a virtual circuit. With DCAN, the operator wrote software on a regular Unix workstation and sent commands through the DCAN control channel down to the switch.

The Cambridge group promoted three benefits. First, the rate of innovation increases sharply because changing the control logic does not require a vendor firmware update. The Computer Lab could test a new QoS algorithm in a few weeks instead of waiting for an 18 to 24 month vendor release cycle. Second, vendor independence becomes feasible: a DCAN controller can talk to switches from many vendors as long as the vendors implement the same control channel protocol. Third, network-wide policy becomes feasible because the controller sees all the state and can compute a globally optimal solution instead of each switch deciding locally.

DCAN never became a commercial product. The fundamental reason was not technical but commercial: ATM vendors did not want to support an architecture that reduced the value of their switches, and when ATM faded away there was no investment to push DCAN to an industry standard. Conceptually, however, DCAN formalized for the first time a principle that SDN later rediscovered: **the switch is a thin device running the data plane, and the external controller is the entity with authority over policy and decisions**.

> **Key topic:** DCAN did not solve commercial ATM's vendor dependence problem, but it laid the conceptual foundation. When Ethane (part 2.4) and OpenFlow (part 3.1) appeared 10 to 12 years later applying the same principle to Ethernet, the Stanford engineers had a clear academic reference from Cambridge. They did not have to reinvent the model.

## 2.0.2 OPENSIG: a workshop series for an open signaling interface (1995 to 2001)

In parallel with DCAN at Cambridge, Aurel Lazar at Columbia University started the OPENSIG workshop series in 1995-10. The first workshop was held in Cambridge UK (co-located with the IFIP/IEEE International Workshop on OPENSIG), then moved between networking research centers in Europe and the US: Columbia in 1996, Ottawa in 1997, Chicago in 1998, and so on through 2001 when the movement transitioned into the IEEE P1520 standards committee.

Where DCAN was a specific research project, OPENSIG was a broader view of "programmable networks". The goal that Lazar and his colleagues formulated in the foundational paper "Realizing a Foundation for Programmability of ATM Networks with the Binding Architecture" (IEEE JSAC 1998, 16(7), 1214-1227) was to **build a standardized open programming interface that lets every vendor expose network device functionality through an API instead of a proprietary CLI**.

Three themes recurred at the OPENSIG sessions:

The first theme is the **binding architecture**. The idea: split the network function into abstract objects (Switch, Port, Connection, QoS_Profile), give each object a standard interface, and let application software "bind" to specific instances through a naming service. This architecture is similar to CORBA or DCOM in the software industry, and it brings component-based thinking into networking.

The second theme is **multi-granularity abstraction**. Different layers need different APIs: low-level (packet forwarding), mid-level (flow management), and high-level (service composition). OPENSIG proposed clear stratification, with each layer having its own interface and the layer above calling the layer below, similar to the OSI model but applied to the control plane instead of the data plane.

The third theme is the **programmable control plane**. Instead of running fixed OSPF, BGP, or Q.2931, the control plane should be loadable as a program: the operator could write a custom routing protocol, deploy it to the controller, and not touch the switch firmware. This is the distant ancestor of SDN controllers with pluggable applications such as NOX, POX, and Ryu, and even further out, intent-based networking.

The OPENSIG legacy spread in three directions after 2001. IEEE P1520 tried to standardize the programmable networking interface but did not see adoption because the ATM industry was shrinking. OPENSIG researchers migrated to Active Networking (part 2.1), ForCES (part 2.3), and later to 4D, Ethane, and OpenFlow. Some binding architecture concepts reappeared in NETCONF and YANG (part 5.0) when the IETF standardized the YANG data model from 2010 to 2016, with a noted conceptual influence from the OPENSIG heritage.

## 2.0.3 GSMP: General Switch Management Protocol (RFC 3292, 2002-06)

While DCAN and OPENSIG were mostly academic, GSMP took a different path: the startup company Ipsilon Networks (analyzed in detail in part 2.1) developed the General Switch Management Protocol from 1996 as the protocol between their IP router and a commercial ATM switch, with the goal of using the ATM hardware fast-switching for IP traffic. GSMP v1.1 was published by Ipsilon as RFC 1987 (1996-08, Peter Newman, William Edwards, Robert Hinden, Eric Hoffman, Fong Ching Liaw, Tom Lyon, Greg Minshall, informational RFC).

After Ipsilon was acquired by Nokia in 1997 and IP Switching ran into commercial trouble, the IETF formed the General Switch Management Protocol Working Group to continue standardization, no longer tied to the Ipsilon product. After almost five years of work, the result was RFC 3292 "General Switch Management Protocol (GSMP) V3" (Avri Doria, Fiffi Hellstrand, Kenneth Sundell, Tom Worster, 2002-06, standards track), and RFC 3293 (the GSMP Management Information Base) at the same time.

### Three main GSMP v3 message groups

GSMP v3 defines a binary protocol that runs over TCP (port 6068) or AAL5 (for an ATM underlay). Messages fall into three functional groups:

**Switch port management**: the Port Management Message and the Port Configuration Message let the controller read the port state (up or down, link speed, error counts), enable or disable a port, and set physical parameters. This is the lowest layer, equivalent to `ip link show` and `ip link set` in the modern Linux kernel API.

**Connection management**: Add Branch, Delete Branch, Move Branch, Delete All Branches. In ATM vocabulary, a "connection" is a virtual circuit that may be unidirectional or bidirectional; a "branch" is a leg of a virtual circuit when it is multicast. The controller sends an "Add Branch" message to install a forwarding entry, the equivalent of the OpenFlow `FLOW_MOD` with `OFPFC_ADD` later on.

**Statistics collection**: Statistics Request and Response for byte counters, packet counters, and drop counters per port and per connection. The ancestor of OpenFlow Flow Stats Request and of sFlow and NetFlow export later on.

A fourth group, the Adjacency Protocol, manages the handshake and keepalive between the controller and the switch, similar to the OpenFlow Hello, Echo Request, and Echo Reply handshake today.

### The key point: the switch is "dumb" because the controller is the "brain"

In the GSMP architecture, the switch does not make routing or policy decisions on its own after it has been configured. It only executes the entries that the controller installs. If the controller loses connectivity, the switch keeps forwarding according to the existing entries but does not create new ones. This is the same "fail-standalone versus fail-secure" principle that OpenFlow 1.0 later formalized as two separate modes (`OFPCR_FAIL_SECURE` by default and `OFPCR_FAIL_STANDALONE`).

GSMP v3 also defined the master and slave roles between the controller (called the "switch manager") and the switch (called the "switch") clearly: only one master is active at any moment, and if a backup controller exists, there must be an explicit election before it takes over. The same model reappears in OpenFlow 1.2 (part 4.1) with the controller roles `EQUAL`, `MASTER`, and `SLAVE`, plus `generation_id`.

## 2.0.4 GSMP message structure: the ancestor of the OpenFlow 1.0 12-tuple

A side-by-side comparison of message structures between GSMP v3 (2002) and OpenFlow 1.0 (2009) shows clear conceptual continuity over a 7-year gap, despite the two protocols working on completely different data plane technologies (ATM cell versus Ethernet frame).

In GSMP, a "connection entry" contains the basic fields:
- Input Port plus Input VPI/VCI (virtual path and channel identifier)
- Output Port plus Output VPI/VCI
- QoS parameters (peak cell rate, sustained cell rate, maximum burst size)
- Action flags (admit, shape, drop precedence)

In OpenFlow 1.0 (the `OFPMT_MATCH` struct per spec 1.0.0 §A.2.3), a "flow match" contains 12 fields, often called the "12-tuple":
- Ingress port
- Ethernet source, destination, type, VLAN ID, and VLAN priority
- IPv4 source, destination, protocol, and ToS
- TCP/UDP source port and destination port

The relationship is not accidental. Both describe "which packet matches" plus "which action applies". The core difference: GSMP matches by a fixed 8-bit and 16-bit VPI/VCI because the ATM cell header is that small; OpenFlow matches by 12 fields drawn from the Ethernet plus IP plus TCP/UDP headers, totaling about 280 bits. The Ethernet header is wider but also more complex, so OpenFlow needs more match fields to express equivalent granularity.

The second notable point: GSMP has no "priority" between entries because ATM is connection-oriented and each VPI/VCI maps to exactly one connection. OpenFlow needs priority because Ethernet is connectionless: many flow entries can match the same packet (with wildcards), and the controller has to specify the order. This is a necessary innovation when moving from ATM to Ethernet.

The third point: GSMP Action only has a few dozen values (forward, drop, shape, mark in a DSCP-like way), while OpenFlow 1.0 Action has 8 basic ones (`OUTPUT`, `SET_VLAN_VID`, `SET_VLAN_PCP`, `STRIP_VLAN`, `SET_DL_SRC`, `SET_DL_DST`, `SET_NW_SRC`, `SET_NW_DST`) and many more added in later versions. OpenFlow inherits the structured-action approach from GSMP but expands the action set to fit Ethernet's diversity.

> **Key topic:** OpenFlow did not invent the "match plus action plus priority" structure. The structure already existed in GSMP from 2002, adapted to ATM. OpenFlow is the rebirth of the GSMP approach on a more successful data plane technology (Ethernet), with adjustments for the connectionless nature of IP. Knowing this lineage helps you read the OpenFlow specification not as a document from zero, but as the next evolutionary step of a 15-year line of thinking.

## 2.0.5 The shared legacy of the three efforts: three principles inherited by SDN

Putting together DCAN (1995), OPENSIG (1995 to 2001), and GSMP (1996 to 2002), the three principles below were inherited almost intact by SDN:

**Principle 1: an external controller has higher authority than the switch local CPU.** DCAN formalized this for the first time, GSMP implemented it for the first time in a commercial protocol, and OpenFlow generalized it for Ethernet. The architectural consequence: every important policy (routing, access control, QoS) lives at the controller, not on the switch. The switch is allowed to refuse a command only when there is an integrity violation (for example, installing an entry that points to a non-existent port). It is not allowed to "disobey" for any other reason.

**Principle 2: a standardized protocol between the controller and the switch.** GSMP was the first IETF standards-track effort in this direction. OpenFlow continued with the philosophy of "open spec, multi-vendor", and the ONF standardized 1.0 through 1.5.1. This principle is politically important for the industry: it breaks the vendor monopoly on the internal communication of the device and forces vendors to compete at the hardware and silicon layer instead of at the proprietary firmware layer.

**Principle 3: the switch is a thin device by intent in the design.** DCAN called it "devolved", GSMP said "managed by an external entity", and OpenFlow said "dumb forwarding, smart controller". The terms differ but the meaning is the same: the switch should be as simple as possible, and the complexity should be concentrated at the controller. This principle has been challenged in some ways in modern SDN (commercial ASICs hold quite a lot of state and logic), but it remains the design compass: push complex logic to the controller unless there is a pressing performance reason to keep it on the switch.

Forward reference: part 2.4 analyzes Ethane as a concrete demonstration of these three principles applied to enterprise Ethernet in 2007. Part 3.1 analyzes the OpenFlow 1.0 specification as the final and most successful formalization. Part 4.1 analyzes OpenFlow 1.2 with the controller roles `EQUAL`, `MASTER`, `SLAVE` as the HA extension that continues the master and slave model from GSMP.

> **Common misconception:** *"OpenFlow was invented entirely from scratch at Stanford in 2008, with nothing similar before."* False. The model "external controller plus structured match-action plus standards-track protocol" already had all three pillars in GSMP (RFC 3292, 2002) and was tested commercially through Ipsilon since 1996. OpenFlow differs from GSMP in the data plane (Ethernet instead of ATM) and in adding priority for connectionless matching, but the architectural core is a direct inheritance. A reader who studies the OpenFlow 1.0 spec without knowing GSMP will see every concept (match, action, stats, timeout, open spec) "appear out of nowhere", but in fact this is the result of 12 to 15 years of accumulated thinking.

> **Common misconception:** *"DCAN, OPENSIG, and GSMP failed because the idea was wrong."* False. All three failed because **the data plane technology was wrong (ATM)**, not because the control-data separation principle was wrong. When Ethernet switching (gigabit in 1999, 10G in 2006) replaced ATM as the dominant LAN and data center technology, the same principle was successfully reapplied through OpenFlow. The lesson: a technical idea cannot be evaluated only on short-term commercial outcome. You must also check whether the underlying principle survives the technology shift.
