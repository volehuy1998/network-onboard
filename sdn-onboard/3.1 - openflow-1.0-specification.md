## Context: from the CCR paper of April 2008 to the specification of December 31, 2009

Part 3.0 described the CCR paper "OpenFlow: Enabling Innovation in Campus Networks" of April 2008 as a manifesto that called industry and academia to adopt the technology. The CCR paper, however, is not a specification. It is a concept paper with a few examples, not detailed enough for a vendor to build a compliant switch. In the 20 months between the CCR paper and the specification release, the Stanford Clean Slate group worked with HP Labs, NEC, and other industry partners to refine the protocol detail and to make sure the specification was feasible on existing commercial hardware.

Timeline of the pre-1.0 specification evolution:

- **OpenFlow Switch Specification Version 0.8.0**, May 2008. The first draft, circulated only inside Stanford and the industry partners. It defined a preliminary 10-tuple match and the basic message types.
- **Version 0.8.9**, December 2008. A revision that incorporated feedback from the HP ProCurve deployment.
- **Version 0.9.0**, July 2009. A major revision that expanded the match fields to a 12-tuple, added the ENQUEUE action, and refined the timeout semantics.
- **Version 1.0.0**, December 31, 2009. The official release, 42 pages, shepherded by the Stanford Open Networking Laboratory (the precursor of ONF, formed in March 2011).

The history shows that OpenFlow 1.0 was not a specification written in a vacuum. It was the distillation of 18 months of practical deployment experience in the Gates Building at Stanford, plus feedback from early hardware vendors. Every design decision in 1.0 had a "motivation from production" grounding, unlike ATM or ForCES (designed committee-first, deployed later).

## 3.1.1 OpenFlow 1.0 specification published December 31, 2009

### Organizational context

When the 1.0.0 specification was released at the end of 2009, the Open Networking Foundation did not yet exist (ONF was founded 15 months later, on March 21, 2011). Instead, the specification was led by the **Stanford Clean Slate Laboratory Open Networking Laboratory**, effectively a research group rather than a standards body. The original cover page of the specification reads:

> *"OpenFlow Switch Specification, Version 1.0.0 (Wire Protocol 0x01), December 31, 2009. Copyright (c) 2008, 2009 Stanford University. Permission is granted..."*

The specification is 42 pages, comprehensive but not exhaustive. The later OpenFlow 1.3 specification grew to more than 160 pages. Document structure:

- **Section 1**: Introduction and overview
- **Section 2**: Switch components
- **Section 3**: Flow table
- **Section 4**: Secure channel
- **Section 5**: OpenFlow protocol
- **Appendix A**: Binary message format reference

The version identifier on the wire is **0x01** (corresponding to OF 1.0). This number is used in the OFPT_HELLO message for version negotiation. Each later release increased it by one: OF 1.1 = 0x02, OF 1.2 = 0x03, OF 1.3 = 0x04, OF 1.4 = 0x05, OF 1.5 = 0x06.

When ONF was founded in March 2011, Stanford transferred specification IP ownership to ONF through a license agreement. From OF 1.1 onwards, ONF is the official author and maintainer of the specification.

### Archival location

The current authoritative archive of the 1.0.0 specification is hosted by the Open Networking Foundation:

```
https://opennetworking.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/openflow-spec-v1.0.0.pdf
```

A 42-page PDF download. ONF later restructured the website (2014, 2017, 2020). Tools such as the Internet Archive Wayback Machine preserve older links.

## 3.1.2 Wire protocol: TCP 6633 plain, TCP 6653 TLS

### The transport ports

The OpenFlow controller and switch communicate over a TCP connection with two port options.

**TCP port 6633**: plain TCP, no encryption. This is the "historical" port used in the pre-IANA Stanford deployment. Specification 1.0.0 §6.1 says: *"The OpenFlow protocol runs on top of TCP, default TCP port 6633."* This port number was not reserved in the IANA database in 2009. Vendors picked 6633 by Stanford convention.

**TCP port 6653**: TLS-encrypted OpenFlow. On September 10, 2013, IANA officially assigned port 6653 to OpenFlow at the request of ONF (IANA Port Number Registry entry). From then on, the best practice was to use 6653 for TLS and to keep 6633 for plain (legacy compatibility). OF 1.3.1 and later recommend that the default port should be 6653.

There is a historical reason for the two ports. When ONF proposed an IANA assignment, IANA could not reassign 6633 (already de-facto in use) to "OpenFlow" because formally 6633 had been registered for "sshell" (an unrelated protocol). IANA assigned 6653, which was free in the range available in 2013, to OpenFlow. The result was a dual-port situation that lasted for many years. Many deployments still used 6633 because the firmware was not updated, especially OF 1.0 implementations.

### Security perspective

Specification 1.0 §6.1 says clearly: *"The controller and switch MAY use TLS to secure their communications."* That is, TLS is **optional**, not mandatory. This reflects the deployment context of 2009: the Stanford campus network was internal, with no critical adversary threat model. The specification only required TLS for cross-administrative-domain scenarios (for example, a cloud operator deploying a controller for an enterprise customer over the Internet).

Pre-shared certificate authentication is described as follows. Each switch has a private key and a certificate chain. The controller has a CA certificate. Mutual TLS authenticates both sides before any OpenFlow message is exchanged. Hardware vendors (HP, NEC) shipped switches with a pre-loaded self-signed certificate, and the operator replaced it with a production CA certificate at deployment time.

A security issue was raised later: plain TCP 6633 is vulnerable to a MITM attack if the controller and the switch are connected over an untrusted network. In production from 2012 onwards, the strict best practice is TLS-only with annual certificate rotation. Hyperscale deployments (Google B4, Microsoft SWAN) have used TLS since 2013.

Part 5.x and Part 9.10 will discuss the security model in detail with `ovs-pki` and production-grade certificate management.

## 3.1.3 Message types: three groups by direction of travel

Specification 1.0 §5.1-5.4 classifies more than 30 message types into three groups based on direction and initiator.

### Symmetric messages (controller and switch, both can initiate)

- **OFPT_HELLO**: the first handshake after the TCP connect. Both controller and switch send HELLO with the version number (0x01 for OF 1.0). If the version does not match, the connection terminates.
- **OFPT_ECHO_REQUEST / OFPT_ECHO_REPLY**: keepalive. Each side sends an ECHO_REQUEST with an arbitrary 8-byte payload. The other side replies with the same payload in an ECHO_REPLY. The default interval is 5 seconds. Three consecutive misses mean the connection is dead.

### Controller-to-switch messages (initiated by the controller)

- **OFPT_FEATURES_REQUEST / OFPT_FEATURES_REPLY**: the controller queries switch capabilities (datapath ID, port list, supported actions, maximum flow table size). Called after the HELLO handshake.
- **OFPT_SET_CONFIG / OFPT_GET_CONFIG_REQUEST / OFPT_GET_CONFIG_REPLY**: the controller sets or queries switch configuration (`miss_send_len`, fragment handling policy).
- **OFPT_FLOW_MOD**: install, modify, or delete a flow entry. The most important message in OF 1.0. Structure: match fields, priority, action list, command (ADD / MODIFY / MODIFY_STRICT / DELETE / DELETE_STRICT), and flags.
- **OFPT_PACKET_OUT**: the controller injects a packet out of a switch port. Used to send GARP, an LLDP probe, or to forward a packet that was previously sent up via PACKET_IN.
- **OFPT_PORT_MOD**: the controller modifies port configuration (administrative up or down, flood restrictions).
- **OFPT_STATS_REQUEST / OFPT_STATS_REPLY**: query statistics (flow stats, port stats, table stats, aggregate stats).
- **OFPT_BARRIER_REQUEST / OFPT_BARRIER_REPLY**: an ordering primitive. When the controller sends BARRIER_REQUEST, the switch must complete every prior FLOW_MOD before replying with BARRIER_REPLY. This lets the controller synchronize state.

### Asynchronous messages (initiated by the switch)

- **OFPT_PACKET_IN**: the switch sends a packet up to the controller when the packet does not match any entry (the default behavior on no match) or when the matched entry has an OUTPUT:CONTROLLER action. The message includes the packet header and (optionally) the packet body, depending on `miss_send_len`.
- **OFPT_FLOW_REMOVED**: the switch announces that a flow entry has been removed because of `idle_timeout`, `hard_timeout`, or an explicit DELETE. The controller uses this to update its internal state.
- **OFPT_PORT_STATUS**: the switch reports a port state change (link up or down, administrative up or down).
- **OFPT_ERROR**: the switch reports an error in a previous request from the controller. Contains an error type and code so the controller can diagnose.

### Flow message: a simplified example

Scenario: the controller installs a flow entry that forwards a packet from port 1 to port 2.

1. **Connect**: the controller opens a TCP connection to TCP:6633 on the switch.
2. **Hello handshake**:
   - Switch to Controller: OFPT_HELLO (version=0x01)
   - Controller to Switch: OFPT_HELLO (version=0x01)
3. **Features query**:
   - Controller to Switch: OFPT_FEATURES_REQUEST
   - Switch to Controller: OFPT_FEATURES_REPLY (`datapath_id`, 4 ports, `supported_actions` bitmask)
4. **Flow install**:
   - Controller to Switch: OFPT_FLOW_MOD (match `in_port=1`, action OUTPUT:port 2, priority=100, `idle_timeout=60`)
5. **Barrier to confirm**:
   - Controller to Switch: OFPT_BARRIER_REQUEST (`xid=42`)
   - Switch to Controller: OFPT_BARRIER_REPLY (`xid=42`)
   - The controller now knows that the flow has been installed successfully.
6. **Periodic keepalive**:
   - Every 5 seconds, either side sends OFPT_ECHO_REQUEST and the other side replies with OFPT_ECHO_REPLY.

This flow looks like a traditional protocol (handshake, then operation, then keepalive), with details specific to control-plane programming.

## 3.1.4 The 12-tuple match fields

### List of the 12 fields

The struct `ofp_match` per specification 1.0 §A.2.3 (in C notation) defines the 12 match fields:

| # | Field name | Size | Layer | Purpose |
|---|------------|------|-------|---------|
| 1 | `in_port` | 16 bit | L1 | Ingress switch port (1-65279) |
| 2 | `dl_src` | 48 bit | L2 | Ethernet source MAC |
| 3 | `dl_dst` | 48 bit | L2 | Ethernet destination MAC |
| 4 | `dl_type` | 16 bit | L2 | EtherType (0x0800 IPv4, 0x0806 ARP, 0x8847 MPLS unicast) |
| 5 | `dl_vlan` | 12 bit | L2 | VLAN ID (802.1Q) |
| 6 | `dl_vlan_pcp` | 3 bit | L2 | VLAN Priority Code Point |
| 7 | `nw_src` | 32 bit | L3 | IPv4 source address |
| 8 | `nw_dst` | 32 bit | L3 | IPv4 destination address |
| 9 | `nw_proto` | 8 bit | L3 | IP protocol number (6=TCP, 17=UDP, 1=ICMP) |
| 10 | `nw_tos` | 8 bit | L3 | IPv4 ToS byte (DSCP plus ECN) |
| 11 | `tp_src` | 16 bit | L4 | TCP/UDP source port |
| 12 | `tp_dst` | 16 bit | L4 | TCP/UDP destination port |

Total bits: the L1 plus L2 fields are large, the L3 fields contribute four 32-bit and 8-bit values, and the L4 fields contribute two 16-bit values. The total is roughly 280 bits, that is, about 35 bytes of match structure. With ternary CAM (TCAM) hardware, each flow entry consumes about 40 bytes of TCAM (match plus priority plus counters).

### Wildcard mask

Specification 1.0 §5.2 allows per-field wildcarding through a 32-bit `wildcards` bitmask. Each bit in the bitmask corresponds to one field. Setting a bit means "match any value for this field". The bitmask lets a flow entry match a subset of the 12-tuple, not all fields.

For example, a flow entry that matches "every TCP packet to port 80" has:
- `wildcards` set on the bits for `in_port`, `dl_src`, `dl_dst`, `dl_vlan`, `dl_vlan_pcp`, `nw_src`, `nw_dst`, `nw_tos`, and `tp_src`.
- `dl_type = 0x0800` (IPv4).
- `nw_proto = 6` (TCP).
- `tp_dst = 80`.

The wildcard constant `OFPFW_ALL` sets every bit, so the entry matches any packet (the default rule when no specific rule applies).

In addition, `nw_src` and `nw_dst` have a **variable prefix wildcard**: the low 6 bits of the `wildcards` field encode a prefix length (0 to 32 bits). This lets you match a subnet such as `10.0.0.0/8` by setting the prefix length to 8 and `nw_src` to `0x0A000000`. This feature lets ACL-style rules stay compact in the flow table.

### Comparison with GSMP and with OVS NXM

We compared with GSMP in Part 2.0 §2.0.4: GSMP uses a 24-bit ATM VPI/VCI total, while OF 1.0 uses 12 fields across Ethernet, IP, and TCP for 280 bits. The difference is fundamental because ATM is connection-oriented and already has an explicit label, while Ethernet and IP are connectionless and need to match on header fields.

Compare also with **NXM (Nicira Extensible Match)**, the match-field format of Open vSwitch before OF 1.0 was released. OVS developed NXM during 2008-2009 and already had more fields than OF 1.0:

- NX_CT_STATE (connection tracking state)
- NX_REG0 through NX_REG7 (general-purpose registers)
- NX_TUN_ID (tunnel metadata)
- NX_IP_FRAG (fragmentation state)

OF 1.0 only standardized the 12 basic fields. Other NXM fields remained available in OVS through a vendor extension (an OFPT_VENDOR message with the Nicira OUI). OF 1.2 later adopted the NXM-style approach and renamed it OXM (OpenFlow Extensible Match). Part 4.1 will analyze that.

A point to note: OVS 1.1 (2010) already supported most of the NXM fields before the OpenFlow specification formalized them. Operators using OVS had the advantage of being able to use advanced match fields, even when the official OF 1.0 specification did not yet cover them.

## 3.1.5 Actions: 8 base actions plus semantics

### Action list

Specification 1.0 §A.2.6 defines 8 action types.

**OUTPUT**: forward the packet out a specific port. Target options:
- A physical port number (1-65279): forward out a specific switch port.
- Reserved values:
  - `OFPP_IN_PORT` (0xfff8): forward back out the ingress port.
  - `OFPP_NORMAL` (0xfffa): handed to the non-OF legacy pipeline (MAC learning, STP).
  - `OFPP_FLOOD` (0xfffb): flood out every port other than the ingress, respecting STP state.
  - `OFPP_ALL` (0xfffc): send out every port, including the ingress.
  - `OFPP_CONTROLLER` (0xfffd): send the packet up to the controller via OFPT_PACKET_IN.
  - `OFPP_LOCAL` (0xfffe): forward to the local switch stack (the management CPU).
  - `OFPP_TABLE` (0xfff9): re-process from table 0 (rarely used).

**SET_VLAN_VID**: set or add a VLAN tag with a specific VID (12 bits). If the packet has no VLAN tag yet, the tag is added. If it already has one, the VID is overwritten.

**SET_VLAN_PCP**: set the VLAN Priority Code Point (3 bits). If the packet has no VLAN tag yet, the PCP is applied when a tag is added by the next action.

**STRIP_VLAN**: remove the VLAN tag from the packet. Has no effect if the packet has no tag.

**SET_DL_SRC / SET_DL_DST**: overwrite the source or destination Ethernet MAC address.

**SET_NW_SRC / SET_NW_DST**: overwrite the IPv4 source or destination address. The IP checksum is recomputed automatically.

**SET_NW_TOS**: overwrite the IPv4 ToS byte (DSCP plus ECN bits).

**SET_TP_SRC / SET_TP_DST**: overwrite the TCP or UDP source or destination port. The TCP/UDP checksum is recomputed (the L4 header checksum includes the IP pseudo-header, so it must be recomputed when IP fields change).

**ENQUEUE**: enqueue the packet into a specific output queue (for QoS). The queue must be configured first via `OFPQCT_CONFIGURE_QUEUES`. The packet is dropped if the queue is full.

### Ordered execution semantics

Specification 1.0 §3.3 specifies that the action list executes in the order in which the actions appear. For example, an action list of [SET_DL_DST=aa:bb:cc:dd:ee:ff, SET_VLAN_VID=100, OUTPUT=port 2] runs as follows:

1. Overwrite the destination MAC in the packet buffer.
2. Add a VLAN tag with VID=100.
3. Forward out port 2.

If there is no OUTPUT action at the end, the packet is implicitly dropped after the other actions execute. This is a convention to remember: **a flow entry without OUTPUT is equivalent to a DROP**.

The action OUTPUT:CONTROLLER sends the entire packet plus metadata up to the controller via PACKET_IN. If `miss_send_len` (the configured default of 128 bytes) is smaller than the packet size, only the leading header is sent. The rest is buffered in the switch with a `buffer_id`. The controller can then send an OFPT_PACKET_OUT with the `buffer_id` to release the packet back to the switch.

The action OUTPUT:NORMAL hands the packet to the legacy non-OpenFlow pipeline. On a hybrid switch (running both OpenFlow and legacy STP plus MAC learning), OUTPUT:NORMAL allows a fall-back to the legacy behavior. On a pure OpenFlow switch (running OpenFlow only, no legacy), OUTPUT:NORMAL has no effect or drops the packet.

### Practical recipes

**Recipe 1: L2 flood**: action list = [OUTPUT=OFPP_FLOOD]. Flood the packet out every non-ingress port. Used for unknown unicast.

**Recipe 2: L3 routing**: action list = [SET_DL_SRC=gateway_mac, SET_DL_DST=next_hop_mac, OUTPUT=port_to_next_hop]. Rewrite the MACs and forward.

**Recipe 3: VLAN translation**: action list = [STRIP_VLAN, SET_VLAN_VID=new_vid, OUTPUT=port_X]. Pop the old VLAN, push the new VLAN, and forward.

**Recipe 4: Controller diversion**: action list = [OUTPUT=OFPP_CONTROLLER]. Every matching packet is sent up to the controller for a policy decision.

## 3.1.6 Anatomy of a flow entry

### Flow entry structure

Specification 1.0 §3.3 defines a flow entry as containing the following parts.

**Header (match plus priority plus timeouts plus cookie)**:

| Field | Size | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| Match | 40 byte | 12-tuple plus wildcards |
| Priority | 16 bit | 0-65535, higher value matched first |
| Idle timeout | 16 bit | Seconds without a matching packet before eviction |
| Hard timeout | 16 bit | Seconds after install before eviction (regardless of traffic) |
| Cookie | 64 bit | Opaque controller bookkeeping value |
| Flags | 16 bit | SEND_FLOW_REM, CHECK_OVERLAP, EMERG |

**Counters (packet count plus byte count plus duration)**:

Each flow entry has counters that the switch updates every time a packet matches:
- Packet count (64-bit).
- Byte count (64-bit).
- Duration in seconds (64-bit, since install time).

The counters let the controller query statistics via OFPT_STATS_REQUEST. They are used for monitoring and ECMP-aware scheduling.

**Instructions (action list)**:

In OF 1.0, instructions are simply a flat action list executed in order. OF 1.1 and later introduced a richer instruction framework (APPLY_ACTIONS, WRITE_ACTIONS, CLEAR_ACTIONS, GOTO_TABLE, WRITE_METADATA). Part 4.0 analyzes the details.

### Priority and matching semantics

Specification 1.0 §3.4 specifies that the switch matches a packet by finding the flow entry with the highest priority whose match fields fit. If several entries share the same priority, the behavior is implementation-defined (often the first-installed entry wins).

Priority 0 is reserved for the **default rule** (the miss rule), often "send to controller" or "drop". Specific rules should have a priority greater than 0.

**Cross-product explosion problem**: when you want to express "a packet from VLAN X to IP range Y matching ACL rule Z gets action W", you must create one flow entry per combination (X, Y, Z, W). With 10 VLANs times 10 IP ranges times 5 ACL rules, you need 500 entries. With 100 by 100 by 50 you need 500,000 entries, well beyond the capacity of hardware tables (typically 1500 to 4000 TCAM entries). A single flow table does not scale when the policy is complex.

### Timeouts: idle versus hard

**Idle timeout**: evict the entry if no packet matches it during N seconds. Default is 0 (no idle timeout, the entry persists until an explicit DELETE).

**Hard timeout**: evict the entry N seconds after install, regardless of traffic. Default is 0 (no hard timeout).

Typically the controller sets both: `idle_timeout=60` (evict an inactive flow) and `hard_timeout=300` (force re-evaluation every 5 minutes). The `OFPFF_SEND_FLOW_REM` flag lets the switch notify the controller via OFPT_FLOW_REMOVED when an eviction happens, so the controller can track flow lifecycle.

### Cookie: bookkeeping on the controller side

The cookie is a 64-bit opaque value. It does not affect matching or forwarding. The controller uses the cookie to:

- **Trace flow origin**: the cookie is a hash of (`application_id`, `tenant_id`, `rule_id`). The controller can then query flows with a specific cookie to find the flows installed by application X.
- **Bulk operations**: a DELETE with a cookie filter removes every flow with a matching cookie prefix (for example, delete every flow installed by "tenant 42").
- **Cache invalidation**: when the policy changes, the controller bumps the cookie prefix, installs new flows, and deletes old flows by cookie.

OVS and most production deployments use cookies extensively. The Ryu and ONOS frameworks have helper functions to generate structured cookies.

## 3.1.7 Single-table limit and cross-product explosion

### The problem

OF 1.0 has only **one** flow table (table 0). Every forwarding decision must be expressed in this single table.

When the policy has several orthogonal dimensions, the number of flow entries grows multiplicatively. A data center use case example:

- 20 tenants (VLANs) by 100 IP subnets per tenant by 10 ACL rules by 5 QoS classes equals 100,000 entries.

With TCAM hardware of 2,000 to 4,000 entries, this scenario does not fit. The operator must:

- Compromise: reduce granularity (fewer ACL rules), reduce scale (fewer tenants), trading features against scale.
- Use controller logic to install entries incrementally, only when traffic demands them, instead of pre-computing every combination.
- Use a software fallback: forward packets that miss the hardware table through a software path (slower, not line-rate).

### Workarounds during 2010-2011

**OVS `resubmit` action** (a Nicira vendor extension): lets one flow entry trigger a second lookup in the same table or a different table. You can sequence match and action steps without doing the cross-product. This is effectively a multi-table simulation on top of a single-table specification.

```
# OVS flow syntax (1.0 era with NXM extension)
table=0, priority=100, dl_vlan=100 actions=resubmit(,1)  # route traffic to "virtual table" 1
table=0, priority=100, dl_vlan=200 actions=resubmit(,2)  # virtual table 2
```

The NXM extension is not standard OF 1.0. It only works between OVS and OVS, or between OVS and an NXM-aware switch.

**Controller-side logic**: the controller compiles high-level rules into flat flow entries by pre-computing the cross-product. The controller is responsible for managing rule explosion, while the switch only executes. This costs controller CPU and memory but works with switches that only support OF 1.0.

**Hybrid flows**: high-priority entries for specific cases (hot flows), low-priority entries for the general case (defaults). When a packet matches a hot flow, the default is skipped. This approach scales better than full cross-product but the policy becomes hard to reason about.

### Motivation for OF 1.1 multi-table

OF 1.1 (February 28, 2011) introduced the multi-table pipeline. The switch can have several flow tables (table 0 to table 1 to and so on), and each table matches one dimension. The cross-product problem is resolved by nesting tables instead of flattening into one table.

Example: table 0 matches VLAN (20 entries), table 1 matches IP subnet (100 entries), table 2 matches ACL (10 entries), table 3 matches QoS (5 entries). The total is 20 plus 100 plus 10 plus 5, that is, 135 entries instead of 100,000 cross-product entries. Linear instead of multiplicative scaling.

Part 4.0 (OpenFlow 1.1) analyzes the multi-table pipeline, the group table, and MPLS support in detail. This is the reason the industry quickly moved from OF 1.0 to OF 1.1 and later as soon as the new specification was released.

> **Key point:** the single flow table of OF 1.0 was an architectural limitation that the industry recognized very quickly after release. The multi-table pipeline of OF 1.1 was a natural evolution. The actual migration from 1.0 to 1.1 took 2 to 3 years because hardware vendors had to update firmware, and many OF 1.0 deployments were locked to a specific feature set. The OVS vendor extension (NXM) provided a bridge for OVS-based deployments. Hardware deployments were often stuck on OF 1.0 for some time.
