## Context: two competing philosophies in commercial SDN

When OpenFlow moved out of the lab (2011 to 2013), two camps formed clearly in the industry. The **open ecosystem** camp believed that SDN means open protocols (OpenFlow, OVSDB, NETCONF) and open software (ODL, ONOS) running on whitebox hardware, similar to the way Linux unseated proprietary UNIX vendors in the 1990s and 2000s. The **vendor ecosystem** camp, led by Cisco, argued the opposite: SDN is not about protocols, it is about policy abstraction and operational simplicity. That is best achieved when the controller and the hardware are designed together (tight integration), not through a shared open protocol (the lowest common denominator).

The Cisco view has its own logic. Each silicon vendor (Broadcom, Barefoot, Cisco CloudScale, Marvell) has a different feature set, and OpenFlow has to pick the smallest common denominator that every vendor's switch supports. As a result, OpenFlow 1.3 cannot use many advanced features of modern silicon (conntrack, encryption offload, in-band telemetry). Cisco argues that a closed architecture, the Cisco controller plus Cisco switches plus Cisco silicon, lets a deployment use 100 percent of the hardware capability, which OpenFlow cannot.

This part surveys the four largest vendor controllers: Cisco ACI (APIC), Juniper Contrail, VMware NSX, and Arista CloudVision. Each vendor represents a slightly different design philosophy. Understanding the differences helps an engineer judge when a vendor controller is the right choice and when an open ecosystem (OVN, ONOS) is more appropriate.

## 7.3.1 Cisco ACI and APIC

Cisco ACI (Application Centric Infrastructure) was announced in November 2013 alongside the Nexus 9000 series switches. The full product set has three components: (a) **APIC** (Application Policy Infrastructure Controller), a three-node controller cluster that manages the entire fabric. (b) **Nexus 9000 leaf-spine** switches with the Cisco CloudScale ASIC, supporting iVXLAN (a Cisco VXLAN extension). (c) **OpFlex protocol**, a proprietary protocol between APIC and the switches used to push policy and to receive telemetry.

**The ACI policy model** is the most important technical contribution. Instead of describing the network with VLANs, subnets, and ACLs, the abstractions of traditional networking, ACI lets the administrator describe the application:

- **Tenant**: the highest administrative boundary, typically corresponding to one customer or one business unit.
- **Application Network Profile (ANP)**: a group of related endpoints that belong to the same application (for example, a three-tier web application with web tier, app tier, and database tier).
- **Endpoint Group (EPG)**: a set of VMs or containers that share the same policy. Instead of attaching IPs to ACLs, the administrator places VMs into an EPG and the EPG carries the policy.
- **Contract**: describes the communication policy between EPGs. The "web" EPG can speak HTTPS to the "app" EPG, the "app" EPG can speak SQL to the "db" EPG, and other communications are blocked by default.

The model is not new conceptually (the Ethane group proposed something similar in 2007), but ACI is the first commercial implementation at data-center scale. The operations team does not need to write ACLs line by line. They only declare the relationships between EPGs through Contracts, and the controller compiles these into tens of thousands of flow rules on the switches.

> **Key point:** The important difference between ACI and OpenFlow or OVN is that **the controller does not program flow entries**. The controller pushes policy (intent) to the switches through OpFlex, and the Cisco silicon decides forwarding on its own (routing tables, MAC learning, ECMP), the same way a traditional switch does. This is the opposite of OpenFlow, where the controller controls every flow entry. The result: ACI scales better with a large number of switches (the hardware forwards on its own, no controller is needed for every packet), but it loses the programmatic flexibility OpenFlow provides.

**Real-world deployment.** ACI is the most widely deployed vendor SDN in enterprise data centers from 2015 to 2022. Large customers include VISA, J.P. Morgan, many United States federal agencies, and large hospitals (Cleveland Clinic, Mayo Clinic). A 2022 estimate from Cisco: more than 50,000 ACI fabrics deployed worldwide, among the most common SDN fabrics.

**Limitations and critique.** ACI requires the full Cisco stack: Nexus 9000 switches, APIC, and the ACI license. The cost is high. A typical 24-switch ACI fabric starts at about 500,000 USD for hardware and base license alone, before advanced features (multi-site, Kubernetes integration). OpFlex has not been implemented at production quality by other switch vendors, which means choosing ACI prevents mixing in other vendor switches. This is clear lock-in, the criticism the open-ecosystem camp pushes hardest.

## 7.3.2 Juniper Contrail and Tungsten Fabric

Juniper Contrail started as Contrail Systems, a startup founded in 2012 by Ankur Singla, Harshad Nakil, and Kireeti Kompella, focused on SDN for service providers. Juniper acquired Contrail Systems in December 2012 for an estimated 176 million USD, and integrated the product into the Juniper portfolio from 2013.

In September 2014, Juniper announced **the open-sourcing of all Contrail** under the name **OpenContrail** with the Apache 2.0 license, a surprising decision for a traditional networking vendor. The business logic: Juniper bet that open-sourcing would drive broader adoption, and Juniper could earn revenue through enterprise support and hardware (MX-series routers, QFX-series switches).

In 2018, OpenContrail moved its governance to Linux Foundation Networking (LFN) and was renamed **Tungsten Fabric** to separate the brand from Juniper's commercial product. Juniper still ships Contrail Enterprise (a product fork of Tungsten Fabric) as the version that Juniper supports and tests thoroughly.

**Tungsten Fabric architecture.** The fundamental difference from ACI is the agent-based architecture. Each compute node runs a process named **vRouter agent** that replaces the standard virtual switch (Linux bridge or OVS). The vRouter agent talks to the controller through two protocols: **XMPP** (Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol) for session-state exchange, and **BGP EVPN** for control-plane routing information.

The choice of BGP EVPN was strategic. BGP is the standard routing protocol every router vendor supports, and EVPN (Ethernet VPN, RFC 7432) is a BGP extension that carries MAC and IP information. Tungsten Fabric uses BGP EVPN at the control plane and a VXLAN overlay at the data plane. This lets the Tungsten Fabric controller peer BGP with Juniper, Cisco, or Nokia routers to exchange routes. The result: Tungsten Fabric integrates into existing service-provider networks without requiring all hardware to be replaced.

**Multi-hypervisor and container support.** Another strength of Tungsten Fabric is concurrent support for multiple hypervisors (KVM, ESXi, Hyper-V) and a CNI plugin for Kubernetes. Many cloud and service providers choose Tungsten Fabric because they have a mixed environment (part legacy VMware, part OpenStack KVM, part Kubernetes), and one controller can manage all of it.

**Movement in 2022 to 2024.** VMware acquired Juniper (the deal closed in July 2024), creating uncertainty about the future of Contrail. VMware also owns NSX, so the same company now ships two products that compete directly. The official roadmap had not been published as of 2026-04, but several analysts predict Contrail will be folded into NSX or be reach end of life within three to five years.

## 7.3.3 VMware NSX-T: from NVP to today

Part 5.1 already covered Nicira NVP (2011) and the VMware acquisition in 2012 for 1.26 billion USD. This section focuses only on the development path of NSX after the acquisition, without repeating the founding history of Nicira.

**NSX-V and NSX-T: two parallel codebases, 2016 to 2021.** In 2013, VMware announced its first SDN product, **NSX for vSphere (NSX-V)**, essentially a rewrite of Nicira NVP for tight integration with vSphere ESXi. NSX-V was tied to ESXi: it ran only on the VMware hypervisor and did not support KVM, Hyper-V, or containers.

In 2016, VMware announced **NSX-T** (T for Transformers), with the goal of expanding beyond ESXi. NSX-T supports KVM, and later added Kubernetes and public clouds (AWS, Azure, GCP). The two codebases (NSX-V and NSX-T) ran in parallel for nearly five years, with separate engineering teams and roadmaps. This was a heavy technical burden. VMware maintained two versions, customers had to choose between them, and migration between the two was not supported.

**NSX 4.0: unified in 2022.** In 2022, VMware announced NSX 4.0, a single unified version, essentially NSX-T extended to replace NSX-V. NSX-V customers were advised to migrate to NSX 4.0. NSX-V officially reached end of life in January 2024. From that point, "NSX" in documentation refers by default to NSX 4.0 and later, with no V or T distinction.

**NSX 4.0 architecture.** It keeps the hypervisor-edge philosophy from NVP and Nicira: a VXLAN or Geneve overlay between hypervisors, without touching the physical underlay network. The **distributed firewall** runs at the hypervisor layer, with policy enforced right at each VM, with no need to route through a centralized firewall. **NSX Edge** provides north-south connectivity (NAT, load balancer, VPN) through a cluster of VMs or bare-metal edge nodes.

**Multi-cloud native integration.** NSX's distinctive strength compared with ACI and Contrail is integration with public clouds. NSX Advanced Threat Prevention runs natively on AWS VPC and Azure VNet, letting a large enterprise apply the same security policy on its on-prem data center and in public cloud. **Antrea CNI** is VMware's product to extend NSX into Kubernetes, based on the OVS data plane and inheriting the policy model from NSX.

## 7.3.4 Arista CloudVision: a different approach

Arista Networks, a switch vendor founded in 2004 by Andy Bechtolsheim and Ken Duda (formerly of Sun Microsystems), takes an SDN approach quite different from the three vendors above. Arista refused to build an SDN controller in the traditional model (a centralized controller that pushes flow rules or policy to the switches), and instead built **CloudVision**, a platform for state management and telemetry.

**The "network state streaming" philosophy.** Arista argues that the real problem in network operations is not the lack of a centralized controller, but the lack of real-time visibility into network state. Arista switches run EOS (Extensible Operating System), a Linux-based operating system in which all configuration and state are stored in a state database called Sysdb. The CloudVision agent on each switch streams Sysdb changes in real time (over gRPC and OpenConfig telemetry) to the CloudVision server, where the entire network state is updated continuously.

**CloudVision eXchange (CVX)** is an agent-to-agent component. It lets Arista switches share state with each other directly, without going through a central controller. For example, when a VM moves between two hosts, CVX lets the leaf switch on the destination host receive a new MAC notification from the leaf switch on the source host and update its forwarding table immediately, without the controller acting as an intermediary.

**API-driven with OpenConfig and gNMI.** Arista pushes two multi-vendor standards: **OpenConfig** (an open YANG data model proposed by AT&T, Google, and Facebook in 2015) and **gNMI** (gRPC Network Management Interface, the transport for OpenConfig). An administrator can configure an Arista switch through gNMI rather than the CLI, and multi-vendor management tools (for example, Kentik, Network to Code) work with Arista through OpenConfig.

**Comparing the philosophies.** Cisco ACI believes in centralized control with intent policy. Juniper Contrail believes in standard protocols (BGP EVPN) running between agent and controller. VMware NSX believes in a self-contained hypervisor-edge overlay. Arista believes in **distributed state streaming**: no controller, every switch knows the entire network state through peer-to-peer exchange, and the central platform exists only for observability and orchestration, not for control. Four different philosophies, and each has a coherent technical case in its own context.

## 7.3.5 Trade-off: open versus vendor

Choosing a vendor controller or an open controller is one of the most important architectural decisions for a new data center. The trade-off analysis needs to consider three main dimensions:

**Lock-in cost and switching cost.** A vendor controller requires switches from the same vendor. ACI runs only with Nexus 9000. NSX runs optimally only with vSphere. Contrail runs with any hypervisor, but enterprise support requires Juniper hardware. Once deployed, the cost of switching to a different vendor is the entire hardware cost plus operations-team retraining plus data migration plus downtime during the transition. With a 500-or-more switch fabric, the switching cost can reach tens of millions of USD. Open controllers (OVN, ONOS) do not have this lock-in. They can mix switches from many vendors, or move completely between ONOS and OVN with the cost mostly being engineer time.

**Feature-development pace.** A vendor controller has a large engineering team (a few hundred full-time engineers) and ships new features every six months, with quality already validated through thorough QA. Open controllers depend on community and part-time contributing teams, with uneven release cycles (OVN about every six months, ONOS about every six months, ODL now in maintenance only). For niche features (for example, integrating with a specific hardware firewall or supporting a new public cloud), vendor controllers usually support them first.

**Support lifecycle and operations-team risk.** A vendor offers 24-by-7 enterprise support. When a production incident hits, there is a named owner and a clear SLA (4-hour response for P1 on Cisco TAC Gold). An open controller requires an operations team with deep skills, who can debug and patch on their own. If an organization does not have in-house engineers with three or more years of SDN experience, choosing an open controller carries high risk when an incident occurs outside business hours.

*Table 7-4: five-year TCO comparison, 50 switches versus 500 switches*

| Cost | Vendor (ACI) 50 switches | Open (OVN) 50 switches | Vendor (ACI) 500 switches | Open (OVN) 500 switches |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Switch hardware | 750k USD (Nexus 9000) | 250k USD (whitebox) | 7.5M USD | 2.5M USD |
| Controller and license | 300k USD | 0 USD (free) | 1.2M USD | 0 USD |
| Five-year support | 200k USD | 0 USD or 300k USD (third party) | 800k USD | 0 USD or 600k USD |
| Operations team training | 50k USD | 150k USD | 80k USD | 250k USD |
| In-house dedicated engineers | Not required | 1 to 2 FTE (1M USD over 5 years) | 2 FTE (2M USD over 5 years) | 5 FTE (5M USD over 5 years) |
| **Total** | **about 1.3M USD** | **about 1.7M USD** | **about 11.6M USD** | **about 8.4M USD** |

At small scale (50 switches), vendor wins by a small margin because the in-house engineering cost for an open controller exceeds the hardware savings. At large scale (500 switches), open wins meaningfully because hardware and license savings grow linearly while engineer cost grows more slowly (5 FTE, not 10 FTE). This is why hyperscalers (Google, Facebook, Microsoft Azure) all choose open or in-house, while smaller enterprises (500 to 2,000 employees) typically choose a vendor.

> **Common misconception:** "An open-source controller is always cheaper than a vendor controller."
>
> **Reality:** Open source is free in license but not free in total cost of ownership. At small scale, the cost of in-house engineers to run an open controller often exceeds the license savings. Open is only truly cheaper at a scale large enough that engineering cost is amortized across many switches, typically from 200 switches and up. Below that threshold, a vendor controller with enterprise support can be the more reasonable TCO choice.

## Key points to remember

First, vendor controllers (ACI, Contrail, NSX, CloudVision) and open controllers (OVN, ONOS, ODL) represent two design philosophies, not "one is better than the other". Vendors prioritize tight integration and operational simplicity. Open prioritizes flexibility and vendor neutrality. The right choice depends on the organization's scale, budget, and engineering capability.

Second, Cisco ACI laid the foundation for intent-based networking with the policy abstraction "Application Network Profile + EPG + Contract". The model was later replicated in NSX, in Contrail, and indirectly influenced Kubernetes NetworkPolicy. The contribution of ACI goes beyond a specific product. It shaped how the industry thinks about SDN policy for a decade.

Third, the five-year TCO analysis shows that the open-versus-vendor trade-off is not linear in scale. At small scale (under 200 switches), vendor often wins because of engineer cost savings. At large scale (500 or more switches), open wins because of hardware and license savings. The crossover threshold depends on the local labor market and the specific cost per switch, but the qualitative pattern is consistent globally.
