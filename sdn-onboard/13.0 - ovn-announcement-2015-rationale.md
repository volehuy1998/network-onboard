## 13.0.1 The 13 January 2015 announcement, four-author context

OVN (Open Virtual Network) was announced on 13 January 2015 on the Network Heresy blog. Title: *"OVN: Bringing Native Virtual Networking to OVS"*. This moment came eight years after OVS first appeared (2007, Part 9.0) and three years after VMware acquired Nicira (July 2012, Part 5.1). The announcement was short, but it marked a meaningful shift in the open-source SDN ecosystem.

### The four authors and their roles

**Justin Pettit** (VMware, ex-Nicira co-founder). Pettit was one of the three Nicira founders in 2007 (with Casado and McKeown from Stanford) and the primary OVS architect from 2007 to 2015. For OVN, Pettit designed the 3-tier architecture and the pipeline semantics. He played the leading technical role.

**Ben Pfaff** (VMware, NSDI 2015 paper lead). Pfaff was the main author of "The Design and Implementation of Open vSwitch" (NSDI 2015 best paper, Part 9.0). For OVN, Pfaff wrote the `ovn-northd` compiler and the distributed control plane semantics. He played the second technical role.

**Chris Wright** (Red Hat VP, later Red Hat CTO from 2019). Wright was the primary VMware and Red Hat liaison. He represented Red Hat's operational and product perspective. His role was strategic: ensuring that OVN fit the Red Hat OpenStack Platform (RHOSP) and OpenShift roadmap.

**Madhu Venugopal** (Red Hat, OpenStack Neutron and Docker libnetwork contributor). Venugopal brought the OpenStack and container networking perspective. He required that OVN remain compatible with the Neutron ML2 driver (released 2017) and with the Kubernetes CNI (later, in 2019).

> **Key point:** The VMware and Red Hat collaboration of 2015 was unusual. VMware had just acquired Nicira for 1.26 billion US dollars in 2012. The straightforward strategic choice would have been to keep OVN proprietary as part of the NSX roadmap. Instead, Pfaff and Pettit (employed by VMware) released OVN under Apache 2.0 with Red Hat co-authors. This decision reflected the NSDI 2015 philosophy carried over from the Nicira heritage: *"community first, product second"*. The result is that OVN became the foundation for OpenStack and OpenShift (Red Hat flagship products). The revenue Red Hat earned on top of OVN exceeded any revenue VMware could have captured by keeping OVN closed.

### Key message of the announcement

The blog post emphasized that OVN was the **"native"** virtual networking layer for OVS. It was built in, not an add-on. Before OVN, OVS needed control from a third party:

- **OpenStack Neutron with ML2 and the linuxbridge or OVS agent.** A Python-heavy orchestration layer translated the Neutron API into OVS commands. It was complex and slow (5 to 30 seconds to provision) and exhibited race conditions at scale.
- **OpenDaylight (ODL) with the OVSDB plugin.** A monolithic Java and Karaf controller. It was too heavy for small deployments and saw little production use.
- **Juniper Contrail or OpenContrail.** Centred on BGP EVPN and a vRouter data plane. It was specific to the Juniper ecosystem.
- **NSX (VMware proprietary).** Closed source, running only on vSphere or KVM enterprise.

There was no native, open-source option purpose-built for OVS. OVN filled that gap.

## 13.0.2 Design motivation: three technical, two commercial

### Technical motivations

**Technical 1: OVS had a strong data plane but no native control plane.**
From 2007 to 2015, OVS was a general-purpose programmable virtual switch. OpenFlow and OVSDB allowed external control, but the controller logic had to be implemented by the user. A developer had to write thousands of lines of Python or Go code to translate "create a virtual network for this tenant" into the corresponding flow rules, tunnel setup, ARP responder, and DHCP reply. OVN provides this logical layer out of the box.

**Technical 2: A distributed control plane native to OVS.**
NSX (from the Nicira heritage) used a centralized controller. The OpenStack Neutron agent was distributed but fragile under race conditions. OVN was designed from the start with a different model: a centralized NBDB holding configuration intent, a single `ovn-northd` instance compiling that intent, an SBDB replicated by Raft, and an `ovn-controller` running per chassis to apply flows locally. This model scales to over a thousand chassis (tested with OVN 24.03) without requiring cross-chassis consensus for data plane decisions.

**Technical 3: A logical abstraction layer separated from the physical underlay.**
The user thinks in terms of `Logical_Switch`, `Logical_Router`, `ACL`, `Load_Balancer`, and `NAT`. The user does not think about flow table numbers, OpenFlow match fields, tunnel encapsulation, or ARP responder implementation. OVN compiles the logical intent into thousands of physical flow rules automatically. This separation is similar to how a C compiler separates the programmer from assembly.

### Commercial motivations

**Commercial 1: Red Hat needed an alternative to Neutron ML2 with linuxbridge.**
RHOSP from 2014 to 2016 had performance and scale issues with Neutron ML2 plus linuxbridge plus the router, DHCP, and metadata agents. Provisioning a tenant took more than 30 seconds. At a scale of 1,000 or more tenants, race conditions appeared frequently. Red Hat needed a new architectural option. Rather than build from zero, Red Hat funded and co-developed OVN with the Nicira team. The result: OVN became the default in RHOSP 16 (2020).

**Commercial 2: VMware wanted OVN as a reference implementation for NSX-T extensions.**
VMware NSX is proprietary and locked to ESXi with an enterprise license. An open-source OVN let VMware reach the developer and operator community beyond the enterprise tier. NSX-T from version 2.5 onward has concepts equivalent to OVN (logical switch, logical router, distributed firewall). A community familiar with OVN could transition to NSX-T for production more easily. The strategy resembled Microsoft's free evaluation license for Windows NT.

> **Common misconception:** *"OVN appeared because OpenFlow 'failed' to scale."*
>
> **Reality:** OpenFlow did not fail. OpenFlow is still the main southbound protocol for OVS and OVN compiles down to OpenFlow. The actual problem was that **a control plane on top of OpenFlow had no logical abstraction language**. A developer had to write each flow rule manually. OVN added the logical abstraction layer (NBDB and SBDB) that OpenFlow itself does not provide. OVN does not replace OpenFlow; OVN uses OpenFlow as its southbound. The distinction between "OpenFlow lost traction as a direct controller protocol" versus "OpenFlow succeeded as a southbound for OVN to compile down to" is important. See Part 20.6 §20.6.4 "Hypervisor overlays winning, 2013 to 2017" for the detailed analysis.

## 13.0.3 The 3-tier architecture, the core contribution

Compass Ch 17: *"OVN sits above OVS as a logical networking controller. Its architecture is three tiers."*

```
 +------------------------------------+
 | Tier 1: ovn-nbctl                  |  <- admin or orchestrator
 |         (writes logical intent)    |
 +---------------+--------------------+
                 | JSON-RPC (OVSDB wire protocol)
 +---------------v--------------------+
 | Tier 2: Northbound DB (NB)         |  <- intent storage (Raft 3-node)
 |    + ovn-northd (translator)       |  <- compile NB to SB
 |    -> Southbound DB (SB)           |  <- computed logical flows
 +---------------+--------------------+
                 | JSON-RPC (subscribe Logical_Flow and Port_Binding)
 +---------------v--------------------+
 | Tier 3: ovn-controller (per host)  |  <- per-chassis agent
 |         -> OpenFlow rules -> br-int|  <- physical application
 +------------------------------------+
```

### Tier 1: configuration intent (NBDB)

The administrator (or an orchestrator such as Neutron or Kubernetes) writes configuration intent through `ovn-nbctl`:

```bash
$ ovn-nbctl ls-add ls-tenant-web
$ ovn-nbctl lsp-add ls-tenant-web lsp-web-01 \
     -- lsp-set-addresses lsp-web-01 "aa:bb:cc:00:00:01 10.0.0.10"
$ ovn-nbctl acl-add ls-tenant-web from-lport 1000 \
     'inport=="lsp-web-01" && tcp.dst==80' allow-related
```

Main NBDB tables:
- `Logical_Switch` (L2 virtual switch).
- `Logical_Router` (L3 virtual router).
- `Logical_Switch_Port` (LSP, an endpoint such as tap, vif, localnet, and so on).
- `Logical_Router_Port` (LRP, an interface between an LS and an LR).
- `ACL` (stateful firewall rule, usually combined with conntrack).
- `NAT` (SNAT and DNAT).
- `Load_Balancer` (VIP to backend pool).
- `Port_Group` (a group of LSPs used to scale ACLs).

### Tier 2: translation (`ovn-northd` and SBDB)

`ovn-northd` (a single singleton process) subscribes to NBDB, computes the logical flows, and writes SBDB. The compilation steps are detailed in Part 13.8:

1. Read all NBDB tables.
2. Generate a logical flow rule for each stage of the ingress pipeline (27 stages) and the egress pipeline (10 stages). These stages are collectively called the OVN "pipeline stages".
3. Allocate a `tunnel_key` for each `Logical_Switch` and `Logical_Router`.
4. Write `Logical_Flow`, `Datapath_Binding`, and `Port_Binding` entries into SBDB.

Main SBDB tables:
- `Chassis` (each hypervisor registers with its encap type and IP).
- `Port_Binding` (binds an LSP to a chassis. At v22.03.8 the `controller/binding.c:915` `get_lport_type()` enumerates 12 types: vif, container, patch, chassisredirect, l3gateway, localnet, localport, l2gateway, virtual, external, remote, vtep).
- `Logical_Flow` (computed flow rule with pipeline = ingress or egress, plus table_id, priority, match, and action).
- `MAC_Binding` (IP to MAC mapping cached from ARP and ND).
- `Datapath_Binding` (logical switch or router with a `tunnel_key`).

### Tier 3: data plane (`ovn-controller` per chassis)

Each chassis runs one `ovn-controller` process. It subscribes to SBDB (the `Logical_Flow` and `Port_Binding` rows relevant to the local chassis). It compiles logical flows into OpenFlow rules and installs them on `br-int` (Part 13.4).

`ovn-controller` also:
- Monitors `br-int` tap ports tagged with `iface-id` and binds the corresponding `Port_Binding`.
- Creates tunnel ports to remote chassis.
- Installs ARP responder flows (Part 18.0).
- Installs FDB flows (Part 17.0) and `MAC_Binding` resolution rules.

### Strengths of the 3-tier model

**Separation of concerns.** An orchestrator developer only writes to NBDB (logical intent). `ovn-northd` handles complex compilation. `ovn-controller` handles physical application. Each tier has a clear responsibility and can be debugged independently.

**Scale target.** A cluster with a 3-node NBDB, a 3-node SBDB, and one `ovn-northd` can drive between 100 and 1,000 chassis. Raft consensus on SBDB ensures consistency under chassis churn.

**Evolution path.** A change in compile logic only updates `ovn-northd`. A change in apply logic only updates `ovn-controller`. A data model change updates the NBDB schema and runs a migration. None of this requires a coordinated restart of the whole system.

## 13.0.4 Comparing four alternatives, when to choose OVN

| Dimension | OVN | VMware NSX | Juniper Contrail | OpenDaylight (ODL) | Pure OVS plus custom |
|-----------|-----|-----------|------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **License** | Apache 2.0 open | Proprietary enterprise | Apache 2.0 (Tungsten Fabric) | Eclipse Public | Apache 2.0 OVS plus DIY code |
| **Orchestrator integration** | Neutron ML2 plus K8s CNI plus bare-metal | ESXi plus KVM plus NSX-T Manager | OpenStack plus K8s plus bare-metal | Multi-protocol umbrella | DIY per orchestrator |
| **Scale target** | Over 1,000 chassis tested | Over 1,000 ESXi hypervisors (VMware production) | 500 to 1,000 compute nodes | 100 to 500 (JVM limit) | Varies, DIY |
| **Data plane** | OVS datapath | OVS proprietary fork | vRouter (userspace) | Any (OpenFlow agnostic) | OVS datapath |
| **Control plane** | Distributed (`ovn-controller` per chassis) | Centralized (NSX Manager cluster) | Distributed (vRouter agent plus BGP EVPN controller) | Monolithic (ODL cluster) | DIY |
| **Underlay protocol** | Geneve (default), VXLAN legacy | Geneve plus STT | BGP EVPN over VXLAN | Flexible | DIY |
| **Operational status** | Active development since 2015, quarterly releases | Enterprise support tiers | Juniper support | Maintenance only since 2023 | Not applicable |
| **Use case sweet spot** | OpenStack plus K8s plus standalone | ESXi-heavy enterprise | Juniper MX plus K8s telco | Research and demo | Lab and POC |

### When to choose OVN

**Choose standalone OVN when:**
- A telco deployment needs a distributed control plane that is not vendor locked.
- A K8s cluster has high tenant scale (over 100 namespaces) and needs precise network policy.
- A custom orchestrator is being built from scratch (not using OpenStack or K8s).

**Choose OpenStack ML2-OVN when:**
- An OpenStack deployment already exists and needs to migrate from ML2-linuxbridge to ML2-OVN.
- The platform is Red Hat RHOSP 16 or higher, or Canonical Ubuntu Cloud Archive.
- The goal is to reduce Neutron agent complexity (no need for `neutron-dhcp-agent`, `neutron-l3-agent`, or `neutron-metadata-agent`).

**Choose OVN-Kubernetes CNI when:**
- The platform is OpenShift 4 or higher (default CNI from 4.12 onward).
- The K8s workload needs EgressIP, EgressFirewall, and HybridOverlay (a mix of Linux and Windows workloads).
- A multi-cluster mesh is required, using OVN-IC (inter-cluster).

### When NOT to choose OVN

**Choose NSX-T when there is an existing ESXi enterprise investment and end-to-end vendor support is required.**

**Choose Cilium eBPF when the K8s workload needs sidecar-less service mesh, XDP DDoS protection, or HTTP-layer policy (L7).** OVN handles L2 to L4 only.

**Choose pure OVS plus Ansible when the lab is small (2 to 5 chassis) and the team is not familiar with the OVN learning curve.**

## 13.0.5 Real-world use cases, 2015 to 2026

**OpenStack Neutron with OVN ML2 (2017 onward).** Red Hat RHOSP 16 (2020) made it the default. Canonical Ubuntu charm-ovn followed (2022 onward). It replaced linuxbridge plus the router, DHCP, and metadata agents. Provisioning time dropped from about 30 seconds to 3 to 5 seconds. Scale grew from about 100 tenants to over 1,000 stable tenants.

**OVN-Kubernetes CNI (2019 onward).** OpenShift 4.6 made it generally available. OpenShift-SDN (the Kubernetes default before 4.5) migrated to OVN-Kubernetes, with the migration completed in OpenShift 4.14 (2023). OVN-IC (inter-cluster) became generally available in 4.14 for multi-cluster networking.

**Standalone OVN (2017 onward).** Telco and carrier custom deployments. Block 17, 18, and 19 case studies are mostly standalone. The pattern: the operator builds the control plane themselves with `ovn-nbctl` and scripting, without going through the OpenStack or K8s abstraction.

**OVN in AI and ML workloads (2024 onward).** Hyperscale AI training requires low-latency intra-rack networking. OVN combined with RoCE and SR-IOV acceleration has been experimented with, but it is not yet generally available in production.

## 13.0.6 Evolution timeline, 2015 to 2026

| Year | Milestone | Version |
|-----|-----------|---------|
| 13/01/2015 | Announcement | not applicable |
| 11/2015 | First release (embedded in OVS 2.5) | OVS 2.5 |
| 06/2016 | Neutron ML2-OVN experimental | OpenStack Mitaka |
| 2017 | Production ML2-OVN in RHOSP 12 | not applicable |
| 2019 | OVN-Kubernetes CNI in production (OpenShift 4.2 onward) | not applicable |
| 08/2020 | OVN split from OVS, own release train | OVN 20.06 |
| 03/2022 | LTS release plus multichassis port feature | OVN 22.03 |
| 09/2022 | `mac_binding_age_threshold` (commit `1a947dd3`, 2022-08-17) plus I-P engine statistics plus lflow-cache | OVN 22.09 |
| 03/2024 | `activation-strategy=rarp` plus distributed gateway port improvements | OVN 24.03 |
| 09/2024 | Raft clustering improvements plus 1,000-chassis scale tested | OVN 24.09 |
| 2026 | Curriculum baseline Ubuntu 22.04 plus OVN 22.03.8 (LTS), upgrade path Ubuntu 24.04 plus OVN 24.03 | not applicable |

### Key inflection points

**2020 OVS and OVN split.** Before 2020, OVN shipped on the OVS release train (OVS 2.13 contained OVN 20.06 alpha). After the split, OVN had its own versioning (20.x, then 22.x, then 24.x). This let OVN release on a faster cycle than OVS (3 to 6 months versus 6 to 12 months).

**2019 incremental-processing engine** (corrected at v3.10 R1.A audit): the I-P engine was introduced by commit `5d1d606b` on 2019-05-17, included in OVN 20.03 (the first OVN-only branch after the OVS/OVN split). I-P statistics were added in v21.06.0; the `inc-engine/recompute` command was added in v22.03.0. Before the I-P engine, `ovn-controller` recomputed the entire state from SBDB on every change. With 10K flows, that took 1 to 2 seconds per event. The I-P engine computes deltas only, dropping latency to under 100 ms. This is crucial for hyperscale chassis counts.

**2024 multichassis port plus activation-strategy.** This addresses the long-standing pain point of VM live migration (Part 19.0 case study). Before OVN 22.03, live migration required about 15 to 30 seconds to rebuild flows on the destination chassis. From OVN 22.03 onward, the multichassis port allows pre-built flows to be installed in advance, with activation triggered by a RARP packet from the guest. Migration downtime is now under 500 ms.

## 13.0.7 Guided exercise: verify the OVN 3 tiers with `ovn-nbctl` and `ovn-sbctl`

**Goal:** observe the journey of configuration intent from NBDB through `ovn-northd` to SBDB and then to `ovn-controller`, in a small lab.

**Setup:** one Ubuntu 22.04 VM running both ovn-central and ovn-controller (single-node development).

**Steps:**

1. Install and start the OVN central and controller components:
   ```bash
   $ sudo apt install -y ovn-central ovn-host
   $ sudo ovn-nbctl set-connection ptcp:6641
   $ sudo ovn-sbctl set-connection ptcp:6642
   $ sudo ovs-vsctl set Open_vSwitch . \
       external_ids:ovn-remote=tcp:127.0.0.1:6642 \
       external_ids:ovn-encap-type=geneve \
       external_ids:ovn-encap-ip=127.0.0.1
   ```
2. Tier 1 (NBDB): create a logical switch and one LSP:
   ```bash
   $ sudo ovn-nbctl ls-add ls-tier1
   $ sudo ovn-nbctl lsp-add ls-tier1 lsp-01 \
       -- lsp-set-addresses lsp-01 "aa:bb:cc:00:00:01 10.0.0.10"
   $ sudo ovn-nbctl show
   ```
3. Tier 2 (SBDB after `ovn-northd` compiles): verify the computed state:
   ```bash
   $ sudo ovn-sbctl list Datapath_Binding
   # Expected: 1 row for ls-tier1 with an auto-assigned tunnel_key.
   $ sudo ovn-sbctl --columns=logical_port,tunnel_key,mac list Port_Binding lsp-01
   # Expected: logical_port=lsp-01, tunnel_key=N (per LSP), mac=aa:bb:cc:00:00:01.
   $ sudo ovn-sbctl --columns=pipeline,table_id,priority,match,actions list Logical_Flow \
       | head -30
   # Expected: tens of entries for pipeline=ingress and egress with table stages.
   ```
4. Tier 3 (`ovn-controller` to OpenFlow): verify the installed rules:
   ```bash
   $ sudo ovs-ofctl -O OpenFlow13 dump-flows br-int | wc -l
   # Expected: about 30 to 40 flow rules (baseline plus 1 LSP).
   $ sudo ovs-ofctl -O OpenFlow13 dump-flows br-int | head -10
   ```

**POE questions:**
- **Predict:** if you delete LSP `lsp-01` from NBDB, how long until the OpenFlow rules on `br-int` disappear?
- **Observe:** typically under 1 second. `ovn-northd` recomputes (50 to 200 ms with the I-P engine in OVN 22.09 onward), SBDB writes (about 50 ms), and `ovn-controller` applies (50 to 100 ms).
- **Explain:** the 3-tier architecture does not poll. All three tiers subscribe through the OVSDB `monitor_cond` mechanism (Part 10.4). Changes propagate as events, near instant.

**Cleanup:**
```bash
$ sudo ovn-nbctl ls-del ls-tier1
$ sudo systemctl stop ovn-controller
$ sudo systemctl stop ovn-central
```

## 13.0.8 Key points to remember

First, OVN was announced on 13 January 2015 by four authors (Pettit and Pfaff at VMware, Wright and Venugopal at Red Hat). The VMware and Red Hat collaboration was unusual and reflected the NSDI 2015 Nicira philosophy of "community first, product second".

Second, the motivation was five-fold: three technical (OVS lacked a native control plane, a distributed control plane was needed, and a logical abstraction layer was needed) plus two commercial (Red Hat needed an alternative to Neutron ML2, and VMware wanted a reference implementation for NSX-T).

Third, the 3-tier architecture (NBDB configuration intent, then `ovn-northd` compile, then SBDB computed flows, then `ovn-controller` OpenFlow apply) is the key architectural contribution. It provides separation of concerns, a 1,000-chassis scale target, and an independent evolution path per tier.

Fourth, OVN does not replace OpenFlow. OVN uses OpenFlow as its southbound. The distinction between "OpenFlow lost traction as a direct controller protocol" and "OpenFlow succeeded as a southbound for OVN to compile down to" is important.

Fifth, the use cases are: OpenStack ML2-OVN (RHOSP 16 onward), OVN-Kubernetes (OpenShift 4.6 onward), and standalone OVN (telco plus Block 17 to 19). Choose OVN when you need a distributed control plane, an open-source license, and a system purpose-built for the OVS ecosystem.
