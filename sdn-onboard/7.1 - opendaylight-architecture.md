## Context: why the industry needed an umbrella project

In 2012 and 2013, the SDN controller ecosystem was severely fragmented. Every large vendor had its own controller. Cisco had ONE Controller (later evolving into APIC for ACI). Juniper had Contrail. NEC had Programmable Flow Controller. HP had Virtual Application Networks. Big Switch had Floodlight (a NOX fork). None of these controllers could program a switch from a different vendor, because each vendor extended OpenFlow in its own way or simply did not fully support standard OpenFlow.

The situation created severe lock-in for buyers. Choosing the Cisco controller meant buying Cisco switches. Switching to the Juniper controller required replacing nearly all the equipment. For large service providers (AT&T, Deutsche Telekom, Telefonica) building infrastructure worth billions of dollars, depending on a single vendor was unacceptable. They had learned the lesson from the 2000s, when Cisco dominated IP networking: once an infrastructure tilted toward one vendor, the cost of switching was high enough for that vendor to raise prices at will.

The Linux Foundation was the only neutral organization that had demonstrated the ability to govern a multi-vendor open-source project at large scale (the Linux kernel itself being the example). In April 2013, the Linux Foundation announced the formation of the OpenDaylight Project as a collaborative project, accepting code and funding contributions from competing vendors but with independent governance.

## 7.1.1 Founding: Linux Foundation, 2013-04-08

On 2013-04-08, the Linux Foundation announced OpenDaylight with eighteen founding members. Among them, the platinum members (the highest contribution tier, 500,000 USD per year) were Cisco, IBM, Juniper Networks, Microsoft, NEC, Red Hat, Brocade, Citrix, Dell, Ericsson, Fujitsu, HP, Intel, and VMware. The silver members (100,000 USD per year) added several smaller vendors.

The governance structure was designed to prevent any single vendor from dominating the project. The Board of Directors had platinum-member representation in proportion to contribution. The Technical Steering Committee was elected by community contributors (regardless of company). Each subproject's project lead was chosen based on technical contribution. The structure was inherited directly from Linux kernel governance, which had demonstrated over two decades the ability to balance vendor interests with technical quality.

**A notable point at the founding moment:** Cisco, the largest networking vendor in the world, contributed significant initial code, mostly from the One Platform Kit (onePK) project. The decision was controversial inside Cisco because it carried a "democratize networking" direction that could erode Cisco's own competitive advantage. However, Cisco leadership at the time (notably Soni Jiandani and Padmasree Warrior) judged that participating in and influencing the project was more important than standing aside while the project moved in a direction unfavorable to Cisco.

> **Key point:** OpenDaylight is a textbook example of "coopetition" (cooperation plus competition): vendors who compete directly in the market also contribute to a shared codebase. The model can work when (a) a sufficiently credible neutral host exists (Linux Foundation), (b) every vendor believes that the gain from a faster-growing market exceeds the gain from holding a monopoly, and (c) governance is transparent enough that no vendor can capture it.

## 7.1.2 The release train: from Hydrogen 2014 to Argon 2023

ODL chose to name its releases after chemical elements in the periodic table, signaling "every release adds one new element to the system". The first release, **Hydrogen**, was published on 2014-02-04, ten months after project founding. That was unusually fast development by open-source standards and reflected the level of resources vendors invested.

The thirteen main releases in order: Hydrogen (2014-02), Helium (2014-10), Lithium (2015-06), Beryllium (2016-02), Boron (2016-09), Carbon (2017-05), Nitrogen (2017-09), Oxygen (2018-03), Fluorine (2018-09), Neon (2019-03), Sodium (2019-09), Magnesium (2020-03), Aluminium (2020-09), Silicon (2021-03), Phosphorus (2021-09), Sulfur (2022-03), Chlorine (2022-09), Argon (2023-03).

**Fluorine (2018-09)** is considered the most stable release for commercial use. Most service-provider deployments are based on the Fluorine branch or its forks, because after Neon (2019-03) the project shifted focus to maintenance and refactoring, with few new features added.

**Argon (2023-03)** is the last major release. From that point, the project officially entered maintenance mode: bug fixes and security patches only, no new feature releases. The Technical Steering Committee announced this with the reasoning that most needed functionality was complete, and the principal contributing vendors had moved their technical focus to ONOS (for service-provider scale) and to proprietary vendor platforms (for the data center).

The codebase size at the time of Argon was over three million lines of Java distributed across about one hundred sub-projects, exceeding every other SDN controller in scale. This was both a strength (broad use-case coverage) and ODL's main weakness, as analyzed in section 7.1.6.

## 7.1.3 MD-SAL: the heart of the architecture

The Model-Driven Service Abstraction Layer, known as MD-SAL, is the architectural foundation that distinguishes ODL from every other controller. While NOX, Ryu, and ONOS are based on an event-driven model (an application registers handlers for events), MD-SAL is based on a data-centric model. Every component reads from and writes to a central in-memory datastore, defined entirely through a YANG data model.

The architecture works as follows. When a plugin or application is loaded into ODL, it registers a YANG schema that defines the data structures it manages. YANG is a hierarchical schema language (similar to JSON Schema but more powerful, with a full type system, constraints, and augmentation), originally designed for NETCONF (RFC 6020, October 2010). ODL extended YANG to define not only configuration but also operational state and RPC endpoints.

When a plugin needs to read or write data, it calls the DataBroker API, a unified interface for all datastore operations. The DataBroker provides two views of the datastore. The **configuration datastore** holds data written by the user or orchestrator (the desired configuration). The **operational datastore** holds data written back by plugins from real devices (the current state). The split is important: the user requests a switch with VLAN 100 (written to config), the OpenFlow plugin confirms that the switch installed VLAN 100 successfully (written to operational), and another application can compare the two sides to detect a state mismatch.

**MD-SAL example:**

```yang
// Simple YANG module that defines a switch inventory
module switch-inventory {
    namespace "urn:example:switch-inventory";
    prefix si;

    container switches {
        list switch {
            key "dpid";
            leaf dpid { type uint64; }
            leaf-list openflow-version { type string; }
            leaf connected-since { type yang:date-and-time; }
        }
    }
}
```

When the OpenFlow plugin receives a connection from a switch, it writes an entry into `/switches/switch[dpid=...]` in the operational datastore. Any application interested in new switches can subscribe to this path through the DataBroker and receive notifications automatically, without needing a direct connection to the OpenFlow plugin.

> **Key point:** The data-centric mechanism in MD-SAL lets plugins evolve independently. An OpenFlow plugin and a NETCONF plugin do not need to know about each other. They only need to agree on a shared YANG schema for reading and writing the datastore. This is why ODL can support ten different southbound protocols in a single instance, while Ryu and ONOS focus mainly on OpenFlow.

**RESTCONF northbound, auto-generated.** A powerful consequence of defining everything through YANG is that ODL automatically generates a RESTCONF API for the entire datastore. The user can query and modify any node through HTTP without writing extra code. For example, listing every switch:

```bash
[operator]$ curl -u admin:admin http://odl-controller:8181/restconf/operational/opendaylight-inventory:nodes/
```

The response is JSON or XML containing the entire sub-tree, structured exactly as the YANG schema defined. Productivity is very high: adding a plugin with a 200-line YANG model automatically produces a complete REST API for that plugin, with no need to write a separate REST controller.

## 7.1.4 Southbound plugins: OpenFlow, NETCONF, BGP-LS, OVSDB

The main difference between ODL and Ryu or POX is the multi-protocol southbound. A single ODL instance can simultaneously speak OpenFlow with some switches, NETCONF with routers, BGP-LS with the core network, and OVSDB with virtual switches, all through the same MD-SAL datastore.

**The OpenFlow plugin** is the most mature, supporting OpenFlow 1.0 and 1.3 at production quality. OpenFlow 1.4 and 1.5 are implemented but not heavily tested in production. A notable feature: a direct flow programming API. Beyond the inventory model, a developer can write a raw flow entry and send it to the switch, bypassing some MD-SAL abstractions when high performance is needed.

**The NETCONF plugin** is especially popular with service providers because most modern Cisco, Juniper, and Huawei routers support NETCONF and YANG. ODL can act as a NETCONF client, querying and configuring routers using the IETF standard YANG models (for example, `ietf-interfaces` and `ietf-routing`). The important advantage is that no new OpenFlow switch is needed. Existing routers in the service-provider network can be managed through ODL without hardware replacement.

**The BGP-LS plugin** (BGP Link-State) is used in the PCEP (Path Computation Element Protocol) and segment-routing WAN use case. BGP-LS lets ODL receive the entire IGP topology (OSPF or IS-IS) from a single BGP speaker in the network, rather than peering with every router. Once it has the full topology, ODL can compute optimal paths and push them to routers through PCEP.

**The OVSDB plugin** connects to the OVS manager database on a compute node and manages bridges and ports of Open vSwitch. This is the main mechanism that OVN (Open Virtual Network) used originally, although OVN has since moved to its own architecture and no longer depends on ODL.

*Table 7-2: important ODL southbound plugins and their typical use cases*

| Plugin | Protocol | Connection target | Main use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| openflowplugin | OpenFlow 1.0 and 1.3 | OVS, whitebox switch, hardware SDN switch | Direct flow programming |
| netconf | NETCONF over SSH plus YANG | Vendor router, modern switch | Router configuration through IETF standard YANG models |
| bgpcep | BGP-LS plus PCEP | Core router that speaks BGP-LS | Topology discovery, traffic engineering |
| ovsdb | OVSDB (RFC 7047) | Open vSwitch instance | Manage OVS bridges and ports |
| snmp | SNMP v2c and v3 | Legacy device | Monitoring and configuring older equipment |

The breadth of southbound protocols is a competitive advantage no one else has. It comes with high development cost: each plugin is a sub-project with its own team, its own release cycle, and at times uneven quality. This is one reason ODL is hard to keep stable at service-provider scale. A bug in a rarely used plugin can still affect the core.

## 7.1.5 Real-world deployments: Telefonica, Comcast, AT&T

ODL achieved many notable deployments at large service providers, but not in the data center, because the multi-protocol model fits the reality of service-provider infrastructure (a mix of NETCONF routers, OpenFlow switches, BGP-LS in the core).

**Telefonica UNICA.** The Telefonica UNICA project (Unified Network Infrastructure for Communications and Applications) started in 2014 with the goal of virtualizing the entire telco infrastructure. Telefonica was a Platinum founding member of ODL and contributed engineers directly to the upstream codebase. UNICA used ODL as the main SDN controller for the VoIP signaling fabric and the vEPC (virtual Evolved Packet Core), combined with OpenStack as the NFV platform. At its peak around 2018 to 2019, UNICA was deployed in over 20 countries in the Telefonica group.

**Comcast.** Comcast deployed ODL for the CDN over IP backbone, using the BGP-LS plugin for path computation and the PCEP plugin to push explicit tunnels to routers. The main use case is traffic engineering: Comcast needs to ensure that video traffic uses paths with sufficient bandwidth and low latency, not affected by congestion on the shortest path. ODL acts as an out-of-band PCE (Path Computation Element), computing and installing tunnels.

**AT&T Network Cloud and ECOMP, becoming ONAP.** AT&T was the largest ODL contributor after Cisco. AT&T's ECOMP project (Enhanced Control, Orchestration, Management and Policy) used ODL as one of its core components, alongside OpenStack and other microservices. In 2017, AT&T donated ECOMP to the Linux Foundation and merged it with Open-O (a similar project from China Mobile) to form ONAP (Open Network Automation Platform). ONAP remains active in 2026 and still uses ODL in its stack, although it has been gradually reducing the dependency over time.

The three deployments above share common traits: (a) all are tier-1 service providers with strong in-house SDN engineering teams, (b) all have multi-protocol needs that a pure OpenFlow controller cannot meet, (c) all accept high development cost and long timelines to build strategic infrastructure.

**The data-center market is completely different.** In the data center, the problem is to manage thousands of virtual switches (OVS) on compute nodes, not a mix of NETCONF routers and OpenFlow switches. The main requirements are: scale linearly with the number of switches, low control-plane latency (under 100 ms for flow installation), and operational simplicity. ONOS with its distributed core (Part 7.2) fits these requirements better than monolithic ODL, and that is why the data center did not choose ODL.

## 7.1.6 Current state in 2026: maintenance only

> **Common misconception:** "ODL is the most popular SDN controller because many vendors contribute to it."
>
> **Reality:** ODL had many vendor contributors, but it never held the most popular position in any single segment. Service providers mostly use proprietary vendor controllers (Cisco, Nokia) or build their own. Data centers use vendor controllers (ACI, NSX, Contrail) or ONOS. ODL had a small share in both segments. From 2023, ODL officially moved to maintenance only, confirming the reality that the community had shrunk significantly.

ODL commit velocity peaked in 2016 to 2018 with about 300 to 500 commits per week, and then declined steadily. By 2023, the figure was below 50 commits per week, mostly bug fixes and security patches. The Technical Steering Committee announced maintenance mode officially in March 2023, alongside the Argon release.

**Reasons for the decline.** Three factors acted at the same time. First, large vendors gradually shifted technical focus to ONOS for service-provider scale and to Kubernetes-native platforms for the data center (Antrea, Cilium, OVN-Kubernetes). The R&D budgets of Cisco, Juniper, and Red Hat for ODL fell sharply from 2019. Second, the ODL architecture itself, a monolithic Java application running in an OSGi Karaf container, became a barrier as the broader trend shifted to microservices and container-native deployments. Running ODL requires 8 to 16 GB RAM for the controller alone, before any workload, much larger than modern cloud-native standards. Third, the development community and the user community were not balanced. Most contributors were paid engineers from vendors. When the vendors withdrew, contributors withdrew with them, and there was no organic community to sustain the project.

**Implications for the SDN learner in 2026.** ODL is still worth studying for its architecture. MD-SAL is an important case study in data-centric design. However, ODL should not be seen as a deployment target for new projects. Teams that already run ODL in production need a migration plan over the next three to five years: to ONOS if they still need a multi-protocol SDN controller, or to a Kubernetes-native stack (OVN-Kubernetes) if the workload is mostly containers.

---

## Key points to remember

First, OpenDaylight is the product of "coopetition" between competing vendors under the neutral governance of the Linux Foundation. Initial success (2013 to 2018) came from vendors willing to contribute code and money. That success was not self-sustaining once vendors withdrew.

Second, MD-SAL is the most important architectural contribution that ODL gave to the industry: a data-centric model with YANG as the source of truth. This model lets plugins evolve independently and automatically generates a REST API. The philosophy continues to influence later projects (RESTCONF, gNMI, the Kubernetes operator pattern).

Third, ODL dominated the service-provider segment from 2015 to 2020 thanks to its multi-protocol southbound (OpenFlow, NETCONF, BGP-LS, OVSDB), but did not capture the data-center segment because the monolithic architecture did not scale the way the ONOS distributed core does. The 2023 transition to maintenance mode marked the end of active development and was a clear signal that the multi-vendor umbrella project model has a natural lifecycle limit.
