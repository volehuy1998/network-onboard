## Context: separating the vertical stack

Part 5.0 analysed API-based SDN. That model keeps the switch as is and adds a configuration layer on top. Part 5.1 analysed the hypervisor overlay, which moves the network edge from hardware into software. This part is the fourth model: **keep the hardware switch as the edge, but disaggregate hardware from software**, so the operator can choose a NOS (Network Operating System) independently from the hardware vendor.

In the traditional network model, Cisco sells a Cisco switch with Cisco IOS firmware. Vendor lock-in is end to end. In the whitebox model, an ODM (Original Design Manufacturer) such as Celestica, Edgecore, or Delta produces a bare-metal switch built to the OCP specification. The operator buys the box and installs a NOS of their choice (Cumulus, SONiC). The analogy with PC servers is direct. Dell sells server hardware. The operator installs Linux or Windows according to preference and is not locked into a single-vendor operating system.

The trigger for the movement was a scaling pain point at Facebook in 2012 and 2013. Facebook had more than 100,000 switches in its fabric, and the vendor licence cost was unbearable. Cisco and Juniper could not drop prices far enough. This forced Facebook to pursue an alternative: build its own hardware (Wedge, 6-pack) and pursue an open-source NOS. The Open Compute Project (OCP) formalised this effort in 2013.

## 5.2.1 OCP Network project (2013 onward)

### Origin of the Open Compute Project

OCP was founded in April 2011 by Facebook with a clear motivation: disaggregate server hardware to reduce cost per compute unit. The initial scope was server design (the Facebook Open Compute Server v1, motherboard specifications, chassis specification). The scope expanded into networking in 2013.

The OCP Networking project launched in January 2013 at the OCP Summit. Its goals were:

- Standardise bare-metal switch hardware specifications.
- Enable a vendor-independent NOS ecosystem.
- Reduce switch cost through disaggregation and through competition among ODMs.

Early contributors were Facebook (primary), Accton Technology (ODM), Broadcom (silicon vendor), and Cumulus Networks (NOS startup).

### OCP specifications

OCP Networking publishes hardware specifications as open source. Notable examples:

- **Wedge** (Facebook 2014): a 16-port 40G top-of-rack switch using Broadcom Trident2 silicon and FBOSS, a Linux-based NOS.
- **6-pack** (Facebook 2015): a modular spine switch based on Trident2.
- **Minipack** (Facebook 2019): 128-port 100G, using Tomahawk 3 silicon.
- **OCP Accepted switches**: ODM products that meet the OCP specification and are certified to interoperate with OCP-compliant NOS images.

The specification covers mechanical dimensions, power connectors, the management port (BMC), the serial console (RS-232 with a specific pinout), and front panel indicators. With this standardisation across ODMs, any OCP NOS works with any OCP switch.

### Reference: OCP project home page

OCP project information is at `https://www.opencompute.org/`. The site is sometimes bot-protected, so access may return 403 for automated tools. As an alternative, search Google or Bing for "OCP networking project specs 2024" to find current state documentation.

## 5.2.2 Whitebox hardware (Celestica, Edgecore, Delta)

### Main ODM vendors

**Accton Technology and Edgecore**: a Taiwanese ODM, the largest manufacturer of OCP switches. The flagship products are the Edgecore Wedge 100BF-32X (32-port 100G, with Tofino silicon for P4 use cases) and the AS-series (Broadcom silicon for general purpose use).

**Celestica**: a Canadian ODM, commonly used by Microsoft Azure data centres. The DX-series switches are broadly deployed.

**Delta Networks**: a Taiwanese ODM that focuses on carrier and enterprise customers. The AG-series switches are the main product line.

**Supermicro**: primarily a server ODM, but also makes switches for cost-sensitive deployments.

**Inventec, Quanta (QCT)**: other Taiwanese ODMs with OCP-compliant product lines.

### Price comparison (estimated)

| Product category | Vendor OEM price (per port) | ODM whitebox price (per port) |
|-------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 100G top-of-rack (32-port) | Cisco, Juniper, Arista: about 500 to 700 United States dollars per port | Edgecore, Celestica: about 200 to 300 United States dollars per port |
| 400G spine | About 1,500 to 2,000 United States dollars per port | About 500 to 800 United States dollars per port |

The discount is 40 to 60 percent per port. For a data centre with 10,000 to 100,000 ports, savings reach millions to tens of millions of United States dollars per data centre, which justifies the learning curve for NOS operation.

### Supply chain

ODMs do not sell at retail. Customers buy through:

- Direct wholesale for hyperscale operators (Microsoft, Facebook, Google).
- Reseller systems for enterprise customers (for example, Penguin Computing, and Mellanox via indirect channels).
- Co-investment with NOS vendors (Cumulus was distributed via Dell and HPE OEM channels before the NVIDIA acquisition).

The challenge is that the support model is different. ODMs do not provide a 24-hour-by-7-day NOC like Cisco. Customers depend on the NOS vendor for support (SUSE for Cumulus before 2020, Microsoft for SONiC enterprise) or on internal expertise.

## 5.2.3 Merchant silicon: Broadcom Tomahawk and Trident

### Broadcom dominance

Broadcom is the dominant merchant silicon supplier for data centre switches from 2013 to 2026. There are two main families.

**Trident family (leaf and top-of-rack)**:

- Trident2 (BCM56850, 2013): 1.28 Tbps, 40G.
- Trident3 (BCM56870, 2017): 3.2 Tbps, 100G.
- Trident4 (BCM56880, 2021): 12.8 Tbps, 400G.
- Trident5 (BCM56890, 2023): 25.6 Tbps, 800G.

**Tomahawk family (spine, high-radix)**:

- Tomahawk 1 (BCM56960, 2014): 3.2 Tbps.
- Tomahawk 2 (BCM56970, 2016): 6.4 Tbps.
- Tomahawk 3 (BCM56980, 2018): 12.8 Tbps.
- Tomahawk 4 (BCM56990, 2020): 25.6 Tbps.
- Tomahawk 5 (2023): 51.2 Tbps, 800G per port.

### Alternative silicon vendors

**Marvell Prestera** (formerly Cavium Xpliant): an alternative general-purpose silicon. It has a smaller market share but is present in some Cisco ASR products.

**Cisco Silicon One** (2019 onward): Cisco's own unified silicon family. Before this, Cisco used custom proprietary silicon per product line. Silicon One consolidates across routers and switches. It is used primarily in Cisco products and is not merchant silicon for whitebox.

**Intel Tofino** (acquired from Barefoot in 2019): P4-programmable silicon. Intel announced end of life in January 2023. P4 momentum continued via the community (p4.org), but no commercial production target remained.

**Nvidia Spectrum** (Cumulus and Mellanox acquisition in 2020): Spectrum-1 through Spectrum-4, with the ASAP² eSwitch hardware offload (see Part 9.5). This is merchant silicon for bare-metal switches.

### Relationship between silicon and NOS

ODMs build the box around a silicon choice. The NOS must support the silicon's SDK:

- **Broadcom SDK**: closed source, NDA required for OEMs.
- **Marvell SDK**: similarly closed.
- **SAI (Switch Abstraction Interface)**: an open-source abstraction layer above the silicon SDK. SONiC and Stratum use SAI.

SAI is a critical enabler. The NOS is written once against the SAI API and runs on any silicon with a SAI driver. This reduces the per-silicon porting cost for the NOS.

## 5.2.4 NOS options: Cumulus Linux, SONiC, OpenSwitch, Stratum

### Cumulus Linux (2011 to present, Nvidia after 2020)

**Founding**: Cumulus Networks was founded in 2010 by ex-Cisco engineers JR Rivers and Nolan Leake. The first product shipped in 2013.

**Architecture**:

- A Debian Linux base with a custom kernel and drivers.
- Standard Linux tools work: `ip`, `bridge`, `ethtool`, `tc`.
- **Policy**: configure the switch with Linux-native commands. Use FRR (Free Range Routing) for BGP and OSPF, and use systemd services.

**Business model**: commercial, with a subscription licence (per port per year). Cumulus was acquired by Nvidia in May 2020 (as part of the Mellanox deal) for about 250 million United States dollars.

**Current state in 2026**: Cumulus Linux 5.x series, distributed by Nvidia as part of the Spectrum Ethernet portfolio. Documentation is at `https://docs.nvidia.com/networking-ethernet-software/cumulus-linux-510/`.

### SONiC (2016 to present, now under the Linux Foundation)

**Founding**: started by the Microsoft Azure team in 2016, open source under the MIT licence from day one.

**Architecture**:

- A Debian Linux base.
- **Container-based architecture**: services (BGP, LLDP, SNMP, teamd) run inside Docker containers.
- **SAI (Switch Abstraction Interface)**: an abstraction layer between SONiC and the silicon SDK. It enables a portable NOS across Broadcom, Nvidia, and Marvell silicon.
- **Redis database**: a central state store, with publish and subscribe channels for inter-service communication.

**Components**:

- `syncd`: silicon SDK interaction (the only container that touches silicon).
- `swss` (Switch State Service): the state management orchestrator.
- `bgp` container: FRR or Quagga BGP.
- `pmon`, `lldp`, `snmp`, `dhcp_relay`: functional containers.

**Adoption**:

- Microsoft Azure production: more than 100,000 ports, primary NOS.
- Alibaba Cloud: large-scale production.
- LinkedIn: migrated to SONiC around 2019.
- Telefonica and Orange: European carrier adoption.

**Governance**: transferred from Microsoft to the Linux Foundation (Open Compute Project plus the SONiC Foundation) in April 2022. The community lives at `https://sonicfoundation.dev/` and `https://github.com/sonic-net/SONiC`.

### OpenSwitch (2015 to 2017, abandoned)

HP (Hewlett-Packard Enterprise) launched OpenSwitch in October 2015 as an open-source NOS for whitebox. The initial contributors were HPE, Accton, and Arista, on a Broadcom silicon foundation.

Issues:

- HPE corporate focus shifted (HPE split in 2015, and networking priorities shifted).
- Competition with SONiC was overwhelming. Microsoft's commitment was much larger than HPE's.
- Governance was unclear and the community was diluted.

The OpenSwitch.net website is still up in 2026, but the last meaningful release was in 2017. The project is abandoned, and migration paths point to SONiC or Cumulus.

### Stratum (ONF, 2018 to present)

ONF Stratum is a thin NOS for P4-based programmable switches. The philosophy differs from Cumulus and SONiC:

**Cumulus and SONiC**: full NOS with BGP, OSPF, and STP built in. The switch can operate autonomously.

**Stratum**: a minimal agent. All intelligence sits in a remote controller. The switch is a dumb executor and the controller (ONOS) orchestrates everything.

**Use case**: a P4-programmable fabric with Tofino silicon. ONOS compiles the P4 program, pushes it to Stratum, and Stratum loads it into the Tofino pipeline.

**Adoption**: niche, mostly research and telco proof of concept. The AT&T CORD architecture uses Stratum and ONOS. It is not mainstream enterprise.

### NOS comparison

| NOS | Base operating system | SAI support | Governance | Production scale |
|-----|---------|-------------|------------|--------------------|
| Cumulus Linux | Debian | No (own abstraction) | Nvidia commercial | Enterprise mid-market |
| SONiC | Debian | Yes (primary abstraction) | Linux Foundation | Hyperscale (Azure, Alibaba) |
| OpenSwitch | Debian | Partial | Abandoned | Not applicable |
| Stratum | Thin agent | P4 runtime (not SAI) | ONF | Niche, research and telco |

## 5.2.5 ONIE (Open Network Install Environment)

### Boot loader standard

ONIE (pronounced "OH-nee") is a boot loader for whitebox switches. Its role is analogous to PXE boot for PC servers.

Flow:

1. The switch powers on. ONIE boots from flash (it comes pre-installed from the ODM).
2. ONIE probes the network (DHCP to obtain an IP address, then retrieves the URL for the NOS image).
3. ONIE downloads the NOS installer (HTTPS, HTTP, TFTP, or USB).
4. The NOS installer runs, writes the NOS image to flash, and reboots.
5. The switch now boots the NOS. ONIE remains dormant until the next reinstall.

### Standardisation role

Without ONIE: each ODM has a custom boot mechanism, and each NOS vendor must support each ODM. The complexity is N times M.

With ONIE: the ODM implements the ONIE specification (a consistent boot interface). The NOS vendor implements an ONIE-compatible installer (retrieved via DHCP option 114 URL). Any NOS works with any ONIE-capable switch.

### Reference implementation

**ONL (Open Network Linux)** is the reference ONIE-compatible Linux distribution, maintained by Big Switch (acquired by Arista in 2020). It is used to test ONIE interoperability.

**Reference URL**: `https://opencomputeproject.github.io/onie/` (the official OCP ONIE subproject).

### Limitations

ONIE does not standardise:

- Firmware upgrade of the NOS (each NOS has its own update mechanism).
- Dual boot (fallback to a previous NOS version).
- Hardware diagnostics (ODM-specific vendor tools).

These require vendor-specific tooling beyond ONIE.

## 5.2.6 Current state in 2026: SONiC wins hyperscale market share

### Market snapshot

**Hyperscale** (more than 10,000 switch ports per customer):

- Microsoft Azure: SONiC primary (more than 95 percent of new deployments).
- Alibaba Cloud: SONiC primary.
- LinkedIn: SONiC.
- Google and Facebook: proprietary forks of a Linux-based NOS (FBOSS at Facebook). Not SONiC, but the same model.

**Enterprise mid-market** (100 to 10,000 ports):

- Cumulus Linux (under the Nvidia Spectrum umbrella).
- SONiC adoption is growing through community editions and enterprise distributions (such as Celestica-managed offerings).
- Traditional Cisco, Juniper, and Arista still hold the majority of the mid-market.

**Service provider**:

- SONiC via Broadcom and Edgecore: Telefonica, Orange, and Deutsche Telekom pilots.
- Cumulus is niche.
- Traditional Cisco and Juniper are dominant via bespoke features.

### Why SONiC wins hyperscale

1. **Microsoft commitment**: Microsoft produces and maintains SONiC. It is the de facto standard at Azure scale. The community grows through Microsoft-initiated pull.
2. **SAI standardisation**: SONiC abstracts silicon uniformly. Operators can swap silicon without a NOS rewrite.
3. **Container architecture**: it is easy to test, upgrade, and roll back individual services. Operational maturity is higher than with a monolithic NOS.
4. **Open source**: no licence fee and no vendor lock-in. Pure operational cost.

### Cumulus limitations for hyperscale

Cumulus Linux is excellent for enterprise (familiar Debian tooling, strong BGP via FRR), but:

- The commercial licence is prohibitive at the 100,000-port scale.
- The 2020 acquisition by Nvidia tied strategic direction to Spectrum silicon. Operators that want to diversify vendors are uncomfortable.
- The container architecture of SONiC is more advanced than the traditional daemon architecture of Cumulus for operational flexibility.

### Forecast 2026 onward

- **SONiC dominance**: continues to grow in hyperscale and increasingly in enterprise mid-market.
- **Cumulus**: a stable enterprise base under Nvidia. It does not expand into hyperscale.
- **Traditional OEMs** (Cisco, Juniper, Arista): retain enterprise via features (deep analytics, tight integration with the vendor stack), but lose hyperscale irreversibly.
- **Stratum and P4**: a possible revival attempt after the Intel Tofino end of life, perhaps via AMD Pensando DPU or other programmable silicon, but the outlook is uncertain.

> **Key point:** the "Opening Up the Device" model (whitebox plus OCP plus ONIE plus SAI) is economically irreversible at hyperscale. Cost savings of 40 to 60 percent per port, combined with SONiC maturity, eliminate vendor lock-in for the largest operators. The traditional vendor (Cisco, Juniper, Arista) response is to move value-add up the stack (network analytics, security, AI-driven operations) instead of competing on price per port. The enterprise mid-market evolves more slowly, but it is on the same trajectory over the next 5 to 10 years.

### Capstone for Block V, Trouble Ticket 5-1

**Scenario**: a multi-tenant data centre with 1000 virtual machines spread across 50 compute nodes, a three-tier rack topology (spine, leaf, top-of-rack). It needs VLAN-based tenant isolation and per-tenant ACL policy. The budget constraint is to minimise per-port hardware cost. The operational constraint is that the team has Linux expertise but has never used OpenFlow.

**Task**: recommend the SDN model (OpenFlow, API-based, hypervisor overlay, or whitebox) that fits best. Justify through trade-off analysis.

**Expected analysis dimensions**:

1. Cost per port (whitebox is the lowest; hypervisor overlay needs only software, with no extra hardware cost).
2. Complexity versus team expertise (a Linux team maps naturally to Cumulus or SONiC; OpenFlow requires learning).
3. Tenant isolation mechanism (hypervisor overlay is native via VXLAN; whitebox VLAN is limited to 4094).
4. Scale target (1000 virtual machines is moderate; do not over-engineer with hypervisor overlay for small scale).
5. Deployment time (hypervisor overlay can be provisioned in minutes; whitebox requires silicon configuration and top-of-rack setup).

**Recommended answer** (a guide, not mandatory): hypervisor overlay using OVN on KVM, with whitebox underlay (SONiC-based) for a simple IP fabric. This combines software flexibility (OVN multi-tenant) with hardware cost efficiency (whitebox underlay). The team's Linux expertise applies to both layers.
